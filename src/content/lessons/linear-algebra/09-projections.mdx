# Projections

Imagine shining a light straight down onto vector **a**. Vector **b** casts a shadow onto **a**'s line — that shadow is the **projection** of **b** onto **a**.

Projection answers a simple geometric question: "how much of **b** lies along the direction of **a**?" And remarkably, we already have the tool to compute it: the dot product.

## The geometry of projection

The projection of **b** onto **a** is a vector that:
- Points along **a**'s direction (or opposite, if the projection is negative)
- Has length equal to the shadow **b** would cast if light hit perpendicular to **a**

<MathBlock expression={'\\text{proj}_{\\vec{a}} \\vec{b} = \\frac{\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b}}{\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{a}} \\, \\vec{a}'} />

Break this down: the scalar (a . b)/(a . a) tells you *how many copies of **a*** to take. When it's positive, the shadow falls along **a**. When it's zero, the shadow vanishes — **b** is perpendicular to **a**.

## Decomposing a vector

Projection lets you split any vector into two clean pieces: the part along a direction, and the part perpendicular to it.

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{b} = \\underbrace{\\text{proj}_{\\vec{a}} \\vec{b}}_{\\text{along } \\vec{a}} + \\underbrace{\\vec{b} - \\text{proj}_{\\vec{a}} \\vec{b}}_{\\text{perpendicular to } \\vec{a}}'} />

This decomposition is the geometric engine behind:
- **Least squares** — finding the best approximation when no exact solution exists
- **Gram-Schmidt** — building orthogonal bases by repeatedly subtracting projections
- **Fourier analysis** — decomposing signals into perpendicular frequency components

All of these reduce to the same picture: drop a perpendicular, measure the shadow.

## In three dimensions

Projection in 3D works identically — **b** drops perpendicularly onto **a**'s line. The right-angle marker (red) confirms orthogonality.

<KeyInsight>
Projection splits a vector into "the part along a direction" and "the part perpendicular to it." The formula is just the dot product divided by the self-dot-product, times the direction vector.
</KeyInsight>

**Connections.** Projection is the geometric key to least squares (lesson 23): when Ax=b has no solution, projecting b onto Col(A) gives the best approximation. Gram-Schmidt (lesson 24) is repeated projection — subtract the projection to get the perpendicular piece. In machine learning, every regression model projects data onto a lower-dimensional subspace.
