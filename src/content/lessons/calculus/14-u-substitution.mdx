The chain rule tells us the derivative of $f(g(x))$ is $f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$. u-Substitution tells us how to *undo* this: if an integrand contains a composite $f(g(x))$ multiplied by something that looks like $g'(x)$, you can reverse the chain rule. Think of it as a **change of coordinates**. You translate the integral into a simpler language — the "$u$-world" — solve it there, then translate the answer back to $x$. The single key insight is that $du$ carries the derivative information: it encodes how the old variable stretches and compresses into the new one. Without $du$, the substitution gives you nothing.

## Theorem: u-Substitution (the chain rule in reverse)

If $u = g(x)$ is a differentiable function whose derivative $g'(x)$ is continuous, and $f$ is a function with antiderivative $F$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int f(g(x))\\, g'(x)\\, dx = \\int f(u)\\, du = F(u) + C = F(g(x)) + C"} />

**Hypotheses:** $g$ differentiable, $g'$ continuous, $F$ an antiderivative of $f$. **Conclusion:** the composite integrand $f(g(x))\,g'(x)$ integrates to $F(g(x))$.

**Proof sketch:** by the chain rule, <MathInline expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}\\!\\left[F(g(x))\\right] = F'(g(x))\\cdot g'(x) = f(g(x))\\cdot g'(x)"} />. So $F(g(x))$ is an antiderivative of the composite, and the substitution formula is just the chain rule read backwards. The "$du = g'(x)\,dx$" step is bookkeeping that records the $g'(x)$ factor so it is not lost.

## When to reach for substitution

u-Substitution is the right tool whenever the integrand is **composite** — an "outer" function applied to an "inner" function, where the inner function's derivative also appears (possibly up to a constant). Classic fingerprints:

- A function **of** a polynomial or a trig argument, with the derivative of that argument nearby: $\int 2x\cos(x^2)\,dx$, <MathInline expression={"\\int \\sin x\\,e^{\\cos x}\\,dx"} />.
- A radical or rational expression whose inside is a simpler function: <MathInline expression={"\\int x/\\sqrt{1+x^2}\\,dx"} />.
- Anything of the form $f'(x)\cdot h(f(x))$.

When you cannot pair an inner function with its derivative anywhere in the integrand, substitution is probably the wrong tool — reach for [integration by parts](/courses/calculus/15-integration-by-parts) or [partial fractions](/courses/calculus/16-partial-fractions) instead.

## The formal procedure

1. **Choose** $u = g(x)$ — pick the "inner function," the part whose derivative might appear elsewhere.
2. **Differentiate** to find $du = g'(x)\, dx$.
3. **Rewrite** the entire integral — both the integrand *and* the $dx$ — in terms of $u$. Every $x$ must vanish; if one stubbornly remains, try a different $u$.
4. **Integrate** the simpler $u$-integral using the [antiderivative table](/courses/calculus/13-antiderivatives).
5. **Substitute back** $u = g(x)$ to return to the original variable.

The third step is where substitutions live or die: if you cannot fully convert to $u$, you have chosen the wrong substitution.

## Three worked examples

### Example 1: $\int 2x \cos(x^2)\, dx$

Spot the pattern: $2x$ is the derivative of $x^2$. Let $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$ — the derivative factor is *exactly* the leftover part.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int 2x \\cos(x^2)\\, dx = \\int \\cos(u)\\, du = \\sin(u) + C = \\sin(x^2) + C"} />

The $2x\, dx$ neatly becomes $du$, and the integral collapses from a complicated expression in $x$ to a basic integral in $u$.

### Example 2: <MathInline expression={"\\int x e^{x^2}\\, dx"} /> (constant multiple)

Let $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$, so $x\, dx = du/2$. The derivative does not appear *exactly*, only up to a constant factor — so we absorb the $1/2$ and carry on.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int x e^{x^2}\\, dx = \\frac{1}{2}\\int e^u\\, du = \\frac{1}{2}e^u + C = \\frac{1}{2}e^{x^2} + C"} />

### Example 3: Definite integral <MathInline expression={"\\int_0^1 \\frac{x}{1+x^2}\\, dx"} />

Let $u = 1 + x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$, so $x\, dx = du/2$. **Crucially, when $x = 0$: $u = 1$; when $x = 1$: $u = 2$.** The bounds travel into the $u$-world too.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_0^1 \\frac{x}{1+x^2}\\, dx = \\frac{1}{2}\\int_1^2 \\frac{du}{u} = \\frac{1}{2}\\bigl[\\ln u\\bigr]_1^2 = \\frac{\\ln 2}{2}"} />

Notice: for definite integrals, **changing the bounds saves you the back-substitution step** entirely — the answer is a number, not a function of $x$. You may instead back-substitute and use the original $x$-bounds; both give the same answer. Pick whichever is less error-prone.

## Trigonometric substitutions

A powerful sub-family of substitutions uses a trig function for $u$ to simplify radicals. The trick is that trig identities like $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$ can *eliminate* a square root:

<MathBlock expression={"\\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}\\,\\xrightarrow{\\,x = a\\sin\\theta\\,}\\, a\\cos\\theta, \\qquad \\sqrt{x^2+a^2}\\,\\xrightarrow{\\,x=a\\tan\\theta\\,}\\, a\\sec\\theta"} />

The three canonical forms — $x = a\sin\theta$ for <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}"} />, $x = a\tan\theta$ for <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{a^2 + x^2}"} />, and $x = a\sec\theta$ for <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}"} /> — turn an impossible radical integral into a routine trig integral. For instance, <MathInline expression={"\\int \\sqrt{1-x^2}\\, dx"} /> with $x = \sin\theta$ becomes $\int \cos^2\theta\, d\theta$, which a half-angle formula resolves. These substitutions are how you derive the antiderivative <MathInline expression={"\\frac{x}{2}\\sqrt{a^2-x^2} + \\frac{a^2}{2}\\arcsin(x/a) + C"} /> from scratch.

## When substitution fails (or is not enough)

Not every integral yields to substitution. If you pick $u$ but $du$ does not appear in the integrand — and no constant multiple can produce it — try a different $u$ or abandon substitution. For instance, $\int x\cos(x)\, dx$: choosing $u = \cos(x)$ gives $du = -\sin(x)\, dx$, which does not appear; choosing $u = x$ gives $du = dx$, which leaves $\cos(u)$ with no antiderivative you can write down. The $x$ and $\cos(x)$ are not connected by a derivative relationship. That is the signature that you need [integration by parts](/courses/calculus/15-integration-by-parts) instead.

## The many faces of substitution

- **Geometry:** substitution is literally a **change of coordinates** — reparametrizing the integration variable so a complicated function becomes simple. It is the one-dimensional version of the coordinate transforms used everywhere from multivariable calculus (the Jacobian) to general relativity.
- **Physics:** converting between coordinate systems (Cartesian to polar, lab frame to center-of-mass) is a substitution; the $du$ generalizes to the Jacobian determinant in higher dimensions.
- **Probability:** computing expectations $\int x f(x)\, dx$ under a change of variable (e.g. standardizing a normal random variable) is substitution, with the density transformed accordingly.
- **Machine learning:** reparametrizing a loss surface (e.g. natural gradient, whitening) is a multivariate substitution that improves the conditioning of optimization.

## Modern applications

In **physics**, every change of variables in a line, surface, or volume integral is a substitution generalizing to higher dimensions — the "$du$" becomes the Jacobian $|\det J|\,du\,dv$. Computing the gravitational potential of a non-uniform body, or the moment of inertia of an off-center object, almost always involves a well-chosen coordinate substitution.

In **probability and statistics**, the standardization $z = (x-\mu)/\sigma$ that converts any normal distribution to the standard normal is a u-substitution; the density acquires a $1/\sigma$ factor that is precisely the $du$ bookkeeping. Monte Carlo methods in **machine learning** (e.g. change of variables in normalizing flows) learn substitutions as invertible neural networks, with the Jacobian determinant playing the role of $du/dx$.

In **engineering**, integration by substitution is the workhorse of computing work, energy, and flux whenever a problem is more naturally described in cylindrical or spherical coordinates.

## Connections

This lesson extends the [antiderivative table](/courses/calculus/13-antiderivatives) to composite functions, by reversing the [chain rule](/courses/calculus/04-visualizing-the-chain-rule) you first met when learning [differentiation rules](/courses/calculus/03-differentiation-rules). With [the Fundamental Theorem](/courses/calculus/12-the-fundamental-theorem) guaranteeing that every continuous integrand has an antiderivative, substitution is the first tool you reach for to *find* that antiderivative. When substitution fails — because the integrand is a product of unrelated functions rather than a composite — the next lesson, [integration by parts](/courses/calculus/15-integration-by-parts), reverses the product rule instead; [partial fractions](/courses/calculus/16-partial-fractions) then handles rational integrands.

Across disciplines, substitution is the universal **change of variables** operation: the $du$ that bookkeeps the derivative becomes the Jacobian determinant in multivariable calculus, the standardizing transform in probability, and the coordinate change at the heart of nearly every physics computation. Whenever a problem looks hard in one coordinate system, substitution is the art of finding a coordinate system where it looks easy.
