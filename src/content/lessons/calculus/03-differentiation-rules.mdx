Computing every derivative from the limit definition is tedious. Fortunately, a small set of rules lets us differentiate almost any function you'll encounter. Think of these rules as the alphabet of calculus — every derivative you'll ever compute, no matter how complicated the function looks, is built by combining these five rules in the right order.

For $f(x) = x^2$, notice that $f'(x) = 2x$ is a straight line through the origin — the slope increases linearly. For $f(x) = \sin(x)$, the derivative is $\cos(x)$ — the slope leads the function by a quarter cycle.

## The power rule

If $f(x) = x^n$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"f'(x) = n \\cdot x^{n-1}"} />

This works for any real exponent — positive, negative, or fractional. The power rule is the workhorse of differentiation.

**Examples:**
- $(x^3)' = 3x^2$
- $(x^{-2})' = -2x^{-3}$
- $(\sqrt{x})' = (x^{1/2})' = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}$

## The sum and constant rules

<MathBlock expression={"[cf(x)]' = c \\cdot f'(x)"} />
<MathBlock expression={"[f(x) + g(x)]' = f'(x) + g'(x)"} />

Constants multiply through, and derivatives distribute over addition. These two rules mean you can differentiate any polynomial term-by-term.

## The product rule

<MathBlock expression={"[f(x) \\cdot g(x)]' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)"} />

"First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first." This is essential whenever two functions are multiplied together — like $x^2 \sin(x)$.

There's a geometric picture that makes this formula feel natural. Imagine a rectangle whose sides have lengths $u(x)$ and $v(x)$. The area is $uv$. Now nudge each side by a tiny amount: $u$ grows by $du$ and $v$ grows by $dv$. The new area is $(u + du)(v + dv) = uv + u\,dv + v\,du + du\,dv$. The change in area consists of two thin strips ($u\,dv$ and $v\,du$) plus a tiny corner square ($du\,dv$) that vanishes as the nudges shrink to zero. What's left is exactly the product rule: $d(uv) = u\,dv + v\,du$.

## The chain rule

<MathBlock expression={"[f(g(x))]' = f'(g(x)) \\cdot g'(x)"} />

The chain rule handles **composed functions** — functions nested inside other functions. You differentiate the outer function, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function. We'll explore this visually in the [next lesson](/courses/calculus/04-visualizing-the-chain-rule).

## The quotient rule

<MathBlock expression={"\\left[\\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\\right]' = \\frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}"} />

While useful, the quotient rule is just the product rule applied to $f(x) \cdot [g(x)]^{-1}$ combined with the chain rule. Many people prefer to rewrite quotients as products to avoid memorizing this formula.

<KeyInsight>
The five rules — power, sum, product, quotient, and chain — compose like LEGO blocks. Any function built from these operations can be differentiated mechanically by applying the right rule at each step.
</KeyInsight>
