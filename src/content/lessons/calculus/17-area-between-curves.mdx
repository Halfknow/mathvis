The [definite integral](/courses/calculus/11-the-definite-integral) gives the area under a single curve. What about the area **between** two curves? The answer is beautifully simple: subtract. At each $x$-position, measure the vertical gap between the two functions, then let the integral accumulate all those gaps. The area between curves is the integral of "top minus bottom." But two subtleties demand care. First, you must know *which* curve is on top — and if the curves cross, the roles swap, and you must split the integral at each crossing. Second, sometimes the curves are easier to describe with $x$ as a function of $y$ (think of sideways-oriented regions), in which case you integrate with respect to $y$ instead. The art is in choosing the right variable and the right intervals.

## The formula: top minus bottom

If $f(x) \geq g(x)$ on $[a, b]$, then the area between them is:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Area} = \\int_a^b \\bigl[f(x) - g(x)\\bigr]\\, dx"} />

The integrand $f(x) - g(x)$ is the **vertical gap** between the curves at each $x$. Where the gap is large, area accumulates quickly; where the curves pinch together, little area is added. The integral turns this collection of gaps into one number.

## When curves cross: split the integral

If $f$ and $g$ cross within $[a, b]$ — say at $x = c$ — the sign of $f(x) - g(x)$ flips there. Integrating $f - g$ across the crossing would let positive and negative contributions cancel, *undercounting* the true area (you'd get the signed area, not the geometric area). The fix is to split the integral at each crossing point:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Area} = \\int_a^c \\bigl[f(x) - g(x)\\bigr]\\, dx + \\int_c^b \\bigl[g(x) - f(x)\\bigr]\\, dx"} />

In each sub-interval, the "top" function is whichever is larger there. The absolute value is implicit: always subtract the lower function from the upper. The intersection points are found by solving $f(x) = g(x)$.

### Example: $\sin x$ and $\cos x$

The curves $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ intersect where $\sin x = \cos x$, i.e. at $x = \pi/4 + n\pi$. On $[0, \pi]$, they cross once at $\pi/4$ and again at $5\pi/4$ (outside this interval) — so within $[0, \pi]$ there is one crossing at $\pi/4$. From $0$ to $\pi/4$, $\cos > \sin$ (top is $\cos$); from $\pi/4$ to $\pi$, $\sin > \cos$ (top is $\sin$). You must split:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Area} = \\int_0^{\\pi/4} (\\cos x - \\sin x)\\, dx + \\int_{\\pi/4}^{\\pi} (\\sin x - \\cos x)\\, dx"} />

## Choosing the variable: $dx$ vs $dy$

Sometimes integrating with respect to $y$ is dramatically simpler. The rule: integrate with respect to whichever variable gives **fewer pieces** (fewer crossings, fewer sub-intervals) and **simpler antiderivatives**.

Consider the region between $y = x^2$ and $y = x + 2$. They intersect at $x = -1$ and $x = 2$. Integrating with respect to $x$ is straightforward: top is $x + 2$, bottom is $x^2$, single interval $[-1, 2]$.

But now consider the region between <MathInline expression={"y = \\sqrt{x}"} /> and $y = x - 2$ (or equivalently $x = y^2$ and $x = y + 2$). If you integrate with respect to $x$, the bottom boundary *changes*: it is <MathInline expression={"y = -\\sqrt{x}"} /> for $x < 4$ and $y = x - 2$ for $x > 4$ — you'd need to split. But if you flip the perspective and integrate with respect to $y$, the right boundary is always $x = y + 2$ and the left is always $x = y^2$ — a single integral. The choice of variable determines the difficulty.

**Mnemonic:** if the curves are naturally written as $y = f(x)$ with no piecewise switching, integrate with $dx$ ("top minus bottom"). If the curves are easier as $x = g(y)$, integrate with $dy$ ("right minus left"). Sketching the region is almost always worth the ten seconds.

## The bounds are often the intersections

The integration bounds $a$ and $b$ are usually the $x$-coordinates (or $y$-coordinates) where the curves meet — because that is where the region "starts" and "ends." To find them, set $f(x) = g(x)$ and solve. Sometimes one bound is given by a constraint (e.g. "the region in the first quadrant"), and the other by an intersection. Always sketch before you integrate.

## Three perspectives on "area between curves"

- **Accumulation (calculus):** area = integral of the gap $\int (f - g)\, dx$. The gap is a function of position; integrate it.
- **Geometry (measurement):** area = sum of infinitely many thin vertical rectangles of height $(f - g)$ and width $dx$. The integral is the limit of these rectangles.
- **Signed area (algebra):** $\int f\, dx - \int g\, dx$ = (area under top) − (area under bottom). The area between is the difference of two single-curve areas — which is why the formula is just subtraction.

These three views are equivalent. The first is the one most often used in computation, the second for intuition (think rectangles), and the third as a quick sanity check.

## The many faces of "between"

- **Probability:** the area between two PDFs is the total variation distance between distributions — a measure of how distinguishable they are.
- **Economics:** the area between demand and supply curves is the **consumer plus producer surplus** — the total welfare created by a market.
- **Physics:** work done by a variable force between two configurations is the area between the force curve and the displacement axis.
- **Engineering:** the area between a signal and a threshold determines bit error rates in digital communications.

## Modern applications

In **machine learning**, comparing distributions is a core operation: the area between two ROC curves measures the difference in classifier quality, and the Wasserstein distance between distributions (used in WGANs) is a generalization of "area between CDFs."

In **economics and operations research**, the area between marginal cost and marginal revenue curves gives the deadweight loss of a tax; the area between demand and price gives consumer surplus. Welfare economics is, in a sense, applied "area between curves."

In **signal processing and control**, the area between a signal and its setpoint (the **integrated error**) is a standard metric of controller performance — the integral of the gap tells you how much the system deviated over time.

In **epidemiology**, the area between two epidemic curves (e.g. with and without intervention) quantifies the cases saved by the intervention.

<KeyInsight>
The area between two curves is the integral of the vertical (or horizontal) gap between them. Always subtract the lower from the upper: ∫ [f_top − f_bottom] dx, or ∫ [g_right − g_left] dy. When curves cross, split the integral at each crossing — otherwise positive and negative gaps cancel and you get the signed area, not the true area. Choose the integration variable (x or y) that minimizes the number of pieces and keeps antiderivatives simple. The bounds are typically the intersection points found by solving f(x) = g(x). Three equivalent views: integral of the gap, sum of thin rectangles, or difference of two single-curve areas.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson is the first **application** of the definite integral beyond computing areas under a single curve — it generalizes [the definite integral](/courses/calculus/11-the-definite-integral) to the space *between* two functions, building on [accumulation](/courses/calculus/09-accumulation-and-area). The same "integrate the gap" idea recurs throughout the applications module: [volumes by slicing](/courses/calculus/18-volumes-by-slicing) integrate a cross-sectional area function, and [volumes of revolution](/courses/calculus/19-volumes-of-revolution) compute areas between a curve and the axis of rotation. The technique of splitting an integral at crossing points generalizes to splitting at any discontinuity or change of formula.

Across disciplines, "integrate the difference between two curves" is a universal measurement: welfare loss in economics, distribution distance in statistics and ML, controller error in engineering, and welfare impact in epidemiology. Wherever you compare two quantities across a range and ask "how much total difference is there?", the area between curves is the answer.

<ExerciseBlock number={4}
  prompt="Find the area of the region bounded by x = y² and x = y + 2. Explain why integrating with respect to y is simpler than with respect to x."
  hint="The curves are naturally x-as-a-function-of-y. They intersect where y² = y + 2. Solve for y, then use right-minus-left with dy."
  answer="Intersections: y² = y + 2 → y² − y − 2 = 0 → (y − 2)(y + 1) = 0 → y = −1, 2. For y in (−1, 2): test y = 0: y + 2 = 2 > 0 = y², so the line x = y + 2 is on the right, the parabola x = y² on the left. Area = ∫_{−1}^2 [(y + 2) − y²] dy = [y²/2 + 2y − y³/3]_{−1}^2 = (2 + 4 − 8/3) − (1/2 − 2 + 1/3) = (10/3) − (−7/6) = 10/3 + 7/6 = 27/6 = 9/2. Why dy is simpler: solving x = y² for y gives y = ±√x (two branches), so integrating with respect to x would require splitting the region at x = 1 (where the lower boundary changes from y = −√x to y = x − 2). With dy, there is no splitting — the right boundary is always y + 2 and the left always y². Choosing the right variable saves work."
/>
