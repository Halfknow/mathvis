A **random variable** is not really "random" and not really a "variable" in the algebra sense. It's a **function** that maps each outcome of an experiment to a number.

## Discrete random variables

A discrete random variable takes on a countable set of values. Its behavior is fully described by its **probability mass function** (PMF):

$$P(X = x_i) = p_i$$

where each $p_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i p_i = 1$.

**Example**: Let $X$ be the number of heads in 3 fair coin flips.

| $x$ | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----|---|---|---|---|
| $P(X=x)$ | 1/8 | 3/8 | 3/8 | 1/8 |

## Expected value

The **expected value** (or mean) of a random variable is its probability-weighted average:

$$E[X] = \sum_i x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$

For the coin flip example: $E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{8} + 1 \cdot \frac{3}{8} + 2 \cdot \frac{3}{8} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{8} = 1.5$

The expected value is not necessarily a value the variable can actually take — you can't get 1.5 heads. It's the long-run average if you repeated the experiment many times.

## Variance

The **variance** measures how spread out the values are:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$

High variance means outcomes are spread far from the mean. Low variance means they cluster tightly.

## Continuous random variables

When a variable can take any value in an interval, we use a **probability density function** (PDF) instead of a PMF:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

The density $f(x)$ is not a probability — it can be greater than 1. Only the *area under the curve* between two points gives a probability.

## Key insight

> A random variable turns random outcomes into numbers we can compute with — expected values, variances, and ultimately, predictions.
