Imagine a vast lake — that is your **population**. You cannot drain the lake to count every fish. Instead, you dip in a bucket and pull out a handful — that is your **sample**. From the properties of that handful, you try to infer the properties of the entire lake.

This is the fundamental act of statistics: reasoning from the part to the whole. Probability theory gives you the rules for how samples behave. Statistics turns those rules around and asks: given what I see in the sample, what can I say about the population? This lesson is about the simplest kind of answer — a single number, a **point estimate** — and the two questions you must always ask of any estimator: is it *unbiased* (does it point at the right place on average), and is it *consistent* (does it stop wandering as you collect more data)?

We will also meet two systematic methods for *constructing* estimators: the **method of moments** (match the sample moments to the theoretical moments) and the framework that sets up maximum likelihood estimation in the next lesson. Along the way, we develop the **bias-variance tradeoff** — the central tension that governs every estimation problem in statistics and machine learning.

## Population, sample, statistic

A **population** is the complete set of things you want to know about. It might be every voter in a country, every light bulb from a factory, or every possible measurement of a physical quantity. The population has **parameters** — fixed numbers like the mean $\mu$ and the variance $\sigma^2$ — that you want to learn.

A **sample** is a subset of the population: $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$. Each $X_i$ is a random variable — before you observe it, it could take many values. After observation, it becomes a fixed number $x_i$. The sample is your window into the population.

A **statistic** is any function of the sample. It is a number you compute from your data. The most important examples:

- **Sample mean**: <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X} = \\frac{1}{n}\\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i"} />
- **Sample variance**: <MathInline expression={"S^2 = \\frac{1}{n-1}\\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i - \\bar{X})^2"} />

A statistic is itself a random variable, because it depends on the random sample. If you drew a different sample, you would get a different value. The distribution of a statistic across all possible samples is called its **sampling distribution** — and by the Central Limit Theorem ([lesson 16](/courses/probability/16-central-limit-theorem)), the sampling distribution of $\bar{X}$ is approximately normal for large $n$.

## Estimators and parameters

An **estimator** is a statistic used to guess a population parameter. We use the notation <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta}"} /> (read "theta hat") for an estimator of the parameter $\theta$.

Think of the parameter as a fixed point on a map — it is there, unchanging, whether you look or not. The estimator is your finger, pointing at the map. Each time you draw a new sample, your finger lands in a slightly different spot. The question is: does your finger, on average, point at the right place? And how far does it typically wander?

## Unbiasedness

The **bias** of an estimator measures whether it systematically misses the target:

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Bias}(\\hat{\\theta}) = E[\\hat{\\theta}] - \\theta"} />$

An estimator is **unbiased** if <MathInline expression={"E[\\hat{\\theta}] = \\theta"} />. Geometrically: if you took infinitely many samples and averaged all the estimates, you would land exactly on the true parameter. Your finger points at the right spot on average.

A biased estimator systematically overshoots or undershoots. It is like a scale that always reads half a kilogram too heavy — precise, perhaps, but not accurate.

### Theorem: The sample mean is unbiased for mu

For i.i.d. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with $E[X_i] = \mu$, the sample mean $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$ satisfies $E[\bar{X}] = \mu$.

*Proof.* By linearity of expectation ([lesson 06](/courses/probability/06-expectation-and-variance)):

$$E[\bar{X}] = E\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} E[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \cdot n\mu = \mu$$

No matter the sample size, no matter the shape of the population distribution, the sample mean points at the true mean on average. Linearity of expectation is the hero — it does not even require independence.

## The n-1 denominator

The sample variance uses $n - 1$ in the denominator, not $n$. This is not a typo. It is a correction that makes $S^2$ unbiased for $\sigma^2$.

Here is the geometric intuition. Each term <MathInline expression={"(X_i - \\bar{X})^2"} /> measures the squared distance from $X_i$ to the sample mean <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} />, not to the population mean $\mu$. But <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> is itself computed from the sample — it tends to sit closer to the data points than $\mu$ does. The deviations from <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> are systematically smaller than the deviations from $\mu$.

Imagine three data points on a line. Their sample mean sits at the center of those three points. The true population mean might be somewhere else entirely. The three distances to <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> are always a bit shorter than the three distances to $\mu$, because <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> chases the data.

The total amount by which those squared deviations shrink is exactly one degree of freedom. Dividing by $n - 1$ instead of $n$ inflates the average just enough to compensate for that shrinkage. The result:

### Theorem: The sample variance is unbiased for sigma^2

For i.i.d. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$, the sample variance <MathInline expression={"S^2 = \\frac{1}{n-1}\\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \\bar{X})^2"} /> satisfies $E[S^2] = \sigma^2$.

*Proof (sketch).* Expand <MathInline expression={"\\sum(X_i - \\bar{X})^2 = \\sum X_i^2 - n\\bar{X}^2"} />. Take expectations: $E[\sum X_i^2] = n(\sigma^2 + \mu^2)$ and $E[\bar{X}^2] = \text{Var}(\bar{X}) + (E[\bar{X}])^2 = \sigma^2/n + \mu^2$. So $E[\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2] = n(\sigma^2+\mu^2) - n(\sigma^2/n + \mu^2) = n\sigma^2 - \sigma^2 = (n-1)\sigma^2$. Dividing by $n-1$ gives $E[S^2] = \sigma^2$.

## Consistency

An estimator is **consistent** if it converges to the true parameter as the sample size grows:

$<MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta}_n \\xrightarrow{P} \\theta \\quad \\text{as } n \\to \\infty"} />$

Picture the sampling distribution as a bell curve centered on the estimator's expected value. As $n$ grows, two things happen: the center slides toward $\theta$ (bias shrinks) and the bell narrows (variance shrinks). A consistent estimator is one whose bell collapses onto the true value — your finger stops wandering and settles on the target.

The sample mean <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> is consistent for $\mu$ by the **Law of Large Numbers** ([lesson 15](/courses/probability/15-law-of-large-numbers)). As you pour more data into the average, it locks onto the true mean. The sample variance $S^2$ is consistent for $\sigma^2$ by the same logic.

Note the difference between unbiasedness and consistency. An estimator can be unbiased but not consistent (if its variance does not shrink with $n$), or biased but consistent (if the bias shrinks to zero as $n$ grows). Consistency is usually the more important property — you want the estimator to eventually be right, even if it is slightly off for small samples.

## Method of moments

How do you *construct* an estimator in the first place? One of the oldest and most intuitive methods is the **method of moments**. The idea is beautifully simple: equate the sample moments to the theoretical moments, and solve for the parameters.

The $k$-th **sample moment** is <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\mu}_k = \\frac{1}{n}\\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^k"} />, which by the LLN converges to the $k$-th **theoretical moment** <MathInline expression={"\\mu_k = E[X^k]"} />. If your distribution has $m$ unknown parameters $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_m$, write down the first $m$ theoretical moments as functions of the parameters, set them equal to the corresponding sample moments, and solve the resulting system.

### Theorem: Method of moments estimator is consistent

Under mild regularity conditions, the method of moments estimator <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta}^{\\text{MM}}_n"} /> converges in probability to the true parameter $\theta$ as $n \to \infty$.

*Intuition.* The sample moments converge to the theoretical moments by the LLN. If the map from parameters to moments is continuous and invertible (a "regularity condition"), then the inverse map sends the convergent sample moments to a convergent estimator of the parameters.

### Example: method of moments for the uniform distribution

Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. uniform on $[a, b]$, with $a$ and $b$ unknown. The first theoretical moment is $E[X] = (a+b)/2$ and the second is $E[X^2] = (b-a)^2/12 + ((a+b)/2)^2 = (a^2 + ab + b^2)/3$. Set these equal to the sample moments:

$$\frac{\hat{\mu}_1}{\hat{\mu}_2} = \frac{a+b}{2}, \quad \hat{\mu}_2 = \frac{a^2 + ab + b^2}{3}$$

Solving (using $\text{Var}(X) = (b-a)^2/12 = \hat{\mu}_2 - \hat{\mu}_1^2$):

$$\hat{a} = \hat{\mu}_1 - \sqrt{3(\hat{\mu}_2 - \hat{\mu}_1^2)}, \quad \hat{b} = \hat{\mu}_1 + \sqrt{3(\hat{\mu}_2 - \hat{\mu}_1^2)}$$

Equivalently, <MathInline expression={"\\hat{a} = \\bar{X} - \\sqrt{3}\\,S_n"} /> and <MathInline expression={"\\hat{b} = \\bar{X} + \\sqrt{3}\\,S_n"} /> where <MathInline expression={"S_n^2 = \\frac{1}{n}\\sum(X_i - \\bar{X})^2"} /> is the (biased) sample variance. The method of moments gives a clean, closed-form estimator from two sample quantities you already know how to compute.

## The bias-variance tradeoff

Every estimator faces a fundamental tension. You want it to be close to the true parameter. The distance between <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta}"} /> and $\theta$ decomposes into two pieces — **bias** (how far the *center* of the sampling distribution is from $\theta$) and **variance** (how much the estimator spreads around its own center). This is the **bias-variance decomposition**:

$<MathInline expression={"E\\!\\left[(\\hat{\\theta} - \\theta)^2\\right] = \\underbrace{\\text{Bias}(\\hat{\\theta})^2}_{\\text{systematic error}} + \\underbrace{\\text{Var}(\\hat{\\theta})}_{\\text{random error}}"} />$

The left side is the **mean squared error** (MSE) — the average squared distance from the estimator to the truth. The decomposition says: total error = squared bias + variance.

*Proof.* Write <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta} - \\theta = (\\hat{\\theta} - E[\\hat{\\theta}]) + (E[\\hat{\\theta}] - \\theta)"} />. The first term has mean zero and variance $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$; the second term is the bias (a constant). When you expand the square, the cross-term vanishes (because the first term has mean zero), leaving $E[(\hat{\theta}-\theta)^2] = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) + \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2$.

The tradeoff: sometimes you can *reduce* variance by *introducing* a little bias. Shrinkage estimators (like the James-Stein estimator) deliberately bias the estimate toward zero to dramatically reduce variance, achieving lower total MSE than the unbiased sample mean. This is the same phenomenon that powers **regularization** in machine learning — ridge regression and LASSO add bias (penalizing large coefficients) to reduce variance (preventing overfitting). The result is a model that is slightly wrong on average but much more stable across datasets.

## Three perspectives on point estimation

1. **As a finger pointing at a target:** the parameter is fixed; the estimator is random. Bias tells you whether the finger points at the right place on average. Variance tells you how much the finger shakes. Consistency tells you whether the shaking calms down as you collect more data. This geometric picture unifies all the evaluation criteria.
2. **As a recipe (construction methods):** the method of moments matches sample moments to theoretical moments — simple, consistent, but sometimes inefficient. Maximum likelihood (next lesson) picks the parameter that makes the data most probable — efficient but requires specifying the full distribution. Both are principled ways to turn "I want to estimate theta" into a concrete formula.
3. **As a tradeoff (bias vs variance):** the MSE decomposition reveals that total error is a sum of two competing quantities. Reducing bias often increases variance and vice versa. The art of estimation — and of machine learning — is navigating this tradeoff: how much bias are you willing to accept in exchange for lower variance?

## Modern applications

**Opinion polling.** A poll estimates the fraction of voters supporting a candidate using the sample proportion $\hat{p} = (\text{number supporting})/n$. This is an unbiased and consistent estimator of the true $p$ (by the LLN). The "margin of error" reported in polls is derived from the variance $\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}$ via the CLT. Every political poll, market survey, and A/B test is a point estimation problem in disguise.

**Regularization in machine learning.** Ridge regression adds an $L^2$ penalty $\lambda\|\beta\|^2$ to the least-squares objective. This shrinks the coefficients toward zero, introducing bias but dramatically reducing variance — especially when features are correlated (multicollinearity). The bias-variance tradeoff is the mathematical heart of all regularization: you accept a small systematic error to gain much larger reductions in random error, lowering the total MSE. LASSO, elastic net, and dropout in neural networks all play the same game.

**James-Stein estimator.** In a surprising result, when estimating three or more means simultaneously, the sample mean is *inadmissible* — there exists an estimator with strictly lower MSE. The James-Stein estimator shrinks each estimate toward a common point (the grand mean), trading bias for variance. This shattered the intuition that unbiasedness is always desirable and launched the modern theory of shrinkage estimation and empirical Bayes methods.

**Sample size determination.** The variance $\sigma^2/n$ tells you exactly how much data you need for a desired precision. To halve the standard error, quadruple the sample size. This is the calculation behind every clinical trial (how many patients to enroll), every engineering tolerance study (how many parts to measure), and every power analysis (how many subjects for a detectable effect). Point estimation is not just about the formula — it is about knowing how much data that formula needs.

An estimator is a finger pointing at a hidden target. The mean squared error decomposes as bias-squared plus variance — the bias-variance tradeoff — which is why regularization in machine learning deliberately introduces bias to reduce variance. The method of moments and maximum likelihood (next lesson) are two principled recipes for constructing estimators with these properties.

## Connections

This lesson is built directly on [lesson 06](/courses/probability/06-expectation-and-variance): the sample mean's unbiasedness is linearity of expectation, and the $n-1$ correction comes from the variance-of-a-sum algebra. The **Law of Large Numbers** ([lesson 15](/courses/probability/15-law-of-large-numbers)) provides consistency — it is the theorem that says $\bar{X} \to \mu$. The **Central Limit Theorem** ([lesson 16](/courses/probability/16-central-limit-theorem)) provides the sampling distribution — the bell curve that the estimator's values form across repeated samples, with width $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.

The forward link is to **maximum likelihood estimation** ([lesson 18](/courses/probability/18-maximum-likelihood-estimation)), the second great construction method for estimators. Where the method of moments matches moments, MLE matches the likelihood function — and it achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound asymptotically, making it the most *efficient* estimator under regularity conditions. The bias-variance tradeoff introduced here then reappears everywhere: in **confidence intervals** ([lesson 19](/courses/probability/19-confidence-intervals)) (which quantify the variance), in **hypothesis testing** ([lesson 20](/courses/probability/20-hypothesis-testing)) (which balances the two types of error), and in the design of every machine learning model.

Cross-disciplinarily, point estimation is the bridge from probability (the forward model: given parameters, what is the distribution of the data?) to statistics (the inverse problem: given data, what are the parameters?). This inversion — inferring hidden causes from observed effects — is the structure of every empirical science, every sensor system, and every signal processing pipeline. The Kalman filter, particle filters, and Bayesian inference all generalize the point estimation framework to dynamic and uncertain settings.

  hint="Write theta-hat - theta = (theta-hat - E[theta-hat]) + (E[theta-hat] - theta). The first term is the 'centered' estimator (mean zero, variance Var(theta-hat)). The second term is the bias (a constant). Expand the square and take expectations."
  answer="Let mu_hat = E[theta-hat] and b = Bias(theta-hat) = mu_hat - theta. Write theta-hat - theta = (theta-hat - mu_hat) + (mu_hat - theta) = (theta-hat - mu_hat) + b. Expand the square: (theta-hat - theta)^2 = (theta-hat - mu_hat)^2 + 2*(theta-hat - mu_hat)*b + b^2. Take expectations: E[(theta-hat - theta)^2] = E[(theta-hat - mu_hat)^2] + 2*b*E[theta-hat - mu_hat] + b^2. Now E[theta-hat - mu_hat] = mu_hat - mu_hat = 0 (the centered estimator has mean zero). So the cross-term vanishes: E[(theta-hat - theta)^2] = E[(theta-hat - mu_hat)^2] + 0 + b^2 = Var(theta-hat) + Bias(theta-hat)^2. QED. Why you cannot minimize MSE by minimizing only bias or only variance: Consider the family of constant estimators theta-hat_c = c (a fixed number regardless of data). Bias(theta-hat_c) = c - theta, which is zero only at c = theta — but theta is unknown! Var(theta-hat_c) = 0 (it's a constant). So MSE(theta-hat_c) = (c - theta)^2, minimized at c = theta, which is unachievable. Conversely, you can drive variance to zero by using a constant, but that maximizes bias unless you already know theta. The only estimator with both zero bias and zero variance would be theta-hat = theta always — i.e., perfect knowledge of the parameter, which defeats the purpose of estimation. In practice, reducing variance (e.g., by averaging more data or adding regularization) often increases bias, and vice versa. The optimal estimator navigates this tradeoff: it accepts as much bias as needed to reduce variance enough that the total MSE is minimized. This is why shrinkage estimators (James-Stein), regularized regression (ridge, LASSO), and Bayesian priors can all beat the unbiased estimator in practice — they trade a small bias for a large variance reduction."
