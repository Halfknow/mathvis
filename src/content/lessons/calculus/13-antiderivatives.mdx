If the derivative takes you from $F$ to $f$, the antiderivative takes you back from $f$ to $F$. It's the "undo" button for differentiation. Antidifferentiation is like running a film backward — each derivative rule has a reverse. But there's a twist: the original function could have had any constant added, so you get a whole *family* of solutions, not just one. Mastering antiderivatives is the price of admission for the Fundamental Theorem — once you can reverse derivatives, every definite integral becomes a subtraction.

## The definition

$F(x)$ is an **antiderivative** of $f(x)$ if $F'(x) = f(x)$.

Simple enough. But there's a catch: antiderivatives are **not unique**. If $F(x)$ is an antiderivative, then so is $F(x) + C$ for any constant $C$, because the derivative of a constant is zero. Derivatives *destroy* constant information; antiderivatives can't recover it.

## Theorem: Antiderivatives differ by a constant

If $F$ and $G$ are both antiderivatives of the same function $f$ on an interval, then $F(x) - G(x) = C$ for some constant $C$. In other words, **all antiderivatives of $f$ are vertical shifts of one another**.

The proof is one line: $(F - G)' = F' - G' = f - f = 0$, and the only functions with zero derivative everywhere on an interval are constants (a consequence of the Mean Value Theorem). So $F - G = C$.

**Hypothesis:** both antiderivatives live on a single connected interval. **Conclusion:** they differ by a constant. **Intuition:** differentiating erases vertical position (the "$+C$"), so running it in reverse leaves that position unspecified — any vertical shift is equally valid.

## The family of antiderivatives

The **indefinite integral** captures the whole family:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int f(x)\\, dx = F(x) + C"} />

where $C$ is the "constant of integration" — an arbitrary constant representing the lost information. The notation intentionally mirrors the definite integral, because (by the Fundamental Theorem) the definite integral $\int_a^b f\, dx$ is computed by evaluating *any* antiderivative at the bounds.

## The standard antiderivative table

Every derivative rule you know has a reverse. Here is the core table — commit it to memory, because every integration technique (substitution, parts, partial fractions) is ultimately a strategy to rewrite an integral until it matches one of these forms.

| $f(x)$ | $F(x) + C$ | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $x^n \; (n \neq -1)$ | <MathInline expression={"\\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C"} /> | **Power rule in reverse.** Drop the exponent, divide by the new one. |
| <MathInline expression={"\\dfrac{1}{x}"} /> | $\ln\lvert x\rvert + C$ | Absolute value: the log is defined for negative $x$ too. The $n=-1$ exception to the power rule. |
| $e^x$ | $e^x + C$ | Exponential is its own antiderivative. |
| $b^x$ | <MathInline expression={"\\dfrac{b^x}{\\ln b} + C"} /> | General exponential; divide by the natural log of the base. |
| $\sin x$ | $-\cos x + C$ | Mind the sign — sine integrates to *negative* cosine. |
| $\cos x$ | $\sin x + C$ | |
| $\sec^2 x$ | $\tan x + C$ | |
| <MathInline expression={"\\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}"} /> | $\arctan x + C$ | The reverse of the arctangent derivative. |
| <MathInline expression={"\\dfrac{1}{\\sqrt{1-x^2}}"} /> | $\arcsin x + C$ | The reverse of the arcsine derivative. |

Each row is just a derivative rule read backwards. The power rule in reverse is the workhorse: <MathInline expression={"\\int x^n\\, dx = \\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C"} /> for $n \neq -1$, and the special case <MathInline expression={"\\int \\frac{1}{x}\\, dx = \\ln|x| + C"} /> covers the forbidden exponent.

## Why "+C" matters

The constant $C$ isn't just a formality — it carries real information that differentiation erased. Consider:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int 2x\\, dx = x^{2} + C"} />

If an object's velocity is $v(t) = 2t$, its position is <MathInline expression={"s(t) = t^{2} + C"} />. The constant $C$ is the **starting position**. Without it, you know the object accelerates uniformly but you don't know *where it started*. To pin down a unique solution, you need an **initial condition** — a single value like $s(0) = 5$ that determines $C = 5$. This is the seed of differential equations: an antiderivative gives a family of solutions, and an initial condition selects one.

In the FTC context, the constant conveniently cancels:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_a^b f(x)\\, dx = \\bigl[F(x) + C\\bigr]_a^b = (F(b) + C) - (F(a) + C) = F(b) - F(a)"} />

The constant doesn't affect definite integrals — only the shape of the antiderivative family. This is why, for computing areas via Part 1 of the FTC, you may pick *any* antiderivative (the $C$ washes out). But for solving differential equations or finding a specific position function, $C$ is essential.

## The connection to the area function

Here is the deep link to the Fundamental Theorem. FTC Part 2 says the area function $A(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$ is itself an antiderivative of $f$, since $A'(x) = f(x)$. So the area function is *one specific member* of the antiderivative family — the one with $A(a) = 0$. By the "differ by a constant" theorem, every antiderivative $F$ satisfies $F(x) = A(x) + C$ for some $C$, which rearranges to:

<MathBlock expression={"A(x) = \\int_{a}^{x} f(t)\\, dt = F(x) - F(a)"} />

Setting $x = b$ recovers FTC Part 1: $\int_a^b f(t)\, dt = F(b) - F(a)$. So the two halves of the FTC are really the statement "antiderivatives and area functions are the same thing up to a constant." The whole theory of integration is, in a sense, the theory of antiderivatives.

## The many faces of antidifferentiation

- **Physics:** recovering position from velocity, velocity from acceleration, work from force, energy from power — every "undo the rate" problem is an antiderivative. Newton's second law $F = ma$ becomes a differential equation whose solution is an antiderivative family.
- **Probability:** the cumulative distribution function is an antiderivative of the density. The "+C" is fixed by the requirement $F(-\infty) = 0$.
- **Machine learning:** integrating a gradient field recovers a potential; Bayesian inference integrates likelihoods over parameters. And the reason automatic differentiation works at all is that differentiation rules (which antiderivatives reverse) compose cleanly through computation graphs.
- **Differential equations:** most physical laws are stated as equations involving derivatives; solving them means finding antiderivatives (often with initial conditions to fix $C$).

## Modern applications

In **physics**, every conservation law hides an antiderivative: energy is the antiderivative of force with respect to position, momentum the antiderivative of force with respect to time. Recovering a trajectory from acceleration is two antidifferentiations — and the constants are the initial position and velocity.

In **machine learning**, the gradient of a loss function is a derivative; optimization algorithms implicitly seek points where an "antiderivative" (the loss itself) is minimized. More concretely, **automatic differentiation** in frameworks like PyTorch and JAX implements the derivative rules whose reverses populate the antiderivative table — and reverse-mode autodiff is what makes training deep networks tractable.

In **differential equations**, the simplest equation $y' = f(x)$ is solved by $y = \int f\, dx + C$, and an initial condition pins down $C$. Antiderivatives are the building blocks for solving harder equations (separable, linear, exact) via the techniques that follow this lesson.

## Connections

This lesson builds the technical skill that [the Fundamental Theorem](/courses/calculus/12-the-fundamental-theorem) demands: finding antiderivatives. FTC Part 1 reduces every definite integral to "find any antiderivative, then subtract" — so fluency with the antiderivative table is the rate-limiting step for actually computing integrals. The next lessons — [u-substitution](/courses/calculus/14-u-substitution) (the reverse of the chain rule) and [integration by parts](/courses/calculus/15-integration-by-parts) (the reverse of the product rule) — extend this table by recognizing more complex integrands as derivatives of composite or products. Each is a strategy to massage an integral into a form the basic table can handle.

Across disciplines, antidifferentiation is the universal "undo the rate" operation: recovering position from velocity in physics, recovering a CDF from a density in probability, recovering a potential from a gradient field, and recovering a trajectory from a differential equation. The "+C" reflects genuine lost information — an initial condition — which is why it matters in dynamics and vanishes in pure area computations.

  prompt="Find the general antiderivative of f(x) = 1/x. Why is the answer ln|x| + C rather than ln(x) + C, and what are the two separate families of antiderivatives?"

  answer="∫(1/x) dx = ln|x| + C. The absolute value is essential because 1/x is defined for both positive and negative x (excluding 0), but ln(x) is defined only for x > 0. Two derivatives erased → two constants → two initial conditions to recover them."

  answer="An antiderivative is F(x) = x⁴ − x² + x (derivative: 4x³ − 2x + 1). By FTC Part 1: ∫₁³ (4x³ − 2x + 1) dx = F(3) − F(1) = (81 − 9 + 3) − (1 − 1 + 1) = 75 − 1 = 74. <ExerciseBlock number={7}
  prompt="Explain why antiderivatives differ by a constant (state the theorem and prove it in one line using the Mean Value Theorem). Then explain how this fact, combined with FTC Part 2, yields FTC Part 1."
  hint="If F and G are both antiderivatives of f, what is (F − G)'? What does the MVT say about functions with zero derivative? How does this relate F to the area function A?"
  answer="Theorem: if F and G are antiderivatives of f on an interval, then F − G = C for some constant C. Proof: (F − G)' = F' − G' = f − f = 0 on the interval. By the Mean Value Theorem, any function with zero derivative everywhere on an interval is constant (otherwise some secant would have nonzero slope, contradicting the MVT). So F − G = C. Now combine with FTC Part 2: the area function A(x) = ∫ₐˣ f satisfies A'(x) = f(x), so A is an antiderivative of f. By the theorem, any other antiderivative F differs from A by a constant: F(x) = A(x) + C. Rearranging, A(x) = F(x) − C, and setting x = a gives 0 = A(a) = F(a) − C, so C = F(a). Therefore A(b) = ∫ₐᵇ f = F(b) − F(a) — which is FTC Part 1. The 'differ by a constant' theorem is the glue joining the two halves of the FTC."
