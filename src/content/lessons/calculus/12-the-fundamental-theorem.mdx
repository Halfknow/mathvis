This is the moment everything connects. The derivative and the integral — two ideas that seem completely different — are actually **inverse operations**. This single insight, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC), is why calculus is one subject, not two.

Think of the dashboard of a car. The **speedometer** reads instantaneous velocity — that's the derivative. The **odometer** records total distance traveled — that's the integral. The FTC says they're inverses of each other: if you know the speed at every moment, you can recover the total distance, and if you know how the distance is changing, you know the speed. Differentiation and integration are two sides of the same coin.

## Part 1: The integral undoes the derivative

If $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ (meaning $F'(x) = f(x)$), then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = F(b) - F(a)"} />

To compute an area, you don't need rectangles. You just need an antiderivative. The integral of the derivative gives you back the original function's net change.

## Part 2: The derivative undoes the integral

Define $A(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$ — the accumulated area from $a$ to $x$. Then:

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = f(x)"} />

The rate at which the accumulated area changes is the original function. If you differentiate the integral, you get back the integrand.

## Why this theorem matters

Before the FTC, computing areas required infinite sums of infinitesimal rectangles — a philosophical headache. After the FTC, you just find an antiderivative and plug in the bounds.

The FTC bridges two worlds:
- **Differential calculus** (rates of change, slopes, derivatives)
- **Integral calculus** (accumulation, areas, integrals)

They are not separate subjects. They are two faces of the same coin. This is why calculus is one unified discipline — differentiation takes you from totals to rates, and integration takes you from rates back to totals.

**Isaac Newton** and **Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** both discovered this connection independently in the late 17th century, each developing his own notation and approach. Newton thought in terms of "fluxions" (rates of change of flowing quantities), while Leibniz thought in terms of sums of infinitesimal differences. Their independent discovery of the same deep structure is one of the great parallel discoveries in the history of mathematics — and it sparked one of its bitterest priority disputes.

## The intuition

Think of it like a bank account:
- $f(t)$ is your income rate at time $t$ (dollars per month).
- $F(t)$ is your total balance at time $t$ (dollars).
- $\int_a^b f(t)\, dt$ is how much you earned between month $a$ and month $b$ — which equals $F(b) - F(a)$.
- The rate of change of your balance is your income rate: $F'(t) = f(t)$.

<KeyInsight>
Differentiation and integration are inverse operations. The derivative of the integral gives back the original function. The integral of the derivative gives back the net change. This is the Fundamental Theorem — the bridge between rates and totals.
</KeyInsight>
