You get a phone call from your doctor. The test came back positive.

Your stomach drops. But before you panic, there is a question hiding inside the result: *given* that the test is positive, what is the actual chance you have the disease? That number is almost always smaller than people think.

This is conditional probability. Every time you learn something new — a test result, a weather report, a card that was revealed — the universe of possible outcomes shrinks. Conditional probability is the mathematics of working inside that smaller universe.

## The shrinking sample space

Imagine the sample space $\Omega$ as a big rectangle. Every point inside is a possible outcome. Now someone tells you: "event $B$ happened." Visically, you throw away everything outside $B$. The region $B$ becomes your new world. Your new sample space.

Inside that smaller world, you ask: what is the probability of $A$? You only count the part of $A$ that still exists — the overlap $A \cap B$. Then you divide by the size of your new world, $B$.

$<MathInline expression={"P(A \\mid B) = \\frac{P(A \\cap B)}{P(B)}"} />$

Read the vertical bar as "given." The formula says: of all the outcomes in $B$, what fraction also lie in $A$?

Think of it spatially. Before the news, $A$ occupied a certain fraction of the full rectangle. After the news, the rectangle has been cut down to $B$. The fraction of $B$ that is also $A$ — that is your conditional probability. The world shrank, and your measurement adapted.

A quick check: if $P(B) = 0$, the formula breaks. You cannot condition on an impossible event. There is no smaller world to move into.

## The multiplication rule

Rearrange the conditional probability formula and you get the multiplication rule:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)$$

This reads naturally from left to right: the chance that both $A$ and $B$ happen equals the chance that $B$ happens, times the chance that $A$ happens *once you already know* $B$.

You can also flip the roles:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)$$

Both expressions are the same quantity, just arrived at from different directions. This symmetry is what powers Bayes' theorem later.

### Chaining with three events

The pattern extends. For three events:

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A) \cdot P(C \mid A \cap B)$$

Picture a tree. At the trunk, the full sample space. The first branch splits into $A$ and not-$A$. If you follow the $A$ branch, the world has already narrowed — now you split into $B$ and not-$B$, but only inside $A$. Each branch narrows further. The probability at any leaf is the product of probabilities along the path that got you there.

This tree metaphor is one of the most useful tools in all of probability. Whenever you see the word "given" or "then," draw a tree.

## Theorem: The Law of Total Probability

If $B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_n$ partition the sample space (they are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive), then for any event $A$:

$<MathInline expression={"P(A) = \\sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \\mid B_i) \\cdot P(B_i)"} />$

This says: to find the probability of $A$, decompose it by *how* $A$ can happen. Each pathway $B_i$ has its own probability $P(B_i)$, and within that pathway, $A$ occurs with probability $P(A \mid B_i)$. Sum over all pathways.

Geometrically: the rectangle $A$ is sliced into pieces by the partition <MathInline expression={"\\{B_i\\}"} />. The total area of $A$ is the sum of the areas of its slices. This theorem is the engine that makes Bayes' theorem work — it computes the denominator $P(E)$ by summing over all hypotheses.

## Independence as a special case

Sometimes learning $B$ tells you nothing about $A$. The world shrinks, but $A$ occupies the same *fraction* of the smaller world as it did of the full world.

$$P(A \mid B) = P(A)$$

When this happens, $A$ and $B$ are **independent**. Plugging into the multiplication rule:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$

Coin flips are the classic example. The second coin does not remember the first. Knowing the first flip was heads does not change the probability of the second flip being heads — it stays $1/2$.

Independence is the exception, not the rule. Most real events are entangled. Rain and wet sidewalks are not independent. Smoking and lung cancer are not independent. Conditional probability is the tool for those entangled situations.

## A worked example: the medical test

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test for the disease has:

- **Sensitivity** 99%: if you have the disease, the test is positive 99% of the time.
- **Specificity** 95%: if you do not have the disease, the test is negative 95% of the time.

You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

Intuition screams "about 99%!" The test is excellent, right? Let us see.

### Building the tree

Imagine 10,000 people taking the test.

**First branch — disease or no disease:**

- 100 people have the disease (1% of 10,000)
- 9,900 people do not have the disease

**Second branch — test result:**

- Of the 100 sick people, 99 test positive (sensitivity 99%)
- Of the 9,900 healthy people, 495 test positive (the test is wrong 5% of the time)

**Total positive tests:** $99 + 495 = 594$

**Positive tests that are actually sick:** 99

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease} \\mid \\text{positive}) = \\frac{99}{594} = \\frac{1}{6} \\approx 16.7\\%"} />$

Sixteen percent. Despite a seemingly excellent test, a positive result only means a one-in-six chance of disease. The reason: the disease is rare. Those 495 false positives from the huge healthy population overwhelm the 99 true positives.

This is why doctors order confirmatory tests. The second test, conditioned on the first being positive, operates in a very different world — the disease prevalence among people who already tested positive is much higher than 1%.

### The formal calculation

Using the conditional probability formula:

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease} \\mid \\text{positive}) = \\frac{P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{disease}) \\cdot P(\\text{disease})}{P(\\text{positive})}"} />$

The denominator expands by the law of total probability:

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{positive}) = P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{disease}) \\cdot P(\\text{disease}) + P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{no disease}) \\cdot P(\\text{no disease})"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{positive}) = 0.99 \\times 0.01 + 0.05 \\times 0.99 = 0.0099 + 0.0495 = 0.0594"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease} \\mid \\text{positive}) = \\frac{0.0099}{0.0594} = \\frac{1}{6}"} />$

Same answer. The tree just makes the counting visible.

### The prosecutor's fallacy

The medical test illustrates a famous error: confusing <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{evidence} \\mid \\text{hypothesis})"} /> with <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{hypothesis} \\mid \\text{evidence})"} />. A prosecutor argues: "The chance of this DNA match given the defendant is innocent is 1 in a million — so the defendant is almost certainly guilty." But this flips the conditional. The relevant question is <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{innocent} \\mid \\text{match})"} />, which depends on the prior <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{innocent})"} /> — how many other people could have left the DNA. In a city of 10 million, a 1-in-a-million match yields ~10 suspects, so <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{this defendant is guilty} \\mid \\text{match})"} /> could be much less than 1. This is exactly Bayes' theorem in action.

<KeyInsight>
Conditional probability is not a new kind of probability — it is the same probability, measured inside a smaller world. When information arrives, the sample space shrinks, and every calculation adapts to that smaller world. The law of total probability decomposes an event into pathways; the multiplication rule chains conditions through a tree.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

Conditional probability is the bridge from the **axioms** of [lesson 01](/courses/probability/01-what-is-probability) to **Bayes' theorem** in [lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-bayes-theorem). The multiplication rule $P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$ is the algebraic fact that, when read in both directions, produces Bayes' theorem. The law of total probability is the denominator of every Bayesian update.

Independence (the special case $P(A|B) = P(A)$) connects forward to [lesson 13](/courses/probability/13-independence), where we explore how joint distributions factor for independent variables. The tree-chaining technique reappears in **Markov chains** (lesson 25), where each step depends only on the previous — a form of conditional probability called the Markov property. The prosecutor's fallacy connects to **hypothesis testing** (lesson 20), where the distinction between <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{data} | H_0)"} /> and <MathInline expression={"P(H_0 | \\text{data})"} /> is the crux of the frequentist vs Bayesian debate.

In machine learning, conditional probability is the foundation of **generative models** — a model specifies <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{output} | \\text{input})"} />, and training adjusts this conditional distribution. Naive Bayes classifiers (lesson 04) assume conditional independence of features given the class.

<ExerciseBlock number={2}

  hint="After removing one red marble, the bag is a different place. How many red marbles remain? How many total?"
  answer="After drawing one red marble, 3 red and 6 blue remain (9 total). P(second red | first red) = 3/9 = 1/3. Compare to P(second red) without conditioning = 4/10 = 2/5. Knowing the first was red lowered the probability — the events are dependent (negatively correlated)."

<ExerciseBlock number={3}
  prompt="In a certain town, 60% of days are cloudy. On cloudy days, it rains 40% of the time. On clear days, it rains only 10% of the time. You look outside and see rain. What is the probability that it was cloudy?"
  hint="Use the multiplication rule to find P(cloudy and rain) and P(clear and rain). Then compute P(cloudy | rain) using the definition of conditional probability."
  answer="P(cloudy ∩ rain) = P(rain | cloudy)·P(cloudy) = 0.40 × 0.60 = 0.24. P(clear ∩ rain) = P(rain | clear)·P(clear) = 0.10 × 0.40 = 0.04. P(rain) = 0.24 + 0.04 = 0.28 (law of total probability). P(cloudy | rain) = 0.24/0.28 = 6/7 ≈ 85.7%. This is Bayes' theorem in disguise — we'll formalize it in lesson 04."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={4}
  prompt="Use the law of total probability: a factory has three machines producing 50%, 30%, and 20% of the output, with defect rates 2%, 4%, and 5% respectively. What is the overall defect rate?"
  hint="P(defect) = Σ P(defect | machine_i) · P(machine_i)."
  answer="P(defect) = 0.02×0.50 + 0.04×0.30 + 0.05×0.20 = 0.01 + 0.012 + 0.01 = 0.032 = 3.2%. The overall defect rate is a weighted average of the individual rates, weighted by production share."

<ExerciseBlock number={5}
  prompt="Events A and B are independent. If P(A) = 0.3 and P(B) = 0.4, find P(A ∩ B), P(A | B), and P(A ∪ B)."
  hint="For independent events, P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B) and P(A | B) = P(A)."
  answer="P(A ∩ B) = 0.3 × 0.4 = 0.12. P(A | B) = P(A) = 0.3 (independence means conditioning doesn't change the probability). P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B) = 0.3 + 0.4 − 0.12 = 0.58."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={6}
  prompt="The 'Monty Hall' setup: 3 doors, one car, two goats. You pick door 1. The host opens door 3 (goat). Using conditional probability, find P(car behind door 2 | host opened door 3) and P(car behind door 1 | host opened door 3)."
  hint="The host knows where the car is and always reveals a goat. His choice is informative. P(host opens 3 | car at door i) differs for each i."
  answer="P(host opens 3 | car at 1) = 1/2 (he picks randomly between doors 2 and 3). P(host opens 3 | car at 2) = 1 (he must open 3). P(host opens 3 | car at 3) = 0 (can't open the car door). Priors: P(car at i) = 1/3 each. P(host opens 3) = (1/2)(1/3) + (1)(1/3) + (0)(1/3) = 1/2. P(car at 2 | opens 3) = (1·1/3)/(1/2) = 2/3. P(car at 1 | opens 3) = (1/2·1/3)/(1/2) = 1/3. Switching wins 2/3 of the time."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={7}
  prompt="Prove that if P(A | B) > P(A), then P(B | A) > P(B). (This says: if B makes A more likely, then A makes B more likely — dependence is symmetric.)"
  hint="Start from P(A | B) > P(A). Write both sides using the definition of conditional probability."
  answer="P(A | B) > P(A) means P(A ∩ B)/P(B) > P(A), so P(A ∩ B) > P(A)·P(B). Now P(B | A) = P(A ∩ B)/P(A) > P(A)·P(B)/P(A) = P(B). So P(B | A) > P(B). The inequality is symmetric: positive correlation goes both ways. This is why 'B causes A' and 'A causes B' are statistically indistinguishable from correlation alone — a core lesson in causal inference."
/>
