You're evaluating a limit and you get $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Both numerator and denominator are collapsing at the same time. Who wins? **Whoever is collapsing faster loses** — and the derivative measures the speed of collapse.

## The rule

If $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ produces $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\pm\infty}{\pm\infty}$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\lim_{x \\to a} \\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \\lim_{x \\to a} \\frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}"} />

provided the right-hand limit exists. You replace the functions with their derivatives and try again.

## Why it works

Near $x = a$, both $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are close to zero. Think of them as lines passing through the origin:

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) \\approx f'(a)(x-a), \\quad g(x) \\approx g'(a)(x-a)"} />

So the ratio simplifies:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \\approx \\frac{f'(a)(x-a)}{g'(a)(x-a)} = \\frac{f'(a)}{g'(a)}"} />

The $(x-a)$ factors cancel. The limit depends on the **slopes**, not the values.

## Three examples

**Example 1**: $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = \frac{0}{0}$. Apply L'Hopital: $\frac{\cos x}{1} \to \frac{1}{1} = 1$.

**Example 2**: $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^x}{x^2} = \frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Apply once: $\frac{e^x}{2x} = \frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Apply again: $\frac{e^x}{2} = \infty$. Exponential growth always wins.

**Example 3**: $\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x = 0 \cdot (-\infty)$. Rewrite as $\frac{\ln x}{1/x}$, then apply L'Hopital: $\frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = -x \to 0$.

## When NOT to use L'Hopital

L'Hopital only works for indeterminate forms ($0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$). If the limit is $\frac{3}{0}$, it's not indeterminate — the limit is $\pm\infty$ (or doesn't exist). Don't differentiate blindly; check the form first.

More subtly: **if the limit exists without L'Hopital, don't use it.** For example, $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x + 1}$ is just $\frac{0}{3} = 0$ — no need for derivatives. Also beware of **circular reasoning**: if you use L'Hopital to evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$ by differentiating to get $\cos x$, you're implicitly assuming you already know the derivative of $\sin x$ — which itself depends on knowing this limit. The logic can eat its own tail.

## A historical note

Guillaume de l'Hopital published this rule in 1696, in what is considered the first textbook on differential calculus, *Analyse des Infiniment Petits pour l'Intelligence des Lignes Courbes*. But the rule was actually discovered by his teacher, **Johann Bernoulli**, as part of a contractual arrangement in which l'Hopital paid Bernoulli for mathematical discoveries and published them under his own name. Bernoulli's priority was only widely recognized in the 20th century.
