## A conveyor belt for functions

Imagine a factory with two machines in a row. The first machine takes raw material `x` and outputs `g(x)`. The second machine takes that output and produces `f(g(x))`. Each machine has its own speed — its own derivative. The chain rule says: **multiply the speeds**.

Think of it this way: $x$ enters the first machine ($g$), gets transformed, comes out as $g(x)$, then enters the second machine ($f$). The overall speed change — how fast the final output responds to a nudge in the input — is the product of each machine's speed change, evaluated at the right point. If the inner machine doubles the speed and the outer machine triples it, the combined effect is a sixfold speedup.

## The formal statement

For a composite function $h(x) = f(g(x))$, the derivative is:

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \\cdot g'(x)"} />

The key insight is that $f'$ is evaluated at $g(x)$, not at $x$. The outer derivative is measured at the output of the inner function.

## Three examples, one pattern

Let's trace the chain rule through concrete examples:

**Example 1: $h(x) = \sin(x^2)$**

The inner function is $g(x) = x^2$ with $g'(x) = 2x$. The outer function is $f(u) = \sin(u)$ with $f'(u) = \cos(u)$. So:

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = \\cos(x^2) \\cdot 2x"} />

**Example 2: <MathInline expression={"h(x) = e^{3x}"} />**

Inner: $g(x) = 3x$, $g'(x) = 3$. Outer: $f(u) = e^u$, $f'(u) = e^u$.

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = e^{3x} \\cdot 3 = 3e^{3x}"} />

**Example 3: $h(x) = (x^2 + 1)^3$**

This is where the chain rule really shines. Expanding this cubic would be painful. Instead: inner $g(x) = x^2 + 1$, $g'(x) = 2x$. Outer $f(u) = u^3$, $f'(u) = 3u^2$.

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = 3(x^2+1)^2 \\cdot 2x = 6x(x^2+1)^2"} />

### Nested chains: peeling the onion

When functions are deeply nested — like <MathInline expression={"h(x) = \\sin(e^{x^2})"} /> — you peel off layers from the outside in. The derivative is a product of one factor per layer:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}\\sin(e^{x^2}) = \\cos(e^{x^2}) \\cdot e^{x^2} \\cdot 2x"} />

Three nested functions, three factors multiplied together. Each factor is the derivative of one layer, evaluated at the output of everything inside it.

<FormulaReadout formulas={[
  { label: "链式法则", expression: "\\frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \\cdot g'(x)" },
  { label: "三层嵌套", expression: "\\frac{d}{dx} f(g(h(x))) = f'(g(h(x))) \\cdot g'(h(x)) \\cdot h'(x)" },
  { label: "Leibniz 记号", expression: "\\frac{dy}{dx} = \\frac{dy}{du} \\cdot \\frac{du}{dx}" },
]} />

## The multivariable preview

The chain rule extends naturally to functions of several variables. If $z = f(x, y)$ where $x = x(t)$ and $y = y(t)$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{dz}{dt} = \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x} \\frac{dx}{dt} + \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial y} \\frac{dy}{dt}"} />

This is the **multivariable chain rule** — a sum of products, one term per intermediate variable. The partial derivatives $\partial f / \partial x$ and $\partial f / \partial y$ are the components of the **gradient** $\nabla f$, and the whole expression is the dot product of the gradient with the velocity $(dx/dt, dy/dt)$. We'll meet this again in lesson 28.

## Why the chain rule is the workhorse

Most functions you'll encounter in science and engineering are compositions. Neural networks are chains of layer functions — backpropagation is just the chain rule applied repeatedly. Physics equations chain coordinate transformations. The chain rule is not just one tool in the toolbox — it's the mechanism that connects all the others.

This is why we can differentiate something like $\sin(x^2)$ without expanding it first. The chain rule decomposes the rate of change into stages: first ask "how fast is $x^2$ changing?" then ask "how fast is $\sin$ changing at that output?" and multiply the answers. Any composition, no matter how deeply nested, yields to the same strategy — peel off one layer at a time.

### Neural networks as function composition

A neural network with $L$ layers is a deeply nested composition:

<MathBlock expression={"\\hat{y} = f_L(f_{L-1}(\\cdots f_2(f_1(x))\\cdots))"} />

Each layer $f_i$ has its own parameters (weights and biases) and its own derivative. **Backpropagation** computes the gradient of the loss with respect to every parameter by applying the chain rule from the output layer back to the input — multiplying derivatives layer by layer. This is why the chain rule is the single most important rule in machine learning: every trained model in the world, from GPT to image generators, runs the chain rule billions of times during training.

<KeyInsight>
The chain rule says: when functions compose, their rates of change multiply. The derivative of a pipeline is the product of the derivatives at each stage. For deeply nested functions, you "peel the onion" from outside in, multiplying one derivative per layer.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

The chain rule is the most consequential rule in calculus for modern applications. In the [previous lesson](/courses/calculus/03-differentiation-rules), we stated it alongside the product and quotient rules; here we saw *why* it works — the "conveyor belt" of function composition. The multivariable version previews the **gradient** and **partial derivatives** of [lesson 28](/courses/calculus/28-multivariable-preview), where a function of several variables changes through multiple input pathways.

The chain rule reappears in two major later contexts. First, **integration by substitution** (lesson 14) is the chain rule run in reverse — if you recognize an integrand as $f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$, you can integrate it as $f(g(x))$. Second, **backpropagation** in neural networks is the chain rule applied to a deep composition of layers; this is the algorithm that makes deep learning trainable at all. The ability to differentiate compositions is what makes the entire pipeline from data → model → loss → gradient → update possible.
