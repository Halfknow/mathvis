Picture a timeline stretching from left to right. Dots appear on it — each dot is an event. They do not come at regular intervals. Sometimes two arrive close together; sometimes there is a long gap. But on average, they arrive at a steady rate. Emails into your inbox. Earthquakes along a fault line. Photons hitting a detector. Radioactive decays in a sample.

All of these are modeled by the same mathematical object: the **Poisson process**. It is the simplest model for events occurring randomly in continuous time, and it captures a surprisingly wide slice of reality.

## The axioms: what makes a process Poisson

A Poisson process with rate $\lambda$ is defined by three axioms. These are not just modeling assumptions — they are the *defining properties* from which everything else follows.

### Theorem: The Poisson process axioms

A counting process <MathInline expression={"\\{N(t) : t \\geq 0\\}"} /> (where $N(t)$ is the number of events in $[0, t]$) is a **Poisson process** with rate $\lambda > 0$ if:

1. **Independent increments**: the numbers of events in disjoint time intervals are independent. What happens in $[0, s]$ has no influence on what happens in $(s, t]$.
2. **Stationary increments**: the distribution of the count in an interval depends only on the interval's length, not its location. Formally, <MathInline expression={"N(t+s) - N(t)"} /> has the same distribution as <MathInline expression={"N(s)"} />.
3. **Rare events (orderliness)**: the probability of two or more events in a tiny interval of length $\Delta t$ is negligible (<MathInline expression={"o(\\Delta t)"} />), while the probability of exactly one event is <MathInline expression={"\\lambda \\Delta t + o(\\Delta t)"} />.

These three axioms — independence, stationarity, and rarity — are the mathematical distillation of "completely random arrivals at a constant rate." From them, two distributions emerge: the **Poisson distribution** for counts, and the **exponential distribution** for waiting times.

## Counting events: the Poisson distribution

Let $N(t)$ be the number of events that occur in a time interval of length $t$. Then:

### Theorem: Poisson counts

$$N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$$

$$P(N(t) = k) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k e^{-\lambda t}}{k!}, \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

with <MathInline expression={"E[N(t)] = \\text{Var}(N(t)) = \\lambda t"} />.

*Sketch.* Chop $[0, t]$ into $n$ tiny intervals of length $t/n$. In each, the probability of one event is approximately $\lambda t/n$ (axiom 3), and two or more is negligible. So <MathInline expression={"N(t)"} /> is approximately <MathInline expression={"\\text{Binomial}(n, \\lambda t/n)"} />. Taking $n \to \infty$ gives the Poisson limit — this is the origin of the Poisson distribution ([lesson 07](/courses/probability/07-the-binomial-distribution)). The mean and variance are both $\lambda t$.

If emails arrive at rate $\lambda = 5$ per hour, then in a 3-hour window the number of emails is Poisson with mean $15$. The most likely count is near 15, but the actual count will fluctuate — occasionally you get 8, occasionally 22.

Picture the distribution as a lump centered at $\lambda t$, with a width (standard deviation) of <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{\\lambda t}"} />. As $\lambda t$ grows, the lump becomes smoother and more symmetric, approximating a bell curve (the normal approximation to Poisson, a special case of the CLT).

## Waiting times: the exponential distribution

Flip the question. Instead of asking "how many events in time $t$?", ask "how long until the next event?" The answer is the other face of the Poisson process.

### Theorem: Exponential inter-arrival times

The waiting time $T$ until the next event in a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$ follows an exponential distribution:

$$T \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda), \qquad f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0$$

with <MathInline expression={"E[T] = 1/\\lambda"} /> and <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(T) = 1/\\lambda^2"} />.

*Proof of the duality.* <MathInline expression={"P(T > t) = P(N(t) = 0) = e^{-\\lambda t}"} /> — the waiting time exceeds $t$ exactly when no events arrive in $[0, t]$. Differentiating gives the density <MathInline expression={"f(t) = \\lambda e^{-\\lambda t}"} />. This is the deep connection: the Poisson count distribution and the exponential waiting time distribution are two views of the same process.

If emails arrive at rate 5 per hour, the average gap between emails is 12 minutes ($1/\lambda = 1/5$ hour). But the distribution is heavily skewed — short gaps are common, long gaps are rare but possible.

Picture the density: a steep drop from height $\lambda$ at $t = 0$, decaying exponentially. Most of the mass is packed near zero. The probability of waiting more than $1/\lambda$ is <MathInline expression={"e^{-1} \\approx 0.37"} />. The probability of waiting more than $3/\lambda$ is <MathInline expression={"e^{-3} \\approx 0.05"} />. Long waits are exponentially unlikely.

### Inter-arrival times are i.i.d.

The gaps between consecutive events — call them $T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots$ — are independent and identically distributed exponential random variables, all with rate $\lambda$. This is what makes the Poisson process so tractable: the gaps are a sequence of independent draws, each from the same exponential distribution, with no memory of the previous gap.

The timeline of events looks like a series of dots with irregular spacing — sometimes bunched, sometimes spread out — but each gap is drawn fresh from the same exponential distribution.

## The memoryless property

The exponential distribution has a property that feels wrong at first:

### Theorem: Memorylessness of the exponential

For <MathInline expression={"T \\sim \\text{Exponential}(\\lambda)"} /> and any $s, t \geq 0$:

$$P(T > s + t \mid T > s) = P(T > t) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

If you have already waited $s$ minutes for an event, the probability of waiting at least $t$ *more* minutes is the same as if you had not waited at all. The distribution does not remember how long you have been waiting.

*Proof.* <MathInline expression={"P(T > s+t \\mid T > s) = P(T > s+t, T > s)/P(T > s) = P(T > s+t)/P(T > s) = e^{-\\lambda(s+t)}/e^{-\\lambda s} = e^{-\\lambda t} = P(T > t)"} />.

The exponential is the *only* continuous distribution with this property. (Among discrete distributions, only the geometric shares it.) Memorylessness is both a mathematical convenience and a genuine modeling assumption.

- For **radioactive decay**, it is physically accurate: an atom that has existed for a billion years is no more or less likely to decay in the next second than a freshly created atom. The decay process has no memory.
- For **server failures** (in the constant-hazard regime), it is roughly accurate: a well-maintained server fails at a roughly constant rate, regardless of age.
- For **bus arrivals**, it is often wrong: if the bus is late, it is probably coming soon (the schedule introduces memory). Use it only when arrivals are genuinely memoryless.

## Superposition and thinning

Two elegant operations on Poisson processes preserve the Poisson structure. Both follow from the independence axiom.

### Theorem: Superposition

If <MathInline expression={"\\{N_1(t)\\}"} /> and <MathInline expression={"\\{N_2(t)\\}"} /> are independent Poisson processes with rates $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, then the combined process <MathInline expression={"\\{N_1(t) + N_2(t)\\}"} /> is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$.

*Picture.* Two timelines, one blue (emails) and one red (texts). Merge them onto a single timeline. The result is another Poisson process — same structure, faster rate. The sum of two independent Poisson random variables is Poisson with the sum of the rates. This is why a customer service center receiving calls from two independent sources can model the total call stream as a single Poisson process.

### Theorem: Thinning (coloring)

If each event of a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$ is independently classified as type $i$ with probability $p_i$ (where $\sum p_i = 1$), then the type-$i$ events form a Poisson process with rate $\lambda p_i$, and these processes are independent of each other.

*Picture.* Start with a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$. For each event, flip a biased coin: keep it with probability $p$, discard it with probability $1-p$. The kept events form a Poisson process with rate $\lambda p$. This is useful for modeling filtered streams. If customers arrive at a store at rate $\lambda$ and each independently makes a purchase with probability $p$, then purchases arrive as a Poisson process of rate $\lambda p$. The thinning preserves the Poisson structure because of independence.

*Application: the M/M/1 queue.* Customers arrive as a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$, service times are exponential with rate $\mu$. The number of customers in the system is a birth-death process (a continuous-time Markov chain) with stationary distribution <MathInline expression={"\\pi_n = (1 - \\rho)\\rho^n"} /> where <MathInline expression={"\\rho = \\lambda/\\mu < 1"} />. This is the foundation of queueing theory — and it works precisely because both arrivals and services are memoryless.

## The full timeline picture

Imagine drawing a horizontal line — the time axis. Events appear as dots. The dots are irregularly spaced, with gaps drawn from Exponential($\lambda$). Sometimes two dots land close together (the exponential distribution allows this). Sometimes there is a long empty stretch. But the average density of dots is $\lambda$ per unit time.

If you zoom in on any small window, the dots look the same — same average density, same randomness. If you zoom out and count dots in a large window, the count is Poisson with mean proportional to the window length.

This **scale-invariant randomness** is the hallmark of the Poisson process. No preferred timescale. No periodicity. No clustering beyond what pure chance produces. It is the maximally random point process on the line — which is why it appears as the limit whenever many independent rare events combine.

## Three perspectives on the Poisson process

1. **As a counting process.** $N(t)$ counts events in $[0, t]$. The Poisson distribution gives the count; the exponential distribution gives the gaps. The two are dual: $P(T > t) = P(N(t) = 0)$. This is the "events in time" view, natural for modeling arrivals, decays, and discoveries.
2. **As a continuous-time Markov chain.** The Poisson process is the simplest continuous-time Markov chain: the state is the current count, and transitions go from $n$ to $n+1$ at rate $\lambda$. The memorylessness of the exponential inter-arrival times IS the Markov property in continuous time. This view generalizes to birth-death processes, queueing models, and continuous-time Markov chains broadly. See [lesson 25](/courses/probability/25-markov-chains).
3. **As the law of rare events.** The Poisson process is the limit of many independent rare Bernoulli trials. Chop time into $n$ tiny bins, put one event in each with probability $\lambda/n$, and as $n \to \infty$ you get the Poisson process. This "law of rare events" explains why the Poisson distribution appears whenever many independent low-probability events combine — radioactive decays (each atom rarely decays), accidents, rare diseases, website hits (each of millions of users rarely visits).

## Modern applications

**Queueing theory and systems design.** Every customer service center, server farm, and cloud computing system is analyzed with queueing models built on Poisson processes. The M/M/1 queue (Poisson arrivals, exponential service, one server) gives closed-form results for average wait time, queue length, and utilization. The memoryless property of exponential service times is what makes these models tractable. When you wait on hold, the math of this lesson is governing your experience.

**Network traffic and reliability.** Packet arrivals on a network link, failures of redundant systems, and session arrivals at a web server are routinely modeled as Poisson processes. The superposition property (many independent arrival streams combine into one Poisson process) justifies treating aggregate web traffic as Poisson even when individual users arrive idiosyncratically. Reliability engineering uses the exponential failure model (constant hazard rate) as the baseline — the "useful life" period before wear-out begins.

**Event-driven simulation and computational finance.** Discrete-event simulators (for manufacturing, logistics, communication networks) are built around Poisson process generators. In finance, the jump-diffusion model adds Poisson-driven jumps to geometric Brownian motion, capturing the sudden crashes and spikes that pure Brownian motion misses. The Poisson component models rare, discrete events (earnings surprises, flash crashes) layered on top of the continuous noise.

**Neural spiking and biology.** Many neurons in sensory systems fire approximately as Poisson processes, with a rate $\lambda$ that depends on the stimulus. The inter-spike intervals are approximately exponential, and the spike counts in fixed windows are approximately Poisson. This is the baseline model in computational neuroscience; deviations from Poisson firing (more regular or more bursty) carry information about the underlying neural code.

<KeyInsight>
A Poisson process is the purest model of random events in time, defined by three axioms: independent increments, stationary increments, and rare (orderly) events. From these axioms flow two dual distributions — the Poisson (for counts in a window) and the exponential (for gaps between events). The exponential's memoryless property (having waited s minutes does not change the distribution of additional waiting time) is the continuous-time analog of the Markov property. Superposition (merge two Poisson streams to get another) and thinning (filter a Poisson stream to get another) preserve the structure, making the Poisson process the universal building block of queueing theory, network modeling, reliability engineering, and computational neuroscience.
</KeyInsight>

<ExerciseBlock number={7}
  prompt="Explain why the Poisson process is the 'law of rare events' — the limit of many independent Bernoulli trials with small probability. Give the derivation showing how a Binomial(n, lambda/n) converges to Poisson(lambda) as n -> infinity, and explain why this justifies using the Poisson process to model phenomena like radioactive decay or website visits."
  hint="Take n Bernoulli trials each with prob p = lambda/n. Show the Binomial PMF converges to the Poisson PMF. Use (1 - lambda/n)^n -> e^(-lambda)."
  answer="Chop a time interval [0, t] into n tiny sub-intervals of length t/n. In each sub-interval, assume at most one event can occur (the 'rare events' axiom), with probability p = lambda*t/n (so the expected count over n sub-intervals is n*p = lambda*t, constant). The total count N is Binomial(n, p) = Binomial(n, lambda*t/n). As n -> infinity: P(N=k) = C(n,k) p^k (1-p)^(n-k). For fixed k, as n -> infinity: C(n,k) ~ n^k/k!, p^k = (lambda*t/n)^k, so C(n,k)*p^k -> (lambda*t)^k/k!. Why this justifies the Poisson model: radioactive decay is the sum of many independent atoms each rarely decaying (n ~ 10^23 atoms, each with tiny decay probability per second) — exactly the rare-events limit. Website visits are the sum of many independent users each rarely visiting (n ~ millions of users) — again the rare-events limit. Whenever a phenomenon is the aggregate of many independent low-probability events, the count is approximately Poisson. This is why the Poisson distribution appears so ubiquitously in nature and engineering: it is the universal limit of rare-event aggregation, just as the normal distribution is the universal limit of additive noise aggregation (CLT)."
