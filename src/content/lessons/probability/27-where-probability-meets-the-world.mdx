## Information theory: the bridge to machine learning

Claude Shannon built information theory on one idea: **information is the resolution of uncertainty**. The information content of an event with probability $p$ is $-\log_2 p$. Rare events carry more information — they are more surprising. The average information content of a distribution is its **entropy**:

### Theorem: Shannon entropy

For a discrete random variable $X$ with PMF $p(x)$, the **entropy** is:

$$H(X) = -\sum_x p(x) \log_2 p(x)$$

Entropy is **expected surprise**, measured in bits. A uniform distribution (maximum uncertainty) has maximum entropy. A degenerate distribution (all mass on one outcome) has zero entropy — no surprise, no information.

Entropy is the bridge between probability and machine learning because every learning algorithm is, at its core, trying to reduce uncertainty about the world. The training loss of a classifier (cross-entropy) is a direct measure of how much surprise remains in the true labels given the model's predictions. Compression (the Shannon source coding theorem) and learning (reducing predictive uncertainty) are two sides of the same coin: both are about representing a distribution efficiently.

### KL divergence: measuring distributional distance

The **Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence** measures how one probability distribution $q$ differs from a reference distribution $p$:

### Theorem: KL divergence

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$$

KL divergence is always non-negative, and equals zero if and only if $p = q$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) \geq 0, \qquad D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = 0 \iff p = q$$

This is **Gibbs' inequality**. KL divergence is not symmetric (<MathInline expression={"D_{\\text{KL}}(p \\| q) \\neq D_{\\text{KL}}(q \\| p)"} /> in general) and does not satisfy the triangle inequality, so it is not a true metric — but it is the central measure of distributional difference in machine learning.

- **Variational inference** ([lesson 23](/courses/probability/23-bayesian-inference-in-practice)) minimizes <MathInline expression={"D_{\\text{KL}}(q \\| p)"} /> to approximate a posterior.
- **Model training** minimizes the KL divergence between the model's predicted distribution and the true data distribution — equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy.
- **Variational autoencoders** use the ELBO, which decomposes as (data fit) minus (KL divergence from the prior).

### Mutual information: the information between variables

The **mutual information** between two random variables $X$ and $Y$ measures how much knowing one reduces uncertainty about the other:

$$I(X; Y) = H(X) - H(X \mid Y) = D_{\text{KL}}(p(x,y) \| p(x)\,p(y))$$

Mutual information is zero if and only if $X$ and $Y$ are independent — it is the definitive measure of statistical dependence, capturing nonlinear relationships that correlation misses. It underpins feature selection, the information bottleneck method, and the theory of communication channels.

## The maximum entropy principle

There is a deep principle that connects probability to physics and to machine learning: when you must choose a distribution subject to known constraints (a given mean, a given variance), the principled choice is the one with **maximum entropy** — the distribution that assumes the least beyond what you know.

### Theorem: Maximum entropy principle

Among all distributions satisfying given constraints (e.g., specified mean and variance), the distribution that maximizes entropy <MathInline expression={"H(X)"} /> is the one that is "maximally uncommitted" given those constraints. Maximum entropy distributions include:

- **No constraints** on $[a, b]$: the **Uniform** distribution.
- **Specified mean** on $[0, \infty)$: the **Exponential** distribution.
- **Specified mean and variance** on $(-\infty, \infty)$: the **Normal** distribution.
- **Specified mean** on counting measure: the **Poisson** distribution.

This is profound. The Normal distribution is not just "common" — it is the **maximum-entropy distribution for a given mean and variance**. When you assume a measurement is Normal, you are implicitly saying "I know the mean and variance, and I refuse to assume anything else." This is why the Normal is the default in statistics: it is the least-assuming choice. The same logic explains why the Exponential is the default for waiting times (it is max-ent given a mean) and why the Poisson process is the default point process (it is max-ent given a rate). The entire catalog of named distributions is a catalog of maximum-entropy solutions to constraint sets.

## How probability connects to physics

Statistical mechanics is probability applied to enormous collections of particles. Temperature is average kinetic energy. Entropy (the physical quantity) is the logarithm of the number of microstates consistent with a given macrostate. The **Boltzmann distribution** gives the probability of a particle having energy $E$ at temperature $T$:

<MathInline expression={"P(E) \\propto e^{-E / (k_B T)}"} />

The exponential form is familiar — it is the same shape as the Poisson process's waiting time distribution. This is not a coincidence. The Boltzmann distribution is the **maximum-entropy distribution** subject to a fixed average energy, just as the exponential is the max-ent distribution with a fixed mean. The math of thermal equilibrium and the math of random arrivals are the same math.

The second law of thermodynamics (entropy increases) is a theorem about probability: in an isolated system, the system evolves toward macrostates with more microstates (higher probability), because there are overwhelmingly more of them. This is the [Law of Large Numbers](/courses/probability/14-law-of-large-numbers) at the scale of <MathInline expression={"10^{23}"} /> particles — fluctuations away from the maximum-entropy state are exponentially unlikely.

Quantum mechanics goes further. The outcomes of quantum measurements are fundamentally probabilistic. A quantum state does not determine what you will observe — it determines the *probabilities* of what you will observe. The wave function is a probability amplitude, and its squared magnitude gives the probability density. At the deepest level, nature does not deal in certainties.

## How probability connects to finance

The Black-Scholes model for pricing options treats stock prices as a **geometric Brownian motion** — a continuous-time random walk on a logarithmic scale. The model's core assumption is that log-returns are independent and normally distributed with variance proportional to the time horizon (the <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{t}"} /> law from [lesson 24](/courses/probability/24-random-walks)). These are the same assumptions behind the random walk and the Central Limit Theorem.

Every option price, every risk calculation, every portfolio optimization in modern finance rests on probabilistic foundations. Value at Risk (VaR) is a quantile of a loss distribution. The efficient frontier of portfolio theory is built from the means and covariances of asset returns. The [capital asset pricing model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_asset_pricing_model) relates expected return to systematic risk (<MathInline expression={"\\beta"} />). Probability is the language of financial risk — and when the models fail (the 2008 crisis, the Gaussian copula), it is usually because the probabilistic assumptions (independent normal returns) broke down in the tails.

## How probability connects to machine learning

At its core, machine learning is probability in disguise. A classifier maps inputs to a distribution over labels. A regression model outputs a distribution over possible values. A language model assigns probabilities to sequences of words.

The **softmax** function at the output of a neural network converts raw scores into a probability distribution:

<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{class } i) = \\frac{e^{z_i}}{\\sum_j e^{z_j}}}"} />

Training minimizes the **cross-entropy loss** — the gap between the model's predicted distribution and the true distribution of the data. Cross-entropy loss is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence between the empirical data distribution and the model distribution, which is equivalent to maximum likelihood estimation. The entire framework of maximum likelihood, encountered in [lesson 17](/courses/probability/17-sampling-distributions), is the standard training objective.

### Bayesian deep learning and variational autoencoders

Bayesian methods extend this further: instead of a single best model, they maintain a **distribution over models**, updating the distribution as data arrives. The prior encodes what you believed before; the posterior encodes what you believe after. The same Bayes' theorem from Module 1, scaled up to millions of parameters.

The two computational engines that make this tractable were developed in [lesson 23](/courses/probability/23-bayesian-inference-in-practice):

- **MCMC (Markov Chain Monte Carlo)**: construct a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the posterior ([lesson 25](/courses/probability/25-markov-chains)), run it, collect samples. Powers Stan, PyMC, NumPyro.
- **Variational inference**: approximate the posterior with a tractable distribution, fit by maximizing the ELBO. Scales to deep neural networks.

**Variational autoencoders (VAEs)** combine all three threads — deep learning, variational inference, and information theory. The encoder outputs parameters of an approximate posterior $q(z \mid x)$ over a latent code $z$; training maximizes the ELBO, which decomposes as:

<MathInline expression={"\\text{ELBO} = \\underbrace{E_{q}[\\log p(x \\mid z)]}_{\\text{reconstruction}} - \\underbrace{D_{\\text{KL}}(q(z \\mid x) \\| p(z))}_{\\text{regularization}}"} />

The first term rewards reconstruction (the data likelihood); the second term penalizes the approximate posterior for straying from the prior (a complexity penalty, measured by KL divergence). This single equation unifies Bayesian inference, information theory, and neural networks. Modern generative AI is built on it.

## Three perspectives on the whole course

1. **As the mathematics of uncertainty.** Probability is the calculus of reasoning under uncertainty. Every concept — from the axioms of probability to MCMC — is a tool for making uncertainty precise, manipulable, and ultimately useful. This is why probability appears in every quantitative field: uncertainty is universal, and probability is its language.
2. **As a bridge between pure and applied mathematics.** Probability theory is built on measure theory (pure math), but its tools (expectation, conditioning, convergence, distributions) are the daily vocabulary of applied statistics, machine learning, physics, and finance. No other branch of mathematics sits so centrally between theory and practice. The same theorem (the CLT) that is a deep result in analysis is also the reason sample means are reliable and bell curves are everywhere.
3. **As the foundation of modern AI.** Every modern AI system is, at some level, a probabilistic model. Language models assign probabilities to token sequences. Diffusion models are random walks in pixel space ([lesson 24](/courses/probability/24-random-walks)). Reinforcement learning optimizes expectations over Markov decision processes ([lesson 25](/courses/probability/25-markov-chains)). Generative models minimize KL divergence. The course you just completed is the mathematical foundation of artificial intelligence.

## Everyday life

You use probability more than you realize:

- **Medical decisions**: a positive test result triggers Bayesian reasoning — how likely is the disease given this evidence, given the base rate, given the test accuracy? ([Lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-bayes-theorem).)
- **Weather forecasts**: "70% chance of rain" means that in all historically similar atmospheric conditions, it rained about 70% of the time. The forecast is a calibrated probability.
- **Spam filters**: naive Bayes classifiers compute <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{spam} \\mid \\text{words in email})"} /> using Bayes' theorem, assuming word occurrences are independent.
- **Insurance premiums**: actuaries estimate the probability distribution of claims and set prices so that expected payouts are covered by expected premiums. The entire business model is a bet on the Law of Large Numbers.
