# Matrix Multiplication

If a matrix is a transformation of space, then **multiplying two matrices** means doing one transformation after another. It's composition — apply the first warp, then apply the second warp to the already-warped space.

This is why matrix multiplication exists. It's not arbitrary arithmetic — it's the algebra of sequential transformations.

## The composition picture

Suppose A rotates by 90° and B stretches by 2x. Then BA (read right to left: A first, then B) means: rotate the space, then stretch it. The combined effect is a different single transformation — and it's represented by a single matrix, the product BA.

<MathBlock expression={'(BA)\\vec{x} = B(A\\vec{x})'} />

Read this right to left: x goes into A, the result goes into B. The matrix BA is the "shortcut" — it encodes the entire two-step transformation in one step.

## How to compute it

The (i,j) entry of the product AB is the dot product of row i of A with column j of B:

<MathBlock expression={'(AB)_{ij} = \\text{row}_i(A) \\cdot \\text{col}_j(B) = \\sum_k a_{ik} \\, b_{kj}'} />

But the geometric meaning is more useful: **column j of AB is A times column j of B**. In other words, apply transformation A to each column of B. The columns of the product are the transformed basis vectors.

## Order matters

Matrix multiplication is **not commutative**: AB ≠ BA in general.

Geometrically this makes perfect sense. Rotate then stretch is different from stretch then rotate. The order of transformations matters — try putting on your socks after your shoes and you'll feel the principle directly.

## The identity matrix

The **identity matrix** I is the "do nothing" transformation:

<MathBlock expression={'I = \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\\\ 0 & 1 \\end{bmatrix}'} />

Multiplying by I changes nothing: IA = AI = A for any matrix A. Every basis vector lands exactly where it started. The identity is to matrix multiplication what zero is to addition and one is to multiplication — the neutral element.

## Associativity: (AB)C = A(BC)

Matrix multiplication is **associative**: the grouping doesn't matter. (AB)C = A(BC) — apply A and B first, then C, or apply A to the result of BC. Either way, you get the same transformation.

The geometric proof is immediate: both expressions mean "apply C, then B, then A." The order of *transformations* is fixed (C first, then B, then A). Parentheses just determine whether you compose the first two or the last two first — but the total composition is the same.

This is why we can write ABC without ambiguity. Three transformations in sequence have one unambiguous combined effect.

## Preview: four perspectives

You've seen matrix multiplication as composition. In lesson 17, you'll see the *same product* from four different angles: entry-by-entry dot products, column combinations, row combinations, and outer product sums. Each perspective reveals a different structure. For now, notice one fact that will unlock everything: **column j of AB = A times column j of B**. The columns of the product are transformed versions of B's columns.

## Connections

Matrix multiplication is the engine of composition. Lesson 17 will show you four perspectives on this same operation, each revealing different structure — the column perspective connects to linear systems, the outer product perspective connects to SVD.

The identity matrix I and associativity ((AB)C = A(BC)) are the algebraic backbone of everything from the determinant product rule (lesson 15) to the eigenvalue equation A(Av) = A(λv) = λ(Av) in lesson 25.
