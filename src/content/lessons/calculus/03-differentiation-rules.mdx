Computing every derivative from the limit definition is tedious. Fortunately, a small set of rules lets us differentiate almost any function you'll encounter. Think of these rules as the alphabet of calculus — every derivative you'll ever compute, no matter how complicated the function looks, is built by combining these six rules in the right order.

For $f(x) = x^2$, notice that $f'(x) = 2x$ is a straight line through the origin — the slope increases linearly. For $f(x) = \sin(x)$, the derivative is $\cos(x)$ — the slope leads the function by a quarter cycle.

## The power rule

If $f(x) = x^n$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"f'(x) = n \\cdot x^{n-1}"} />

This works for any real exponent — positive, negative, or fractional. The power rule is the workhorse of differentiation.

**Examples:**
- $(x^3)' = 3x^2$
- <MathInline expression={"(x^{-2})' = -2x^{-3}"} />
- <MathInline expression={"(\\sqrt{x})' = (x^{1/2})' = \\frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}"} />

The geometric intuition: $x^n$ curves upward (for $n > 1$), and the power rule says the slope at height $x^n$ is proportional to <MathInline expression={"x^{n-1}"} /> — exactly one power lower. The derivative "shifts the exponent down by one and brings it out front as a multiplier."

## The sum and constant rules

<MathBlock expression={"\frac{d}{dx}[c\,f(x)] = c\,f\'(x)"} />
<MathBlock expression={"\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f\'(x) + g\'(x)"} />

Constants multiply through, and derivatives distribute over addition. These two rules mean you can differentiate any polynomial term-by-term.

The sum rule is deeper than it looks: it says **differentiation is a linear operator**. This is the same linearity property that defines matrix-vector multiplication in linear algebra — $T(f + g) = T(f) + T(g)$ and $T(cf) = c\,T(f)$. In fact, the derivative *is* a linear transformation on the vector space of differentiable functions.

## The product rule

<MathBlock expression={"[f(x) \\cdot g(x)]' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)"} />

"First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first." This is essential whenever two functions are multiplied together — like $x^2 \sin(x)$.

### The geometric picture

There's a geometric picture that makes this formula feel natural. Imagine a rectangle whose sides have lengths $u(x)$ and $v(x)$. The area is $uv$. Now nudge each side by a tiny amount: $u$ grows by $du$ and $v$ grows by $dv$. The new area is $(u + du)(v + dv) = uv + u\,dv + v\,du + du\,dv$. The change in area consists of two thin strips ($u\,dv$ and $v\,du$) plus a tiny corner square ($du\,dv$) that vanishes as the nudges shrink to zero. What's left is exactly the product rule: $d(uv) = u\,dv + v\,du$.

## The chain rule

<MathBlock expression={"[f(g(x))]' = f'(g(x)) \\cdot g'(x)"} />

The chain rule handles **composed functions** — functions nested inside other functions. You differentiate the outer function, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function. We'll explore this visually in the [next lesson](/courses/calculus/04-visualizing-the-chain-rule).

The chain rule is the most important rule for machine learning — it is the mathematical engine of **backpropagation**, the algorithm that trains neural networks. Every layer of a neural network is a function composition; backpropagation applies the chain rule repeatedly to compute how each parameter affects the final loss.

## The quotient rule

<MathBlock expression={"\\left[\\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\\right]' = \\frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}"} />

While useful, the quotient rule is just the product rule applied to <MathInline expression={"f(x) \\cdot [g(x)]^{-1}"} /> combined with the chain rule. Many people prefer to rewrite quotients as products to avoid memorizing this formula.

A mnemonic that works: **"Low D-High minus High D-Low, over Low squared"** — where D-High means derivative of the numerator.

## Implicit differentiation

Sometimes a function is defined implicitly — not as $y = f(x)$ but as a relation like $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ (the unit circle). You can still find $dy/dx$ without solving for $y$ explicitly. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ and applying the chain rule:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 + y^2] = \\frac{d}{dx}[1] \\implies 2x + 2y \\cdot \\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \\implies \\frac{dy}{dx} = -\\frac{x}{y}"} />

Implicit differentiation is how you find slopes on curves that aren't functions (like circles, ellipses, and implicitly defined surfaces). It's also the foundation of **Lagrange multipliers** in multivariable optimization.

## The six rules in one table

| Rule | Formula | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Power | <MathInline expression={"(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}"} /> | Polynomials, roots, negative powers |
| Constant multiple | $(cf)' = cf'$ | Scaling a function |
| Sum | $(f + g)' = f' + g'$ | Adding functions |
| Product | $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$ | Multiplying functions |
| Quotient | $(f/g)' = (f'g - fg')/g^2$ | Dividing functions |
| Chain | $(f \circ g)' = (f' \circ g) \cdot g'$ | Composing functions |

<KeyInsight>
The six rules — power, constant multiple, sum, product, quotient, and chain — compose like LEGO blocks. Any function built from these operations can be differentiated mechanically by applying the right rule at each step. The derivative is a linear operator (sum and constant rules), and the chain rule is the engine that connects them through composition.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

The differentiation rules turn the limit definition from [lesson 01](/courses/calculus/01-what-is-a-derivative) into practical, mechanical tools. The chain rule is explored visually in [lesson 04](/courses/calculus/04-visualizing-the-chain-rule), and it becomes the backbone of **backpropagation** in neural networks — each layer composes with the next, and the chain rule propagates gradients backward through the entire network.

The sum rule reveals that differentiation is a **linear transformation** — a direct bridge to linear algebra. The product rule generalizes to the **Leibniz rule** for higher derivatives, and the implicit differentiation technique connects forward to **partial derivatives** and **gradient vectors** in multivariable calculus. These rules also reappear in reverse during integration (lessons 14–16): integration by substitution is the chain rule "undone," and integration by parts is the product rule "undone."
