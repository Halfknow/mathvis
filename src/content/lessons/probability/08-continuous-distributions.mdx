With a continuous random variable, asking for the probability of a single exact value is meaningless. <MathInline expression={"P(X = \\text{exactly } 3.14159) = 0"} />. There are infinitely many possible values, and each individual one gets zero weight. Probability doesn't live at points — it lives in **intervals**.

The right picture is a landscape. Imagine a smooth hill plotted over the number line. The height of the hill at each point tells you where values concentrate — the hill is tall where outcomes are likely, and short where they are rare. The probability of landing between two markers is the **area under the curve** between those markers. Not the height — the area. This single shift, from counting bars to measuring area, is what makes continuous distributions feel different from their discrete cousins.

## From bars to a smooth curve

Start with a histogram. You have bins along the x-axis, and each bin has a bar whose height represents the proportion of data that landed there. Now shrink the bins. Make them half as wide. Then half again. The bar chart gets finer and finer, stepping closer to a smooth shape.

When the bins become infinitesimally narrow, the staircase of bars becomes a smooth curve $f(x)$. This is the **probability density function** (PDF).

Here is the crucial point: $f(x)$ is **not** a probability. It is a density. Just as physical density (mass per unit length) can exceed 1, the value of $f(x)$ can be greater than 1. The density can be tall and narrow or short and wide — what matters is that the **total area under the curve is exactly 1**.

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

The integral is the area. Shaded regions of the landscape are probabilities.

Two conditions define a valid PDF:

- $f(x) \geq 0$ everywhere (the landscape never dips below ground)
- <MathInline expression={"\\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f(x)\\,dx = 1"} /> (the total area is exactly one)

### Theorem: The PDF-CDF relationship

For a continuous random variable with PDF $f$ and CDF $F$:

$<MathInline expression={"F(x) = P(X \\leq x) = \\int_{-\\infty}^{x} f(t)\\,dt, \\qquad f(x) = F'(x)"} />$

The CDF is the accumulated area under the PDF; the PDF is the slope (derivative) of the CDF. They are two views of the same object: the PDF is the landscape (hills and valleys), the CDF is the odometer (always climbing from 0 to 1).

A useful consequence: probabilities are differences of CDF values.

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)$$

You read the odometer at $b$, read it at $a$, and subtract. The area between the two markers is the difference in accumulated totals. This is the continuous analog of summing PMF values over a range — except it works even when there are infinitely many values in between.

## Uniform(a, b) — the flat landscape

The simplest continuous distribution is the **uniform distribution**. Imagine a spinner on a dial marked from $a$ to $b$. Where it lands is completely equally likely across the whole range.

The density is a rectangle:

$<MathInline expression={"f(x) = \\frac{1}{b - a}, \\quad a \\leq x \\leq b"} />$

The height is $1/(b - a)$ because the rectangle must have area 1. A wider interval means a shorter rectangle. A narrow interval means a tall, skinny one. The geometry forces the trade-off.

The mean sits exactly in the center:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X] = \\frac{a + b}{2}"} />$

The variance grows with the square of the width:

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X) = \\frac{(b - a)^2}{12}"} />$

Why the 12? It comes from the integral of <MathInline expression={"(x - \\text{midpoint})^2"} /> across a flat strip. The geometric intuition: spread the mass uniformly over a wider range and the squared distances from the center grow fast.

**Example**: A random number generator produces values uniformly between 0 and 1. The density is a flat line at height 1 over $[0, 1]$. The mean is $0.5$, the variance is $1/12 \approx 0.083$. Every sub-interval of length 0.2 has probability exactly 0.2. The CDF is a straight ramp $F(x) = x$ for $x \in [0,1]$ — slope 1, because the density is flat at height 1.

## Exponential($\lambda$) — the waiting-time landscape

The **exponential distribution** models waiting times. If events arrive at rate $\lambda$ (think: buses at a stop, customers at a counter, radioactive decays), the time until the next arrival follows an exponential distribution.

The density drops off sharply:

$<MathInline expression={"f(x) = \\lambda e^{-\\lambda x}, \\quad x \\geq 0"} />$

Picture a cliff at the origin that plunges rightward. The rate $\lambda$ controls how steep the drop is. Large $\lambda$ means events arrive fast, so short waits are very likely and the cliff is very steep. Small $\lambda$ means long waits are common and the cliff is gentler.

The mean and variance are:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X] = \\frac{1}{\\lambda}, \\quad \\text{Var}(X) = \\frac{1}{\\lambda^2}"} />$

The mean waiting time is $1/\lambda$. If buses arrive at rate 4 per hour ($\lambda = 4$), the average wait is 15 minutes. The standard deviation is also $1/\lambda = 15$ minutes — the exponential distribution's spread equals its mean. The CDF is <MathInline expression={"F(x) = 1 - e^{-\\lambda x}"} />, rising steeply near zero and asymptoting toward 1.

### Theorem: The memoryless property

The exponential distribution is **memoryless**: for any $s, t \geq 0$,

$$P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$$

*Proof.* By the definition of conditional probability, <MathInline expression={"P(X > s + t \\mid X > s) = \\frac{P(X > s + t \\text{ and } X > s)}{P(X > s)} = \\frac{P(X > s + t)}{P(X > s)}"} />. Using the survival function <MathInline expression={"P(X > x) = e^{-\\lambda x}"} />: this equals <MathInline expression={"\\frac{e^{-\\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\\lambda s}} = e^{-\\lambda t} = P(X > t)"} />.

The intuition is striking. If you've been waiting 10 minutes for a bus and no bus has come, the probability of waiting at least 5 *more* minutes is the *same* as the probability of waiting at least 5 minutes from the start. The distribution doesn't age. The landscape ahead of you looks identical no matter where you stand on it — as long as you haven't seen the event yet.

The exponential is the **only** continuous distribution with this property (just as the geometric is the only discrete one). This makes it the natural model for "time until the next event" in memoryless processes — radioactive decay, lightbulb lifetimes under constant hazard, inter-arrival times in a Poisson process. But it is also a warning: if your system *does* age (mechanical parts that wear out, humans whose mortality increases), the exponential is the wrong model and you need something like the Weibull distribution instead.

## The Normal distribution — a preview

The most important continuous distribution is the **normal** (Gaussian), whose density is the bell curve:

$<MathInline expression={"f(x) = \\frac{1}{\\sigma\\sqrt{2\\pi}} \\exp\\!\\left(-\\frac{(x - \\mu)^2}{2\\sigma^2}\\right)"} />$

It has two parameters: the mean $\mu$ (where the peak sits) and the standard deviation $\sigma$ (how wide the bell spreads). The normal is so central to statistics that [lesson 09](/courses/probability/09-the-normal-distribution) is devoted to it entirely. For now, note that it is the limit shape that sums of random variables converge to (the central limit theorem, lesson 16), which is why it appears in heights, measurement errors, test scores, and stock returns.

## How to compute $P(a < X < b)$

For any continuous distribution, the probability of landing in an interval is the area under the PDF between the endpoints. There are two equivalent ways to compute it:

1. **Integrate the PDF:** $P(a < X < b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$. This is direct area measurement — literally computing the shaded region.
2. **Subtract CDF values:** $P(a < X < b) = F(b) - F(a)$. This reads the odometer at both ends and subtracts. Often much faster, because CDFs of named distributions have closed forms.

**Worked example (uniform).** For <MathInline expression={"X \\sim \\text{Uniform}(0, 1)"} />, find $P(0.3 < X < 0.7)$. Integrating the PDF: <MathInline expression={"\\int_{0.3}^{0.7} 1\\,dx = 0.4"} />. Via CDF: $F(0.7) - F(0.3) = 0.7 - 0.3 = 0.4$. Same answer — the geometry of a rectangle makes both methods trivial.

**Worked example (exponential).** For <MathInline expression={"X \\sim \\text{Exponential}(2)"} />, find $P(X > 1)$. Via survival function: <MathInline expression={"P(X > 1) = e^{-2 \\cdot 1} = e^{-2} \\approx 0.135"} />. Via CDF: <MathInline expression={"1 - F(1) = 1 - (1 - e^{-2}) = e^{-2} \\approx 0.135"} />. About 13.5% of the time you wait longer than 1 unit.

## The family summary table

| Distribution | PDF | CDF | Mean | Variance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Uniform($a$, $b$)** | $1/(b-a)$ on $[a,b]$ | $(x-a)/(b-a)$ | $(a+b)/2$ | $(b-a)^2/12$ |
| **Exponential($\lambda$)** | <MathInline expression={"\\lambda e^{-\\lambda x}"} />, $x \geq 0$ | <MathInline expression={"1 - e^{-\\lambda x}"} /> | $1/\lambda$ | $1/\lambda^2$ |
| **Normal($\mu$, $\sigma^2$)** | <MathInline expression={"\\frac{1}{\\sigma\\sqrt{2\\pi}} e^{-(x-\\mu)^2/(2\\sigma^2)}"} /> | (no closed form; use tables/erf) | $\mu$ | $\sigma^2$ |

Note the normal CDF has no elementary closed form — it is computed numerically via the **error function** <MathInline expression={"\\text{erf}"} />. This is why statistics classes hand out z-tables and why lesson 09 emphasizes standardization.

## Three perspectives on continuous distributions

1. **As density (PDF):** the landscape view. Hills where probability concentrates, valleys where it thins. The height $f(x)$ is a density, not a probability — only the area under a region is a probability. This is the most intuitive view but the one that trips up beginners (density can exceed 1).
2. **As accumulation (CDF):** the odometer view. A single curve climbing from 0 to 1, rising fast through the hills and flattening through the valleys. Works for every continuous distribution uniformly, and probabilities are just differences $F(b) - F(a)$. This is the most computational view.
3. **As a story:** each named distribution models a specific physical process. Uniform = "no preference across a range." Exponential = "constant hazard, memoryless waiting." Normal = "the sum of many small independent effects." Match the story to your data and the distribution is chosen for you — same principle as the discrete family in lesson 07.

## Modern applications

**Queueing theory and systems.** The exponential distribution is the backbone of queueing theory — models of servers, latency, and throughput that underpin everything from call center staffing to web server capacity planning. The memoryless property makes the math tractable: the state of the system at any moment depends only on the present, not the past. The M/M/1 queue (Markovian arrivals, Markovian service, one server) is built entirely from exponential distributions.

**Reliability engineering.** The exponential models "constant failure rate" components — electronics in their useful-life phase, where age doesn't matter. When components do wear out (failure rate increases with age), the Weibull distribution generalizes the exponential. The choice of distribution is a statement about the physics of failure.

**Differential privacy and randomness.** Modern privacy-preserving machine learning (differentially private training) adds carefully calibrated noise to data, and the noise is almost always drawn from continuous distributions — Laplace or Gaussian. The shape of the noise distribution directly determines the privacy-utility tradeoff. Uniform noise underpins randomized rounding and secure multiparty computation.

<KeyInsight>
In a continuous distribution, probability is not height — it is area. The PDF is the landscape (density, can exceed 1), and the CDF is the running total of land you've swept past (always climbing 0 to 1). Every probability is a shaded region under the curve, computed as $F(b) - F(a)$. The exponential distribution's memoryless property — $P(X > s+t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$ — makes it the unique model for age-independent waiting times.
</KeyInsight>
