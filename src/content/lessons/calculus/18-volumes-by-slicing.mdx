We've computed areas -- 2D regions. Now we compute **volumes** -- 3D solids. The strategy is the same: slice, compute each piece, and integrate. The key insight is that slicing reduces a 3D problem to a 2D problem. At each x-position, you see a 2D cross-section with area $A(x)$. Stack all the areas and you get volume -- the integral does the stacking for you, adding up infinitely many paper-thin slices.

## The idea

Imagine slicing a solid with a knife perpendicular to the x-axis. Each slice is a thin disk with some cross-sectional area $A(x)$ and thickness $dx$. The volume of each slice is $A(x)\, dx$. Stack all the slices:

<MathBlock expression={"V = \\int_a^b A(x)\\, dx"} />

The integral adds up infinitely many infinitesimally thin slices. Just as the Riemann sum converges to the area under a curve, the slice sum converges to the exact volume.

## The disk method

When the solid is formed by rotating $y = f(x)$ around the x-axis, each cross-section is a circular disk of radius $f(x)$:

<MathBlock expression={"A(x) = \\pi [f(x)]^2"} />

So:

<MathBlock expression={"V = \\pi \\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\\, dx"} />

### Example: Volume of a cone

A cone of height $h$ and base radius $R$ is formed by rotating the line $y = (R/h)x$ from $x = 0$ to $x = h$.

<MathBlock expression={"V = \\pi \\int_0^h \\left(\\frac{R}{h}x\\right)^2 dx = \\pi \\frac{R^2}{h^2} \\int_0^h x^2\\, dx = \\pi \\frac{R^2}{h^2} \\cdot \\frac{h^3}{3} = \\frac{1}{3}\\pi R^2 h"} />

The familiar formula $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$ emerges from the integral.

## The washer method

When the solid has a hole (rotating the region between two curves), the cross-section is a ring — a disk with a disk removed:

<MathBlock expression={"A(x) = \\pi [R(x)]^2 - \\pi [r(x)]^2"} />

where $R(x)$ is the outer radius and $r(x)$ is the inner radius.

### Example: A bowl

Rotate the region between $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = x/2$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 4$ around the x-axis.

<MathBlock expression={"V = \\pi \\int_0^4 [\\sqrt{x}]^2 - [x/2]^2\\, dx = \\pi \\int_0^4 [x - x^2/4]\\, dx = \\pi [x^2/2 - x^3/12]_0^4 = \\pi[8 - 64/12] = \\frac{8\\pi}{3}"} />

<KeyInsight>
Volume by slicing: cut the solid into thin disks (or washers), compute each slice's area A(x), and integrate A(x) dx from one end to the other. The integral replaces an infinite stack of infinitesimal slices.
</KeyInsight>

## Practice

<ExerciseBlock number={1}
  prompt="Find the volume of the solid formed by rotating y = x² from x = 0 to x = 2 around the x-axis."
  hint="Use the disk method: A(x) = π(x²)² = πx⁴."
  answer="V = π∫₀² x⁴ dx = π[x⁵/5]₀² = π(32/5) = 32π/5."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={2}
  prompt="Find the volume of a sphere of radius R using the disk method."
  hint="The sphere is y = √(R²−x²) rotated around the x-axis, from x = −R to x = R."
  answer="V = π∫_{−R}^R (R²−x²) dx = π[R²x − x³/3]_{−R}^R = π[(R³ − R³/3) − (−R³ + R³/3)] = π(4R³/3) = 4πR³/3."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={3}

  hint="No rotation needed — just integrate A(x) directly."
  answer="V = ∫₀⁴ (3 + x) dx = [3x + x²/2]₀⁴ = 12 + 8 = 20 cubic units."
