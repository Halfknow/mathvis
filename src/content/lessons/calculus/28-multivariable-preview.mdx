Everything you've learned -- derivatives, integrals, optimization, series -- extends to functions of several variables. The ideas are the same; there are just more directions to look. In 3D, the derivative becomes a gradient arrow on the landscape. The arrow points uphill; its length tells you how steep the slope is. To descend fastest -- the core idea behind gradient descent in machine learning -- walk in the opposite direction of the gradient arrow.

Drag the (x₀, y₀) sliders to move a point on the surface. Toggle the gradient arrow and slicing planes to see partial derivatives as slopes on the surface.

## Functions of two variables

A function $z = f(x, y)$ takes a point $(x, y)$ in the plane and outputs a height $z$. The graph is a **surface** in 3D space — a landscape with hills, valleys, and saddle points.

## Partial derivatives

Instead of one derivative, you get two:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x} = \\text{slope in the } x \\text{-direction (hold } y \\text{ fixed)}"} />
<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial y} = \\text{slope in the } y \\text{-direction (hold } x \\text{ fixed)}"} />

For $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ (a paraboloid): $\partial f/\partial x = 2x$, $\partial f/\partial y = 2y$.

The partial derivatives measure how steep the surface is in the $x$ and $y$ directions independently. It's like standing on a hillside: the slope depends on which direction you face.

## The gradient

The gradient packs the partial derivatives into a vector:

<MathBlock expression={"\\nabla f = \\left(\\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x}, \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial y}\\right)"} />

The gradient arrow points in the direction of **steepest ascent** on the surface. Its length is the slope in that direction. To descend fastest (like gradient descent in machine learning), follow $-\nabla f$.

## The multivariable chain rule

If $z = f(x, y)$ and $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{dz}{dt} = \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial x}\\frac{dx}{dt} + \\frac{\\partial f}{\\partial y}\\frac{dy}{dt}"} />

The single-variable chain rule gets a term for each input variable.

## Double integrals

The integral extends too. Instead of area under a curve, you get **volume under a surface**:

<MathBlock expression={"V = \\iint_R f(x, y)\\, dA = \\int_a^b \\int_c^d f(x, y)\\, dy\\, dx"} />

You integrate in one variable, then the other. Fubini's theorem says the order doesn't matter (for nice functions).

For $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ over $[0,1] \times [0,1]$:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_0^1 \\int_0^1 (x^2 + y^2)\\, dy\\, dx = \\int_0^1 [x^2 y + y^3/3]_0^1\\, dx = \\int_0^1 (x^2 + 1/3)\\, dx = 1/3 + 1/3 = 2/3"} />

<KeyInsight>
Multivariable calculus lifts every single-variable idea one dimension higher. The derivative becomes a gradient (a vector of partial derivatives), the integral becomes a volume, and optimization searches a landscape with more directions. The core ideas — rates, accumulation, optimization — are unchanged.
</KeyInsight>
