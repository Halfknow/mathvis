# Vector Addition

Forget the formula for a moment. Imagine you're standing at the origin. You walk 3 blocks east and 1 block north — that's one vector. Then from *where you ended up*, you walk another 1 block east and 2 blocks north — that's a second vector. Where are you now?

You're at **4 east, 3 north**. That's the sum. Vector addition is nothing more mysterious than **doing one displacement, then doing another**. The result is the single arrow that takes you from your start to your final position.

## Head-to-tail

The geometric picture is called **head-to-tail** addition: slide the second arrow so its tail sits on the first arrow's tip. The sum is the arrow from the first tail to the second head. Try the presets below to see classic configurations animate into place, or drag the blue and green dots freely.

<VectorAddition client:visible />

Notice: the result doesn't depend on which vector you walk first. Walk green then blue, and you land at the same spot. This is the **commutative property** — not a theorem to memorize, but something you can *see*.

### Commutativity in action

Click the "Parallelogram" preset. Notice the two paths through the parallelogram: blue-then-green along the top, and green-then-blue along the bottom. Both reach the same corner. This is **a + b = b + a**, visible as a geometric fact. The order of the walks does not matter — you always end up at the same spot.

## The parallelogram rule

Draw both vectors starting from the origin. Complete the parallelogram. The diagonal — from the origin to the opposite corner — is the sum. This is the same head-to-tail picture, just drawn twice (once in each order), making the commutativity visible.

The parallelogram is not just a drawing trick — it reveals that vector addition is really about **two independent displacements happening simultaneously**. One displacement along **a**'s direction, one along **b**'s direction. The diagonal is the "shortcut" through the parallelogram, the single arrow that accomplishes both displacements at once.

## Special cases

Vector addition has a few configurations worth recognizing instantly:

- **Canceling**: Try the "Cancel out" preset. When **b** = -**a**, the sum is the zero vector — you walk there and walk right back. The arrows are equal in length but opposite in direction.
- **Same direction**: Try "Same direction." When both arrows point the same way, the sum is a longer arrow in that direction. The lengths add: |**a** + **b**| = |**a**| + |**b**|.
- **Orthogonal**: Try "Orthogonal." When the arrows are perpendicular, the Pythagorean theorem kicks in: |**a** + **b**|² = |**a**|² + |**b**|². The sum arrow is the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by **a** and **b**.
- **Opposite directions**: Try "Opposite dirs." When the arrows point opposite ways but have different lengths, the sum points in the direction of the longer arrow, with length equal to the difference.

### Vector subtraction

Subtraction is addition in disguise: **a - b = a + (-b)**. Geometrically, draw b reversed (rotated 180°), then add head-to-tail. The result points from b's tip to a's tip — the "displacement from b to a."

This is useful when you need to measure the gap between two points. If point P is at position vector p and point Q is at position vector q, then q - p is the arrow from P to Q. Subtraction reveals *relative position*.

## In three dimensions

Head-to-tail works in 3D just as in 2D. The blue arrow is **a**, the green arrow continues from its tip, and the red arrow is the sum.

## Connections

Head-to-tail addition is exactly what happens inside every matrix-vector product. When you compute Ax = x₁c₁ + x₂c₂, you're doing two weighted walks — x₁ steps along c₁'s direction, then x₂ steps along c₂'s direction. The result lands at the linear combination. Lesson 7 will make this explicit.

The triangle inequality ‖a+b‖ ≤ ‖a‖+‖b‖ you explored in exercise 3 is not just about vectors — it's the foundation of all distance-based reasoning in analysis, probability, and machine learning.

## When coordinates arrive

In components, addition is coordinate-wise:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} + \\vec{b} = \\begin{bmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\\\ a_2 + b_2 \\end{bmatrix}'} />

This formula is just the head-to-tail picture written in numbers. **The geometry came first.**
