We've seen that slicing the area into rectangles and adding them up gives an approximation. Now we take the limit and arrive at the **definite integral**.

## The definition

The definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ is:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = \\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \\Delta x"} />

where $\Delta x = (b - a) / n$ and $x_i^*$ is any sample point in the $i$-th sub-interval. As $n$ grows, the Riemann sum approaches a single number: the signed area under $f$ from $a$ to $b$.

For $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 1]$:
- $n = 4$: sum ≈ 0.2188, error ≈ 0.115
- $n = 50$: sum ≈ 0.3234, error ≈ 0.010
- $n = 200$: sum ≈ 0.3329, error ≈ 0.0004

The convergence is relentless. More rectangles → thinner slices → smaller error.

## Properties of the definite integral

The integral inherits properties from the Riemann sum:

**Linearity**: constants pull out, sums split apart.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_a^b [cf(x) + g(x)]\\,dx = c\\int_a^b f(x)\\,dx + \\int_a^b g(x)\\,dx"} />

Geometrically, linearity means you can stretch the height of a region by a factor $c$ (which stretches the area by $c$), or place two regions side by side and add their areas — both give the same result as integrating the combined function.

**Additivity over intervals**: you can split the integral at any point.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_a^c f(x)\\,dx = \\int_a^b f(x)\\,dx + \\int_b^c f(x)\\,dx"} />

Additivity means you can cut the region into pieces and add them up. If you know the area from $a$ to $b$ and from $b$ to $c$, the total is just the sum — just like cutting a physical shape into two parts and weighing them separately.

**Reversal**: flipping the bounds negates the integral.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_b^a f(x)\\,dx = -\\int_a^b f(x)\\,dx"} />

Think of this as traversing the region backwards — the "accumulation direction" reverses, so the sign flips.

## When does the integral exist?

If $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$, then the definite integral exists. This is a powerful guarantee — continuity is enough. The Riemann sums will converge regardless of which sample points you choose.

Discontinuous functions may or may not be integrable. A function with a finite number of jump discontinuities is still integrable. But a function like the Dirichlet function ($f(x) = 1$ if $x$ is rational, $0$ if irrational) is not Riemann integrable.

<KeyInsight>
The definite integral is the limit of Riemann sums as the slices thin to zero width. It yields a single number — the exact signed area — and it exists for any continuous function. The definite integral turns an infinite problem (adding up infinitely many infinitely thin rectangles) into a finite number: the area.
</KeyInsight>
