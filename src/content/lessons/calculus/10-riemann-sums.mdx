How do you compute the area under a curve when the shape isn't a simple triangle or rectangle? **Chop it into rectangles.** This single strategy — approximate with rectangles, then let the rectangles multiply and thin out — turns the fuzzy idea of "area under a curve" into something a computer can calculate to any precision. It is the bridge from the discrete world of sums to the continuous world of integrals.

## The idea

Divide the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ equal slices of width $\Delta x = (b-a)/n$. In each slice, measure the function's height at some sample point, and build a rectangle. Add up all the rectangle areas. That sum is your **Riemann sum**.

<MathBlock expression={"S_n = \\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^{*}) \\cdot \\Delta x, \\qquad \\Delta x = \\frac{b-a}{n}"} />

The sample point <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*}"} /> lives somewhere in the $i$-th sub-interval. Where you put it defines a different *sampling rule*. Three standard choices:

- **Left endpoint rule** $L_n$: sample at <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*} = x_{i-1} = a + (i-1)\\Delta x"} />.
- **Right endpoint rule** $R_n$: sample at <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*} = x_i = a + i\\Delta x"} />.
- **Midpoint rule** $M_n$: sample at <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*} = \\tfrac{1}{2}(x_{i-1} + x_i)"} />.

Each choice gives a slightly different sum for finite $n$, but — and this is the crucial fact — they all converge to the same number as $n \to \infty$.

Start with $n = 4$ and see the chunky approximation. Then slide up to $n = 50$ or $n = 200$ — the rectangles become so thin they virtually disappear into the curve. As $n$ grows, the rectangles "melt" into the area: their jagged tops smooth out, the overestimates and underestimates cancel, and the sum converges to the exact answer.

## Three sampling rules, compared

The three rules differ in *bias* — the direction of their typical error:

- **Left endpoint**: overestimates when $f$ is decreasing, underestimates when $f$ is increasing. The error is "one-sided."
- **Right endpoint**: the bias reverses — overestimates when $f$ is increasing, underestimates when decreasing.
- **Midpoint rule**: samples at the center of each sub-interval. Because the curve is roughly equally above and below the rectangle's top edge within each slice, positive and negative errors *partially cancel*. This makes the midpoint rule dramatically more accurate for the same $n$.

The practical punchline: for a given computational budget (a fixed number of function evaluations), the midpoint rule usually wins. We'll quantify exactly *how much* it wins below.

## Theorem: The integral as a limit of Riemann sums

If $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$, then the Riemann sums converge to a single number as $n \to \infty$, **independent of the choice of sample points** <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*}"} />. That number is the **definite integral**:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = \\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^{*})\\, \\Delta x"} />

The hypothesis (continuity) is what guarantees the limit exists and is the *same* no matter how you sample. For discontinuous functions the limit may fail to exist or may depend on the sample points — continuity tames the approximation so that all roads lead to the same value. This limit definition is the formal birthplace of the integral: chop, sample, sum, then let the slices collapse.

## The convergence, worked out

For $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 1]$, the exact integral is $1/3$. The Riemann sum with the left endpoint rule and $n$ rectangles is:

<MathBlock expression={"L_n = \\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \\left(\\frac{i}{n}\\right)^{2} \\cdot \\frac{1}{n} = \\frac{1}{n^{3}} \\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} i^{2} = \\frac{(n-1)n(2n-1)}{6n^{3}}"} />

As $n \to \infty$, this approaches <MathInline expression={"\\tfrac{2n^{3}}{6n^{3}} = \\tfrac{1}{3}"} />. Verify in the interactive: set $n = 200$ and watch the error shrink to near zero. The right-endpoint rule gives a similar formula tending to $1/3$ from above; both squeeze the true value.

## Darboux sums: squeezing the integral

A cleaner way to see *why* the limit exists is the **Darboux** formulation. Instead of choosing a sample point, in each sub-interval take the *largest* and *smallest* values of $f$:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Upper sum } U_n = \\sum_{i=1}^{n} M_i\\, \\Delta x, \\qquad \\text{Lower sum } L_n = \\sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\\, \\Delta x"} />

where $M_i = \sup f$ and $m_i = \inf f$ on the $i$-th sub-interval. Every Riemann sum — left, right, midpoint, or arbitrary — is *trapped* between the lower and upper Darboux sums: $L_n \le S_n \le U_n$.

For a continuous function on a closed interval, as $n \to \infty$ the gap $U_n - L_n$ shrinks to zero (because $f$ is uniformly continuous, the oscillation within each shrinking sub-interval vanishes). The lower sums creep upward and the upper sums creep downward, squeezing toward a common value — and that value is the integral. This "squeeze" is the rigorous reason the choice of sample point doesn't matter: no matter what you pick, your sum is pinned between two sequences that agree in the limit.

## Why the midpoint rule converges faster

Here is a quantitative surprise. The error of each rule shrinks at a characteristic *rate* as $n$ grows:

<MathBlock expression={"|L_n - I|,\\ |R_n - I| = O\\!\\left(\\tfrac{1}{n}\\right), \\qquad |M_n - I| = O\\!\\left(\\tfrac{1}{n^{2}}\\right)"} />

where $I$ is the exact integral. The left and right rules improve only proportionally to $1/n$ — doubling $n$ halves the error. The midpoint rule improves as $1/n^2$ — doubling $n$ shrinks the error by a factor of **four**. This is why exercise 2 below shows the midpoint rule picking up two decimal places when $n$ goes from 10 to 100.

The intuition: within each sub-interval, the midpoint rectangle's error comes from the *curvature* of $f$ (the second-order term in its Taylor expansion), not its slope. The linear part cancels because the midpoint is symmetric. Symmetry kills the first-order error, leaving only the much smaller quadratic term — a one-step-head-start that compounds as $n$ grows. (The trapezoid rule, which averages the left and right heights, also achieves $O(1/n^2)$ by a similar cancellation.)

## What "area under a curve" really means

The Riemann sum gives us the formal definition of the definite integral:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = \\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^{*})\\, \\Delta x"} />

The integral is the limit of Riemann sums. Infinite thin slices collapsing into an exact signed area. This definition made precise what Cauchy had started in the 1820s. **Bernhard Riemann** (1826--1866), one of the most imaginative mathematicians of the 19th century, formalized the integral by specifying exactly what it means for those sums to converge. His version is not the only definition of the integral (Lebesgue would later generalize it), but it is the one that matches geometric intuition most directly: chop the region into strips, add them up, let the strips shrink to zero.

## Modern applications

Riemann sums are not just a theoretical stepping stone — they are the **actual algorithm** computers use to compute integrals that have no closed form.

- **Numerical integration (quadrature):** the midpoint rule, trapezoid rule, and Simpson's rule are all direct descendants of Riemann sums. Scientific computing, physics simulations, and financial models rely on them daily. When a GPU computes the expected loss of a neural network by averaging over a batch, it is computing a Riemann sum.
- **Machine learning:** training a model minimizes an *expected* loss <MathInline expression={"\\mathbb{E}[L]"} />, which is an integral over the data distribution. In practice we approximate it with a finite sum over a mini-batch — a Riemann sum in disguise. Larger batches give lower-variance estimates, exactly mirroring the $1/n$ convergence.
- **Rendering and graphics:** the color of each pixel in a ray-traced image is an integral over incoming light directions; it is approximated by summing finitely many sampled rays. More samples → less noise, the same convergence story.
- **Probability and statistics:** Monte Carlo methods estimate high-dimensional integrals by averaging function values at random sample points. This is a *randomized* Riemann sum, and it is the only practical way to integrate in hundreds of dimensions.

Darboux upper and lower sums squeeze the integral into existence, and the midpoint rule converges quadratically (O(1/n²)) because symmetry cancels the linear error. This is the geometric meaning of ∫, and it is also the algorithm computers actually run.

## Connections

This lesson makes the [accumulation idea](/courses/calculus/09-accumulation-and-area) rigorous: the informal "stack up slices" becomes a precise sequence of sums whose limit is the integral. The next lesson, [the definite integral](/courses/calculus/11-the-definite-integral), adopts that limit as a definition and derives its algebraic properties (linearity, additivity, order), and introduces **improper integrals** — what happens when an interval or a function runs off to infinity. Then [the Fundamental Theorem](/courses/calculus/12-the-fundamental-theorem) will show that, miraculously, you usually don't need Riemann sums at all — an antiderivative gives the exact answer in one step.

Across disciplines, the Riemann-sum strategy is universal: any time you approximate a continuous quantity by a finite sample (a mini-batch in ML, a Monte Carlo estimate in probability, a pixel grid in graphics), you are running the same chop-sample-sum playbook. The convergence rate tells you how fast the approximation sharpens, and that governs everything from batch sizes to ray counts.
