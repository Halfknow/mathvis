Every physical law that describes how a system evolves is written as a **differential equation** (ODE): an equation that ties a function to its own derivatives. Newton did not write $F = ma$ as a static identity — he wrote it as $m\\,d^2x/dt^2 = F(x)$, a *rule for change* that, once solved, predicts the trajectory of a planet or a pendulum. The derivative tells you *how fast* something is changing *right now*; the equation says that this rate is determined by the current state. Solving the equation means reconstructing the entire future (and past) from that local rule. This is why ODEs are the native language of physics, biology, chemistry, and engineering: they turn a law of nature into a predicted trajectory.

A first-order ODE has the general form:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y)"} />

Read this as: "at every point $(x, y)$ in the plane, the slope of the solution curve is $f(x, y)$." The whole subject is the art of recovering the function $y(x)$ from this instruction.

## The slope field — the geometric picture

Before solving anything analytically, draw the **slope field** (direction field): at each grid point $(x, y)$, plot a small tick whose slope is $f(x, y)$. The solution curves must be tangent to these ticks everywhere — they *flow* through the field like leaves carried by a river. The slope field is the geometric portrait of an ODE; it shows the global structure (where solutions grow, shrink, oscillate) even when no closed-form solution exists.

Try each equation in the explorer:

- $dy/dx = y$: solution curves grow or decay exponentially. Ticks point steeply upward for $y > 0$ and downward for $y < 0$; $y = 0$ is an equilibrium (a horizontal solution every curve either flees from or approaches).
- $dy/dx = -x/y$: solution curves are circles centered at the origin. The field rotates around the center — a hallmark of conservative systems.
- $dy/dx = x + y$: curves that start negative get pulled positive by the $+y$ term, then accelerate upward — a feedback loop.

## Separable equations — the first technique

An ODE is **separable** when $f(x,y)$ factors as $g(x)\\,h(y)$, so you can put all the $y$'s on one side and all the $x$'s on the other:

## Theorem: Method of Separation of Variables

If $dy/dx = g(x)\\,h(y)$ with $h(y) \\ne 0$, then the general solution is given implicitly by

<MathBlock expression={"\\int \\frac{dy}{h(y)} = \\int g(x)\\, dx + C"} />

Evaluating the two integrals yields an implicit relation between $y$ and $x$; solving for $y$ explicitly (when possible) gives the solution family. The constant $C$ is fixed by an **initial condition** $y(x_0) = y_0$.

**Example: $dy/dx = xy$.** Separate: $dy/y = x\\,dx$. Integrate: $\\ln|y| = x^2/2 + C$. Exponentiate: <MathInline expression={"y = A e^{x^2/2}"} /> (absorbing $\\pm e^C$ into $A$). Each choice of $A$ is a different solution curve in the slope field.

**Example: exponential growth/decay, $dP/dt = kP$.** Separate: $dP/P = k\\,dt$. Integrate: $\\ln|P| = kt + C$, so $P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}$. This single equation models population growth, radioactive decay, compound interest, and the charging of an RC circuit — whenever a quantity's rate of change is proportional to its current value.

## First-order linear ODEs — the integrating factor

Not every ODE is separable. The next class is **first-order linear**: $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)$. These are *always* solvable via the **integrating factor** trick.

## Theorem: Integrating Factor for Linear First-Order ODEs

For $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)$, define the integrating factor <MathInline expression={"\\mu(x) = e^{\\int P(x)\\,dx}"} />. Then

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}\\bigl[\\mu(x)\\, y\\bigr] = \\mu(x)\\, Q(x)"} />

so the left side is a perfect derivative. Integrating both sides and dividing by $\\mu(x)$ gives the general solution:

<MathBlock expression={"y(x) = \\frac{1}{\\mu(x)} \\left( \\int \\mu(x)\\, Q(x)\\, dx + C \\right)"} />

**Why it works.** Multiplying by $\\mu(x) = e^{\\int P}$ is engineered so that the product rule collapses the left side: $\\mu y' + \\mu P y = (\\mu y)'$. The factor $\\mu$ is precisely what makes $y' + Py$ into the derivative of a single quantity. This is the same "recognize a perfect derivative" move that appears in [integration by parts](/courses/calculus/14-integration-techniques) — the antidifferentiation is forced by the structure of the equation.

## The Existence and Uniqueness Theorem (Picard-Lindelöf)

So far we have *methods* for special cases. The deep theorem of the subject guarantees that, under mild conditions, a solution exists *and is unique* — so every initial condition pins down exactly one trajectory.

## Theorem: Picard-Lindelöf (Existence and Uniqueness)

Consider the initial value problem $dy/dx = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0) = y_0$. If $f$ is continuous in a rectangle <MathInline expression={"R = \\{(x,y) : |x - x_0| \\le a,\\; |y - y_0| \\le b\\}"} /> and **Lipschitz continuous** in $y$ on $R$ — meaning there is a constant $L$ such that

<MathBlock expression={"|f(x, y_1) - f(x, y_2)| \\le L|y_1 - y_2| \\quad \\text{for all } (x, y_1), (x, y_2) \\text{ in } R"} />

then there exists a unique solution $y(x)$ on some interval <MathInline expression={"[x_0 - h,\\; x_0 + h]"} />.

A convenient sufficient condition: if $\\partial f / \\partial y$ exists and is continuous on $R$, then $f$ is Lipschitz in $y$ and the conclusion holds.

**What this buys you.** Existence means the ODE is not contradictory — a trajectory through $(x_0, y_0)$ really does exist. Uniqueness is the deeper gift: it means **solution curves never cross**. Two trajectories through different initial conditions can never meet; if they did, the shared meeting point would be an initial condition with two solutions, violating uniqueness. This is why the slope field picture is coherent — the curves tile the plane without intersecting.

**Where uniqueness fails.** The equation $dy/dx = \\sqrt{y}$ (with $y \\ge 0$) has $f$ continuous but not Lipschitz at $y = 0$ (the derivative $1/(2\\sqrt{y})$ blows up). From the initial condition $y(0) = 0$, there are *infinitely many* solutions: $y(x) = 0$ always, or $y(x) = \\tfrac{1}{4}(x - c)^2$ for any $c \\ge 0$ (the curve stays flat at zero until $x = c$, then lifts off as a parabola). The Picard-Lindelöf hypotheses rule out exactly this pathology.

## Equilibrium points and stability

For an **autonomous** ODE $dy/dt = f(y)$ (no explicit $t$ dependence), a value $y^*$ with $f(y^*) = 0$ is an **equilibrium**: the constant function $y(t) = y^*$ is a solution. The central question is **stability** — do nearby solutions return to the equilibrium or flee it?

- **Stable:** solutions starting near $y^*$ move *toward* it. Equivalent to $f$ changing sign from $+$ to $-$ as $y$ increases through $y^*$ ($f'(y^*) < 0$).
- **Unstable:** solutions starting near $y^*$ move *away* from it. Sign change from $-$ to $+$ ($f'(y^*) > 0$).
- **Semi-stable:** solutions approach from one side and flee from the other ($f'(y^*) = 0$ and same sign on both sides).

**Logistic growth, $dP/dt = kP(1 - P/K)$.** Equilibria at $P^* = 0$ (unstable — $f'(0) = k > 0$) and $P^* = K$ (stable — $f'(K) = -k < 0$). Any positive population tends to the carrying capacity $K$. This is the simplest model that captures the *saturation* every real population exhibits — exponential growth corrected by a crowding term.

## The many faces of differential equations

- **Newtonian mechanics:** $F = ma$ is a second-order ODE; gravity gives planetary orbits, Hooke's law $F = -kx$ gives simple harmonic motion, and the pendulum $\\theta'' = -(g/L)\\sin\\theta$ reduces to $\\theta'' = -(g/L)\\theta$ via the [small-angle Taylor approximation](/courses/calculus/26-taylor-series).
- **Circuits:** an RL circuit obeys $L\\,dI/dt + RI = V(t)$, a first-order linear ODE solved by the integrating factor. The transient response $I(t) = (V/R)(1 - e^{-Rt/L})$ is the complementary solution plus particular solution.
- **Population dynamics:** exponential growth $dP/dt = kP$ generalizes to the logistic equation (carrying capacity), the Lotka-Volterra predator-prey system, and the SIR epidemic model — each an ODE system whose equilibria encode ecological balance.

## Modern applications

In **biology and pharmacokinetics**, drug concentration in the bloodstream follows $dC/dt = -kC$ (exponential clearance) or, with repeated dosing, a forced linear ODE whose steady-state sets the dosing interval. Tumor growth models, enzyme kinetics (Michaelis-Menten), and neural firing (Hodgkin-Huxley) are all ODE systems whose equilibria and stability determine the physiology.

In **finance**, the Black-Scholes option pricing equation is a *partial* differential equation (the multivariable generalization of an ODE); its simpler ODE cousins model bond pricing and the term structure of interest rates. The vocabulary of equilibria and stability maps directly to "market clearing" and "arbitrage-free."

In **machine learning and control**, the training dynamics of gradient descent are themselves an ODE in the continuous-time limit: $dw/dt = -\\nabla L(w)$. Convergence to a minimum is a stability analysis of this ODE — the loss landscape's equilibria are the critical points $\\nabla L = 0$, and their stability (governed by the Hessian's eigenvalues) decides whether you converge to a true minimum, a saddle, or diverge.

In **climate and epidemiology**, the SIR model $dS/dt = -\\beta SI$, $dI/dt = \\beta SI - \\gamma I$, $dR/dt = \\gamma I$ predicts epidemic peaks and herd immunity thresholds from three ODEs. The basic reproduction number $R_0 = \\beta / \\gamma$ is read off from the stability of the disease-free equilibrium — pure ODE analysis, with life-or-death consequences.

<KeyInsight>
A differential equation specifies the slope at every point; the slope field visualizes this landscape, and solution curves flow along it without crossing — guaranteed by the Picard-Lindelöf uniqueness theorem (f Lipschitz in y means one initial condition gives one trajectory). Separable equations dy/dx = g(x)h(y) yield to separation of variables; first-order linear equations y' + P(x)y = Q(x) yield to the integrating factor mu = e to the integral of P. Autonomous equations dy/dt = f(y) have equilibria at the zeros of f, and an equilibrium y-star is stable when f-prime of y-star is negative (nearby solutions return) and unstable when it is positive (they flee). Logistic growth, Newton's laws, RC circuits, the SIR epidemic model, and continuous-time gradient descent are all ODEs whose behavior is governed by these same few ideas.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson closes the series module by showing where all the previous machinery is *used*. The [Taylor series](/courses/calculus/26-taylor-series) of the previous lesson becomes a solution method: the Frobenius method assumes $y = \\sum a_n x^n$ and solves for the coefficients, producing series solutions for ODEs with no closed form — this is how Bessel and Legendre functions are defined. The [chain rule](/courses/calculus/04-chain-rule) underlies separation of variables (the $dy$ and $dx$ are not magic — the substitution is the chain rule run in reverse). The [integrating factor](/courses/calculus/14-integration-techniques) trick is an application of recognizing perfect derivatives, and the [Fundamental Theorem of Calculus](/courses/calculus/12-fundamental-theorem) is what turns the separated integrals into an explicit solution.

The next lesson, [multivariable preview](/courses/calculus/28-multivariable-preview), generalizes derivatives to *partial* derivatives and gradients — the engine of ODEs in high dimensions. And the finale, [where calculus lives](/courses/calculus/29-where-calculus-lives), shows how ODEs appear in essentially every quantitative discipline: they are how a law of nature becomes a predicted future.
