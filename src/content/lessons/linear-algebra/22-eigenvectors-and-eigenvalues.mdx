# Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues

When a matrix warps space, most vectors get knocked off their original direction — they point somewhere new. But a few special vectors **keep their direction**. They might get stretched or shrunk, but they don't rotate. These are the **eigenvectors**, and their stretch factors are the **eigenvalues**.

This is one of the deepest ideas in linear algebra. Eigenvectors reveal the "skeleton" of a transformation — the axes along which it acts most simply.

## The equation

An eigenvector **v** of a matrix A satisfies:

<MathBlock expression={'A\\vec{v} = \\lambda \\vec{v}'} />

Lambda (λ) is the eigenvalue. Read this geometrically: applying A to **v** gives the same direction **v**, just scaled by λ. No rotation. No shear. Pure stretch.

## Finding eigenvalues

Rearrange the equation:

<MathBlock expression={'(A - \\lambda I)\\vec{v} = \\vec{0}'} />

This has a nonzero solution **v** only if det(A - λI) = 0. So eigenvalues are the roots of the **characteristic polynomial** det(A - λI).

For a 2x2 matrix with entries a, b, c, d:

<MathBlock expression={'\\lambda^2 - (a+d)\\lambda + (ad - bc) = 0'} />

### Trace and determinant reveal everything

Two numbers already sitting in the matrix encode everything about its stretching behavior:

- **trace = λ₁ + λ₂**: The trace (a + d) is the *sum* of the eigenvalues. It measures the "average stretching" — a trace of 3 means the transformation expands by 1.5 on average.
- **det = λ₁ · λ₂**: The determinant (ad - bc) is the *product* of the eigenvalues. It measures the total area scaling — a determinant of 6 means the transformation multiplies areas by 6.

These two numbers, already visible at a glance, determine the eigenvalues completely. You can find them without solving the characteristic polynomial if you know the trace and determinant: the eigenvalues are the two numbers that add to the trace and multiply to the determinant.

## Visual examples

Click each preset and observe:

- **"Shear"**: Only the x-axis stays on its line (eigenvalue 1). All other vectors get tilted. The transformation has only one eigendirection — horizontal.

- **"Rotation"**: The banner reads "No real eigenvalues." Rotation moves every vector off its line — the eigenvalues are imaginary numbers (±i), reflecting the rotation's spiraling nature. No direction is respected.

- **"Symmetric"**: Both eigenvectors are **perpendicular to each other**. This is not a coincidence — symmetric matrices (where A = Aᵀ) always have orthogonal eigenvectors. The symmetric preset (a=2, b=1, c=1, d=2) has eigenvectors along (1,1) and (1,-1), which are at right angles.

- **"Diagonal"**: The eigenvectors are exactly the coordinate axes. For a diagonal matrix, the eigenvalues are sitting right on the diagonal — nothing to compute.

- **"Defective"**: This matrix [[2,1],[0,2]] has eigenvalue 2 (repeated), but only **one** eigendirection — the x-axis. The algebra says there should be two eigenvectors, but geometry says there's only one. This is a **defective** matrix.

### Defective matrices

Most matrices have a full set of eigenvectors. But some don't. The matrix [[2,1],[0,2]] has eigenvalue 2 with **algebraic multiplicity** 2 (it appears twice as a root of the characteristic polynomial), but **geometric multiplicity** only 1 (only one linearly independent eigenvector exists).

Geometrically, this means the transformation has only one "preferred axis." Along the x-axis, vectors are stretched by 2. Everywhere else, vectors are both stretched and shoved sideways. The eigenvector isn't enough to build a complete coordinate system for this transformation — you need a generalized eigenvector to fill the gap.

## Why eigenvectors matter

Eigenvectors give you the **natural coordinate system** for a transformation. If you rewrite a matrix in its eigenbasis, it becomes diagonal — just a list of stretch factors. Complex transformations simplify to independent stretches along independent axes.

This idea powers:
- **Differential equations**: Solutions are combinations of eigenvectors, each evolving independently at its own rate e^(λt). The eigenvalues determine whether solutions grow, decay, or oscillate.
- **Principal component analysis**: The eigenvectors of the covariance matrix point along the most "interesting" directions in your data — where the variance is largest.
- **Google's PageRank**: The steady-state distribution of web surfing is an eigenvector of the link matrix with eigenvalue 1 — the distribution that doesn't change when you click another link.
- **Quantum mechanics**: Observable values (energy, momentum, spin) are eigenvalues of Hermitian operators, and the measured states are eigenvectors — the only states that give a definite answer when you measure.
