# Orthogonal Bases and Gram-Schmidt

Two arrows are easiest to work with when they're perpendicular. Orthogonal bases are "clean" — each direction is independent, measurements don't interfere, and projections are trivial.

But what if your basis vectors aren't perpendicular? The **Gram-Schmidt process** is a systematic way to "straighten" any set of independent vectors into an orthogonal set.

## The idea, in two steps

Start with two non-perpendicular vectors v1 and v2.

**Step 1:** Keep v1. Normalize it to length 1. Call this u1.

**Step 2:** Take v2 and subtract its projection onto u1. What's left is the part of v2 that's perpendicular to u1. Normalize it. Call this u2.

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{u}_1 = \\frac{\\vec{v}_1}{\\lVert\\vec{v}_1\\rVert}, \\quad \\vec{u}_2 = \\frac{\\vec{v}_2 - (\\vec{v}_2 \\cdot \\vec{u}_1)\\vec{u}_1}{\\lVert\\vec{v}_2 - (\\vec{v}_2 \\cdot \\vec{u}_1)\\vec{u}_1\\rVert}'} />

The key insight: subtracting the projection "cleans" v2 of any component along u1's direction. Only the perpendicular piece survives.

## Why orthogonal bases matter

Orthogonal bases simplify everything:
- **Projection** onto an orthogonal basis doesn't require matrix inversion — just dot products
- **Least squares** reduces to projecting onto the column space
- **QR decomposition** is just Gram-Schmidt expressed as a matrix factorization
- **Fourier series** decomposes functions into an orthogonal basis of sines and cosines

## Why orthogonal bases simplify everything

Three calculations become trivial with an orthogonal basis:

**Projection** onto an orthogonal basis doesn't require matrix inversion. To project v onto the span of orthogonal vectors u₁, u₂, just take dot products: proj(v) = (v·u₁)u₁ + (v·u₂)u₂. No (AᵀA)⁻¹ needed.

**Coordinates** in an orthogonal basis are just dot products. If (u₁, u₂) is orthonormal, then v = (v·u₁)u₁ + (v·u₂)u₂ — the coefficients are simply the dot products with each basis vector. No system of equations to solve.

**Parseval's identity**: ‖v‖² = |v·u₁|² + |v·u₂|². The total energy of a vector equals the sum of energies in each orthogonal direction. No cross-terms — orthogonal directions don't "interfere."

This is why Fourier analysis works: sines and cosines form an orthogonal basis for functions, and Fourier coefficients are just "dot products" (integrals) with each basis function.

## QR decomposition preview

Gram-Schmidt is a matrix factorization in disguise. If you collect the orthogonal vectors u₁, u₂ as columns of a matrix Q, and the Gram-Schmidt coefficients into an upper-triangular matrix R, then the original vectors satisfy A = QR. Lesson 28 will develop this into the QR decomposition — one of the most important tools in numerical linear algebra.

### Numerical stability

Classical Gram-Schmidt (as presented here) can lose orthogonality due to floating-point rounding errors. **Modified Gram-Schmidt** subtracts projections one at a time, which is more stable. In practice, numerical software uses **Householder reflections** — an entirely different approach that achieves the same QR factorization with even better stability. The principle is the same: build an orthogonal basis, but the algorithm changes for robustness.

## Connections

Gram-Schmidt connects projection (lesson 10) to factorization (lesson 28). It's the algorithmic heart of QR decomposition, which underlies numerical linear algebra — numpy.linalg.solve uses QR internally. Gram-Schmidt is also the conceptual ancestor of the QR algorithm for computing eigenvalues, the most important numerical algorithm in linear algebra.

The orthogonal basis you build here will become the columns of Q in lesson 28, and the projection coefficients will fill the matrix R.
