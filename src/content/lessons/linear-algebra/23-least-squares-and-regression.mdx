# Least Squares and Regression

Real data never exactly satisfies a linear equation. If you measure eight data points, no single line passes through all eight. The measurements are noisy, the world is messy, and the equations you write down are approximations. Least squares finds the *best* line — the one that minimizes total squared error. The remarkable fact is that "best approximation" and "projection onto a column space" are the same idea, expressed in two different languages.

## The geometry of best approximation

When Ax = b has no solution, the vector b sits outside the column space of A. You cannot reach b exactly — no input x will ever land there. But you can get close. The closest reachable point is the **projection** of b onto Col(A). Call that projected point Ax̂. The difference

<MathBlock>{`\\vec{r} = \\vec{b} - A\\hat{x}`}</MathBlock>

is the **residual** — the gap between where you want to be and where you can get. That residual is perpendicular to the column space. It points straight out from Col(A) toward b, making a right angle with every column of A. This perpendicularity is not a coincidence — it is the defining condition for "closest point."

## The normal equations

The condition "the residual is perpendicular to Col(A)" translates into algebra. Every column of A is orthogonal to r, so:

<MathBlock>{`A^T \\vec{r} = A^T(\\vec{b} - A\\hat{x}) = \\vec{0}`}</MathBlock>

Rearranging gives the **normal equations**:

<MathBlock>{`A^T A \\hat{x} = A^T \\vec{b}`}</MathBlock>

Why does left-multiplying by <MathInline>{`A^T`}</MathInline> work? Each row of <MathInline>{`A^T`}</MathInline> is a column of A. So <MathInline>{`A^T \\vec{r}`}</MathInline> collects the dot product of every column of A with the residual. Setting all those dot products to zero is exactly the orthogonality condition — the residual is perpendicular to every column, hence perpendicular to the whole column space.

When the columns of A are linearly independent, <MathInline>{`A^T A`}</MathInline> is invertible and the solution is unique:

<MathBlock>{`\\hat{x} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T \\vec{b}`}</MathBlock>

## Why it works geometrically

Think of it in two dimensions. The column space is a plane (or a line, or all of space). The vector b is a point somewhere nearby, possibly off the plane. You want the closest point on the plane to b.

The shortest path from b to the plane is the perpendicular — the line that makes a right angle with the plane. Any other point on the plane is farther away, because the perpendicular distance is always the shortest. This is just the Pythagorean theorem: if the residual is perpendicular to Col(A), then

<MathBlock>{`\\|\\vec{b} - A\\hat{x}\\|^2 = \\|\\vec{b}\\|^2 - \\|A\\hat{x}\\|^2`}</MathBlock>

and any non-perpendicular choice would add an extra term. The perpendicular projection is the global minimum of the squared error. No other x can do better.

## From lines to planes

So far we have talked about fitting a line <MathInline>{`y = mx + c`}</MathInline> through scattered data points. But the same framework handles any linear model.

To fit a line, build the **design matrix**:

<MathBlock>{`A = \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & x_1 \\\\ 1 & x_2 \\\\ \\vdots & \\vdots \\\\ 1 & x_n \\end{bmatrix}, \\quad \\vec{b} = \\begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\\\ y_2 \\\\ \\vdots \\\\ y_n \\end{bmatrix}`}</MathBlock>

Each row is <MathInline>{`[1,\\, x_i]`}</MathInline>, and the solution <MathInline>{`\\hat{x} = (c,\\, m)`}</MathInline> gives intercept and slope.

To fit a parabola <MathInline>{`y = ax^2 + bx + c`}</MathInline>, add a column:

<MathBlock>{`A = \\begin{bmatrix} 1 & x_1 & x_1^2 \\\\ 1 & x_2 & x_2^2 \\\\ \\vdots & \\vdots & \\vdots \\\\ 1 & x_n & x_n^2 \\end{bmatrix}`}</MathBlock>

The normal equations <MathInline>{`A^T A \\hat{x} = A^T \\vec{b}`}</MathInline> still give the best-fit coefficients. The geometry is identical — project b onto the (now three-dimensional) column space of A. More columns means a richer column space, and the fit gets closer to the data.

## Connections

Least squares is the child of two ideas you already know. The **projection** you saw earlier (projecting a vector onto a subspace) is the geometry. The **column space** (what outputs a matrix can reach) is the algebra. Least squares says: when b is unreachable, project onto the reachable set.

This is also the foundation of statistical regression and machine learning. Every linear model — from simple linear regression to neural networks trained with mean squared error — is doing a version of this projection. The QR decomposition (lesson 28) will give us a numerically stable way to solve the normal equations without ever forming <MathInline>{`A^T A`}</MathInline> explicitly.

Least squares projects b onto the column space of A. The normal equations A<sup>T</sup>Ax&#770; = A<sup>T</sup>b find the unique point in Col(A) closest to b — the best approximation when an exact solution doesn't exist.
</KeyInsight>
