# 3D Transformations

Everything you learned in 2D extends naturally to 3D. A 3x3 matrix transforms a grid *cube* into a parallelepiped (a slanted box). The columns are still where the basis vectors land — now there are three of them.

## Same rules, more room

A 3x3 matrix has three columns, each a 3D vector:

<MathBlock expression={'A = \\begin{bmatrix} a & d & g \\\\ b & e & h \\\\ c & f & i \\end{bmatrix} = \\bigl[\\, A\\hat{e}_1 \\;\\; A\\hat{e}_2 \\;\\; A\\hat{e}_3 \\,\\bigr]'} />

The three columns tell you where (1,0,0), (0,1,0), and (0,0,1) land. Everything else follows by linearity — just as in 2D.

## The determinant in 3D

The 3D determinant measures **volume scaling**. It tells you how much the unit cube's volume changes under the transformation.

- det = 6: the cube's volume becomes 6
- det = 0: the cube collapses flat (rank drops)
- det < 0: the cube turns inside-out (orientation flips)

The formula is longer, but the geometric meaning is identical to 2D: **how much does the transformation scale the fundamental unit of space?**

## 3D rotation

Rotations in 3D are more nuanced than 2D. In 2D, rotation happens around a point. In 3D, it happens around an *axis*. Rotation around the z-axis:

<MathBlock expression={'R_z(\\theta) = \\begin{bmatrix} \\cos\\theta & -\\sin\\theta & 0 \\\\ \\sin\\theta & \\cos\\theta & 0 \\\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\end{bmatrix}'} />

The third column (0, 0, 1) shows that e3 stays put — the z-axis is the rotation axis. The upper-left 2x2 block is the familiar 2D rotation.

<FormulaReadout formulas={[
  { label: "3D 变换", expression: "A\\vec{x} = x_1\\vec{c}_1 + x_2\\vec{c}_2 + x_3\\vec{c}_3" },
  { label: "3D 行列式", expression: "\\det(A) = \\text{signed volume of parallelepiped}" },
]} />

<KeyInsight>
3D transformations follow the same rules as 2D — columns are destinations, determinant measures volume scaling, and eigenvectors are the "unchanged directions." The only new complexity is that rotation happens around an axis, not a point.
</KeyInsight>
