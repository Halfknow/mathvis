The chain rule says: the derivative of $f(g(x))$ is $f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$. u-Substitution says: if you see something that looks like $f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$ inside an integral, you can undo the chain rule. Think of it as a change of coordinates: you translate the integral into a simpler language (the u-world), solve it there, then translate back to x. The key insight is that $du$ carries the derivative information -- it encodes how the old variable stretches and compresses into the new one.

## The method

1. **Choose** $u = g(x)$ — pick the "inner function."
2. **Compute** $du = g'(x)\, dx$.
3. **Rewrite** the integral in terms of $u$.
4. **Integrate** the simpler $u$-integral.
5. **Substitute back** $u = g(x)$.

## Example 1: $\int 2x \cos(x^2)\, dx$

Spot the pattern: $2x$ is the derivative of $x^2$.

Let $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int 2x \\cos(x^2)\\, dx = \\int \\cos(u)\\, du = \\sin(u) + C = \\sin(x^2) + C"} />

The $2x\, dx$ neatly becomes $du$, and the integral simplifies from a complicated expression in $x$ to a basic integral in $u$.

## Example 2: $\int x e^{x^2}\, dx$

Let $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$, so $x\, dx = du/2$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int x e^{x^2}\\, dx = \\frac{1}{2}\\int e^u\\, du = \\frac{1}{2}e^u + C = \\frac{1}{2}e^{x^2} + C"} />

## Example 3: Definite integral $\int_0^1 \frac{x}{1+x^2}\, dx$

Let $u = 1 + x^2$, then $du = 2x\, dx$, so $x\, dx = du/2$. When $x = 0$: $u = 1$. When $x = 1$: $u = 2$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_0^1 \\frac{x}{1+x^2}\\, dx = \\frac{1}{2}\\int_1^2 \\frac{du}{u} = \\frac{1}{2}[\\ln u]_1^2 = \\frac{\\ln 2}{2}"} />

Notice: for definite integrals, you change the bounds too. No need to substitute back.

## When u-substitution fails

Not every integral yields to substitution. If you pick $u$ but $du$ doesn't appear in the integrand (or can't be isolated), try a different $u$ or a different technique entirely.

<KeyInsight>
u-Substitution is the chain rule in reverse. Spot the inner function, let u equal it, and hope its derivative appears somewhere in the integrand. If it does, the integral simplifies.
</KeyInsight>
