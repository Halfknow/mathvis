# Basis Change

A basis is a pair of arrows that defines a coordinate system. You've been using the **standard basis**: e1 = (1, 0) pointing right, e2 = (0, 1) pointing up. Every point in the plane gets coordinates by asking "how many e1's and how many e2's do I need?"

But what if you tilt the axes? The point doesn't move — but its coordinates change. **Basis change** is the algebra of switching between coordinate systems.

## The same point, two languages

Take the point P = (3, 2) in standard coordinates. Now define a new basis:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{b}_1 = (2, 1), \\quad \\vec{b}_2 = (-1, 2)'} />

In this new system, P has *different* coordinates (c1, c2) satisfying:

<MathBlock expression={'c_1 \\vec{b}_1 + c_2 \\vec{b}_2 = (3, 2)'} />

Solving: c1 = 8/5, c2 = 1/5. The point is "0.8 copies of b1 plus 1 copy of b2" — same point, different description.

## The change-of-basis matrix

The relationship between old coordinates x and new coordinates c is a matrix equation:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{x} = P\\vec{c}'} />

where P is the matrix whose columns are the new basis vectors. To go from old to new: invert P.

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{c} = P^{-1}\\vec{x}'} />

This is why basis change is a matrix operation — and why it matters. Every time you "rotate your head" to see the same space from a different angle, you're applying a basis change.

## Why it matters

Basis change is the hidden engine behind:
- **Diagonalization** — switching to the eigenbasis makes the matrix diagonal (lesson 23)
- **Compression** — JPEG and MP3 use basis changes to find efficient representations
- **Principal Component Analysis** — rotating to the "natural" basis of data reveals its structure

The pattern is always the same: the *same object* looks simpler when described in the *right coordinates*.

<KeyInsight>
A basis change doesn't move the point — it relabels it. The change-of-basis matrix P has the new basis vectors as columns, and P inverse converts standard coordinates to new coordinates.
</KeyInsight>

**Connections.** Basis change is the algebra behind diagonalization (lesson 26): rewriting a matrix in its eigenbasis makes it diagonal — just a list of stretch factors. Every change of basis is a matrix multiplication. SVD (lesson 27) is a double basis change: different bases for input and output, connected by singular values.

  hint="Solve 6 = c1*2 and 6 = c2*3."
  answer="c1 = 3, c2 = 2. The new coordinates are (3, 2). This basis just rescales the axes — the x-axis is stretched by 2, the y-axis by 3."

<ExerciseBlock
  number={2}
  prompt="Can b1 = (1, 1) and b2 = (2, 2) form a basis? What happens to the determinant?"
  hint="Check if they point in independent directions."
  answer="No. b2 = 2*b1, so they point in the same direction. The determinant is 1*2 - 1*2 = 0. The change-of-basis matrix is not invertible — you cannot describe every point with these two vectors."
/>

<ExerciseBlock
  number={3}
  prompt="If P = [[2, 1], [1, 2]] (columns are the new basis), what is P inverse? Use it to find the new coordinates of (5, 4)."
  hint="det(P) = 2*2 - 1*1 = 3. P inverse = (1/3)*[[2, -1], [-1, 2]]."
  answer="P inverse = (1/3)*[[2,-1],[-1,2]]. New coords = P^(-1) * (5,4) = (1/3)*(2*5 + (-1)*4, (-1)*5 + 2*4) = (1/3)*(6, 3) = (2, 1). Verify: 2*(2,1) + 1*(1,2) = (5,4). ✓"
/>

<ExerciseBlock
  number={4}
  prompt="The basis {(1,0),(1,1)} has coordinates (3, 2). What are the standard coordinates? What matrix converts from this basis to standard?"
  hint="Multiply coordinates by the basis vectors: 3*(1,0) + 2*(1,1). The change-of-basis matrix has basis vectors as columns."
  answer="Standard coordinates = 3*(1,0) + 2*(1,1) = (3,0) + (2,2) = (5,2). The matrix P = [[1,1],[0,1]] — its columns are (1,0) and (1,1). Indeed P*(3,2) = (1*3+1*2, 0*3+1*2) = (5,2)."
