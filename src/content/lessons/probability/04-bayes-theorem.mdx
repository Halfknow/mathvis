Bayes' theorem is about **flipping your perspective**. A doctor knows: if you have the disease, the test comes back positive 99% of the time. That is <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{disease})"} />. But what you, the patient, actually need to know is the reverse: given that the test came back positive, what is the chance I actually have the disease? That is <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease} \\mid \\text{positive})"} />.

These two quantities are *not* the same, and confusing them leads to profoundly wrong conclusions. In the scenario above — a disease that affects 1 in 100 people, a test that is 99% sensitive and 95% specific — a positive result means you have roughly a **16.6% chance** of being sick. Not 99%. Not even close. Bayes' theorem is the bridge that gets you from the doctor's perspective to the patient's.

## The formula

Bayes' theorem in its simplest form:

$<MathInline expression={"P(H \\mid E) = \\frac{P(E \\mid H) \\cdot P(H)}{P(E)}"} />$

Each piece has a name and a geometric meaning:

- **Prior** $P(H)$ — your belief in the hypothesis *before* you see any evidence. This is the base rate. It shrinks the world before you even look at the data. If the disease is rare (1%), the prior is tiny, and it takes extraordinary evidence to overcome it.
- **Likelihood** $P(E \mid H)$ — how well the hypothesis *predicts* the evidence. If the disease is present, how likely is a positive test? This is the direction the doctor knows.
- **Posterior** $P(H \mid E)$ — your updated belief *after* seeing the evidence. This is what you actually want.
- **Evidence** $P(E)$ — the total probability of seeing this evidence under *all* possible hypotheses. It acts as a normalizing constant, making sure the posterior is a valid probability.

The evidence term can be expanded using the law of total probability. For two mutually exclusive hypotheses $H$ and $\neg H$:

$$P(E) = P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H) + P(E \mid \neg H) \cdot P(\neg H)$$

This says: the chance of seeing the evidence is the sum over every way the evidence could appear — weighted by how likely each path is.

## The visual picture

Imagine a rectangle representing all possible outcomes. Divide it into **horizontal strips** — one strip for each hypothesis. The height of each strip is proportional to the prior probability $P(H_i)$. A rare hypothesis gets a thin strip. A common one gets a wide strip.

Now draw a **vertical line** through each strip, dividing it into two regions: the evidence $E$ occurs on the left, and $\neg E$ on the right. The position of the vertical line is determined by the likelihood $P(E \mid H_i)$ — if the hypothesis strongly predicts the evidence, the left portion of that strip is large.

Bayes' theorem asks: look at the entire left column (all the $E$-regions stacked up). What fraction of that column comes from the $H_1$-strip?

$<MathInline expression={"P(H_1 \\mid E) = \\frac{\\text{area of } H_1\\text{-strip's } E\\text{-region}}{\\text{total area of the entire } E\\text{-column}}"} />$

When the prior for $H_1$ is tiny, its strip is thin. Even if nearly all of that thin strip falls in the $E$-column, the absolute area is small compared to the $E$-contributions from the fat $\neg H_1$-strip. That is why a rare disease with an accurate test still yields a surprisingly low posterior.

## Worked example: the medical test

A disease affects **1%** of the population. A test has:

- **Sensitivity** (true positive rate): 99% — if you are sick, the test says positive with probability 0.99
- **Specificity** (true negative rate): 95% — if you are healthy, the test says negative with probability 0.95

You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

### Step 1: Set up the priors

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease}) = 0.01, \\quad P(\\text{healthy}) = 0.99"} />$

The healthy strip is 99 times wider than the sick strip. This is the base rate at work.

### Step 2: The likelihoods

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{disease}) = 0.99"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{positive} \\mid \\text{healthy}) = 1 - 0.95 = 0.05"} />$

### Step 3: The 10,000-person table

Imagine 10,000 people tested:

| | Sick (100) | Healthy (9,900) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Test +** | 99 | 495 | 594 |
| **Test -** | 1 | 9,405 | 9,406 |

Look at the top row. There are 594 positive results in total. Of those, only 99 come from actually sick people. The other 495 are **false positives** — healthy people who happened to test positive.

### Step 4: Apply Bayes

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{disease} \\mid \\text{positive}) = \\frac{99}{594} = \\frac{99}{99 + 495} \\approx 0.166"} />$

**About 16.6%.** Despite a test that seems highly accurate, a positive result means you probably do *not* have the disease. The reason: the healthy population is so large that even a 5% false positive rate generates far more false positives (495) than the true positives (99) from the tiny sick population.

This is not a paradox. It is the prior doing its job. A rare hypothesis needs strong evidence to become probable.

## The odds form of Bayes' theorem

Dividing Bayes' theorem for $H$ by the same for $\neg H$ gives a cleaner version:

$<MathInline expression={"\\underbrace{\\frac{P(H \\mid E)}{P(\\neg H \\mid E)}}_{\\text{posterior odds}} = \\underbrace{\\frac{P(E \\mid H)}{P(E \\mid \\neg H)}}_{\\text{likelihood ratio (Bayes factor)}} \\times \\underbrace{\\frac{P(H)}{P(\\neg H)}}_{\\text{prior odds}}"} />$

This says: the **posterior odds** equal the **prior odds** times the **Bayes factor** (how much more likely the evidence is under $H$ than under $\neg H$). The odds form is computationally cleaner — you multiply rather than normalize — and it reveals that evidence updates your beliefs *multiplicatively*, not additively.

## The Monty Hall problem

You are on a game show. Three doors. Behind one door is a car; behind the other two, goats. You pick door 1. The host, Monty, opens door 3 to reveal a goat. He asks: do you want to switch to door 2?

Most people feel the remaining two doors should be equally likely. They are wrong. **You should switch.**

### Bayes' thinking

Your prior for each door: <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{car behind } i) = 1/3"} /> for $i = 1, 2, 3$.

Monty's action is **evidence**. He always opens a goat door, and he chooses which door to open based on where the car actually is. Let $E$ be the event "Monty opens door 3."

- If the car is behind door 1 (your pick), Monty opens door 2 or door 3 at random. So <MathInline expression={"P(E \\mid \\text{door 1}) = 1/2"} />.
- If the car is behind door 2, Monty *must* open door 3. So <MathInline expression={"P(E \\mid \\text{door 2}) = 1"} />.
- If the car is behind door 3, Monty cannot open it. So <MathInline expression={"P(E \\mid \\text{door 3}) = 0"} />.

The total probability of the evidence:

$<MathInline expression={"P(E) = \\frac{1}{3} \\cdot \\frac{1}{2} + \\frac{1}{3} \\cdot 1 + \\frac{1}{3} \\cdot 0 = \\frac{1}{2}"} />$

Now apply Bayes:

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{door 1} \\mid E) = \\frac{(1/2)(1/3)}{1/2} = \\frac{1}{3}"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{door 2} \\mid E) = \\frac{(1)(1/3)}{1/2} = \\frac{2}{3}"} />$

Door 2 is **twice as likely** as door 1. Switching wins 2/3 of the time.

The intuition: when you picked door 1, there was a 2/3 chance the car was behind one of the other two doors. Monty's revelation does not change that — it concentrates the 2/3 into the single remaining door. He has filtered out one goat for you.

## Application: the Naive Bayes classifier

Bayes' theorem powers one of the simplest yet effective machine learning classifiers. To classify an email as spam or not, given its words $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n$:

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{spam} \\mid w_1, \\ldots, w_n) = \\frac{P(w_1, \\ldots, w_n \\mid \\text{spam}) \\cdot P(\\text{spam})}{P(w_1, \\ldots, w_n)}"} />$

The **naive** assumption: words are conditionally independent given the class:

$<MathInline expression={"P(w_1, \\ldots, w_n \\mid \\text{spam}) = \\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \\mid \\text{spam})"} />$

This reduces a hard joint probability to a product of easy-to-estimate word frequencies. Despite the unrealistic independence assumption, Naive Bayes works remarkably well for text classification, spam filtering, and sentiment analysis. It is Bayes' theorem applied at industrial scale.

## A preview: Bayesian updating

Bayes' theorem describes a single update: prior → posterior. But what happens when you get *another* piece of evidence? The posterior from the first update becomes the prior for the second:

$<MathInline expression={"P(H) \\xrightarrow{E_1} P(H \\mid E_1) \\xrightarrow{E_2} P(H \\mid E_1, E_2) \\xrightarrow{E_3} \\cdots"} />$

This is **Bayesian updating** — the core of Bayesian inference (lessons 21–23). When the prior and posterior belong to the same family (e.g., Beta), they are called **conjugate priors**, and updating is computationally trivial. For example, a Beta prior combined with Binomial data yields a Beta posterior — just update the parameters. This is why the Beta distribution is the default prior for probabilities.

<KeyInsight>
Bayes' theorem says: your posterior belief is the prior times the likelihood, normalized. A tiny prior acts like a narrow funnel — even strong evidence can only push so much probability through a narrow opening. The odds form reveals that evidence updates beliefs multiplicatively, and Naive Bayes turns this into a practical classifier.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

Bayes' theorem is the capstone of Module 1 — it unifies [conditional probability](/courses/probability/03-conditional-probability) (the definition of $P(H|E)$) with the [law of total probability](/courses/probability/03-conditional-probability) (the denominator $P(E)$). It also looks *backward* to [counting](/courses/probability/02-counting-enumeration): the medical test table is just careful enumeration of the sample space.

Forward, Bayes' theorem splits into two major threads. The **frequentist** thread (lessons 17–20) uses likelihood and the law of total probability for hypothesis testing and confidence intervals, treating parameters as fixed but unknown. The **Bayesian** thread (lessons 21–23) treats parameters as random variables and uses Bayes' theorem to update beliefs — culminating in Bayesian inference with conjugate priors and MCMC methods.

The Naive Bayes classifier connects to machine learning directly — it's one of the simplest generative models. The odds form (Bayes factors) connects to model comparison and **Bayesian model selection**, where you compare how well competing models predict data. The prosecutor's fallacy from lesson 03 is resolved precisely by Bayes' theorem: <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{evidence} | \\text{innocent}) \\neq P(\\text{innocent} | \\text{evidence})"} />.

<ExerciseBlock number={6}
  prompt="Explain why P(evidence | hypothesis) ≠ P(hypothesis | evidence), and give a real-world example where confusing them leads to a serious error."
  hint="Think about what each probability conditions on. The 'prosecutor's fallacy' is the classic example."
  answer="P(E|H) asks 'if H were true, how likely is E?' while P(H|E) asks 'given that we observed E, how likely is H?' These differ by the prior P(H). Example (prosecutor's fallacy): 'The DNA match probability is 1 in a million, so the defendant is guilty with 99.9999% certainty.' This confuses P(match|innocent)=10⁻⁶ with P(innocent|match). <ExerciseBlock number={7}
  prompt="Derive Bayes' theorem from the definition of conditional probability and the multiplication rule. Show that P(H|E)·P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) and explain why."

  answer="By the multiplication rule: P(H ∩ E) = P(H|E)·P(E) and also P(H ∩ E) = P(E|H)·P(H) (just swap roles). Setting them equal: P(H|E)·P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H). The key insight: P(H ∩ E) is a single quantity that can be reached from two directions — conditioning on E first, or conditioning on H first. Bayes' theorem equates the two paths."
