## A conveyor belt for functions

Imagine a factory with two machines in a row. The first machine takes raw material `x` and outputs `g(x)`. The second machine takes that output and produces `f(g(x))`. Each machine has its own speed — its own derivative. The chain rule says: **multiply the speeds**.

Think of it this way: $x$ enters the first machine ($g$), gets transformed, comes out as $g(x)$, then enters the second machine ($f$). The overall speed change — how fast the final output responds to a nudge in the input — is the product of each machine's speed change, evaluated at the right point. If the inner machine doubles the speed and the outer machine triples it, the combined effect is a sixfold speedup.

## The formal statement

For a composite function $h(x) = f(g(x))$, the derivative is:

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \\cdot g'(x)"} />

The key insight is that $f'$ is evaluated at $g(x)$, not at $x$. The outer derivative is measured at the output of the inner function.

## Three examples, one pattern

Let's trace the chain rule through concrete examples:

**Example 1: $h(x) = \sin(x^2)$**

The inner function is $g(x) = x^2$ with $g'(x) = 2x$. The outer function is $f(u) = \sin(u)$ with $f'(u) = \cos(u)$. So:

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = \\cos(x^2) \\cdot 2x"} />

**Example 2: $h(x) = e^{3x}$**

Inner: $g(x) = 3x$, $g'(x) = 3$. Outer: $f(u) = e^u$, $f'(u) = e^u$.

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = e^{3x} \\cdot 3 = 3e^{3x}"} />

**Example 3: $h(x) = (x^2 + 1)^3$**

This is where the chain rule really shines. Expanding this cubic would be painful. Instead: inner $g(x) = x^2 + 1$, $g'(x) = 2x$. Outer $f(u) = u^3$, $f'(u) = 3u^2$.

<MathBlock expression={"h'(x) = 3(x^2+1)^2 \\cdot 2x = 6x(x^2+1)^2"} />

## Why the chain rule is the workhorse

Most functions you'll encounter in science and engineering are compositions. Neural networks are chains of layer functions — backpropagation is just the chain rule applied repeatedly. Physics equations chain coordinate transformations. The chain rule is not just one tool in the toolbox — it's the mechanism that connects all the others.

This is why we can differentiate something like $\sin(x^2)$ without expanding it first. The chain rule decomposes the rate of change into stages: first ask "how fast is $x^2$ changing?" then ask "how fast is $\sin$ changing at that output?" and multiply the answers. Any composition, no matter how deeply nested, yields to the same strategy — peel off one layer at a time.

<KeyInsight>
The chain rule says: when functions compose, their rates of change multiply. The derivative of a pipeline is the product of the derivatives at each stage.
</KeyInsight>
