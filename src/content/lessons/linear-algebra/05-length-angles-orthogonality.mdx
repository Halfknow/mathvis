# Length, Angles, and Orthogonality

Last lesson, we met the dot product — a single number that measures how much two arrows agree in direction. Now let's extract two geometric ideas from it: **how long** an arrow is, and **what angle** two arrows make.

One result will feel like magic: when two arrows are perpendicular, their dot product is exactly zero. This isn't a coincidence — it's the deepest geometric fact hidden inside the dot product.

## Length from the dot product

Dot a vector with itself:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{v} \\cdot \\vec{v} = v_1^2 + v_2^2'} />

That's the square of its length. So:

<MathBlock expression={'\\lVert\\vec{v}\\rVert = \\sqrt{\\vec{v} \\cdot \\vec{v}}'} />

The length of an arrow is the square root of its self-dot-product. This is why the dot product is the foundation of all distance and measurement in vector spaces.

## The angle between two arrows

Here's the formula that connects dot product to angle:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = \\lVert\\vec{a}\\rVert \\, \\lVert\\vec{b}\\rVert \\cos\\theta'} />

Rearranging:

<MathBlock expression={'\\cos\\theta = \\frac{\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b}}{\\lVert\\vec{a}\\rVert \\, \\lVert\\vec{b}\\rVert}'} />

Three landmarks to remember:
- **Same direction** ($\theta = 0$): dot product is positive and maximal
- **Perpendicular** ($\theta = 90°$): dot product is exactly **zero**
- **Opposite directions** ($\theta = 180°$): dot product is negative and minimal

## Orthogonality — the most important special case

Two vectors are **orthogonal** when their dot product is zero. This is stronger than "kind of perpendicular" — it's a precise algebraic condition:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\perp \\vec{b} \\quad\\iff\\quad \\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = 0'} />

Why does this matter? Orthogonal vectors are "clean" — they don't interfere with each other. When you add two perpendicular arrows, the length of the result follows the Pythagorean theorem:

<MathBlock expression={'\\lVert\\vec{a} + \\vec{b}\\rVert^2 = \\lVert\\vec{a}\\rVert^2 + \\lVert\\vec{b}\\rVert^2 \\quad \\text{(when } \\vec{a} \\perp \\vec{b}\\text{)}'} />

This is no accident. Orthogonality is the geometric foundation for projection, basis decomposition, and nearly every application that follows.

## In three dimensions

Orthogonality extends naturally — the red right-angle marker shows that **a** and **b** are perpendicular in 3D too, and their dot product is zero.

<KeyInsight>
The dot product encodes both length and angle. When two arrows are perpendicular, their dot product is exactly zero — and this algebraic condition is the foundation for everything from projection to the Pythagorean theorem in higher dimensions.
</KeyInsight>

**Connections.** The cos θ = (a . b)/(‖a‖‖b‖) formula is the bridge between geometry and algebra — it lets you compute angles from coordinates. This formula underpins the entire theory of orthogonal bases (lesson 24) and the covariance matrix in PCA (lesson 29), where "angle between variables" measures their correlation.

  prompt="Vector (3, 4) has length 5 (a 3-4-5 triangle). Verify this using the dot product formula for length."
  hint="Compute the dot product of (3,4) with itself, then take the square root."
  answer="3*3 + 4*4 = 9 + 16 = 25. Square root of 25 = 5. The self-dot-product gives length squared."

<ExerciseBlock
  number={2}
  prompt="Is (1, 2) perpendicular to (-4, 2)? Check using the dot product."
  hint="Two vectors are perpendicular when their dot product is zero."
  answer="1*(-4) + 2*2 = -4 + 4 = 0. Yes, they are perpendicular. Geometrically: one points upper-right, the other points upper-left — they form a right angle."
/>

<ExerciseBlock
  number={3}
  prompt="Find a vector perpendicular to (3, 1). How many such vectors exist?"
  hint="You need a vector (x, y) such that 3x + 1y = 0."
  answer="Any vector (x, y) where y = -3x works: (1, -3), (2, -6), (-1, 3), etc. There are infinitely many — they all point along the same line (the perpendicular direction). The 'perpendicular world' of a 2D vector is always a 1D line."
/>

<ExerciseBlock
  number={4}
  prompt="Two vectors have dot product 0. What angle separates them? If you double the length of one vector, does the angle change? Why or why not?"
  hint="Dot product zero means perpendicular. What does the cos formula say when you scale one vector?"
  answer="Dot product zero means the angle is 90 degrees — they are orthogonal. Doubling one vector's length doubles both the numerator and denominator of cos θ = (a . b)/(‖a‖‖b‖), so the ratio stays the same and the angle does not change. Orthogonality depends on direction, not length."
