A point estimate gives you a single number — your best guess. But a single number carries no information about how precise that guess is. You might estimate the average height of a population as 172 cm, but is that 172 give or take 1 cm, or 172 give or take 20 cm? The answer determines whether your estimate is useful or meaningless.

A **confidence interval** gives you a range instead of a point. It says: "based on the data, the true parameter likely lives somewhere in here." The width of that range encodes your uncertainty. Narrow interval: you know a lot. Wide interval: you know little. This lesson is about how to build those ranges and what they really mean.

## The correct interpretation

A **95% confidence interval** means: if you repeated the sampling process many times, about 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true parameter.

This is subtle, and it is the most commonly misunderstood concept in statistics. The correct mental picture:

- The **parameter** (say, $\mu$) is a fixed point. It does not move. It is unknown, but it is not random.
- The **interval** is random. Each new sample produces a new interval — different center, different width.
- Over many repetitions, about 95% of those random intervals will enclose the fixed point.

The incorrect interpretation — "there is a 95% probability that the true parameter lies inside this interval" — treats the parameter as random and the interval as fixed. That reverses the correct picture. The parameter is fixed; the interval dances around it.

## The dancing intervals

Picture the true population mean $\mu$ as a vertical line drawn on a wall. You stand across the room and throw a lasso. Each throw corresponds to a new sample, and the lasso's loop is the confidence interval. Sometimes the loop lands around the vertical line — the interval catches $\mu$. Sometimes the loop lands to the left or right — the interval misses.

With a 95% confidence level, you expect to catch the line on 95 out of every 100 throws. The 5 misses are not errors in your technique — they are the inevitable consequence of random sampling.

If you could watch 100 intervals stacked vertically on the same plot, you would see most of them crossing the true $\mu$ line, with a few strays that miss entirely. Those strays are not mistakes. They are the 5%.

## The formula for a normal mean

When the population is normal with known variance $\sigma^2$, or when the sample size is large enough for the Central Limit Theorem to kick in, the confidence interval for the mean is:

$$\bar{X} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The pieces have clear geometric meanings:

- $\bar{X}$ is the center of the interval — your point estimate.
- $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ is the standard error — how much the sample mean typically wanders from the true mean.
- $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the multiplier that controls the confidence level. For 95%, $z_{0.025} \approx 1.96$. For 99%, $z_{0.005} \approx 2.576$.

The interval extends symmetrically from the estimate by an amount proportional to the standard error. The more your estimate typically wanders (large $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$), the wider the interval needs to be to catch the true value with the specified probability.

## Width, confidence, and sample size

Three forces control the width of the interval:

**Higher confidence = wider interval.** Moving from 95% to 99% confidence replaces $z = 1.96$ with $z = 2.576$. You demand that more of the lassos catch the line, so each lasso must be bigger. There is no free lunch: more confidence costs more width.

**More data = narrower interval.** The standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ shrinks as $n$ grows. The sample mean becomes a more precise estimator, and the interval tightens around it. To halve the width of the interval, you need four times as much data (because the $\sqrt{n}$ in the denominator means doubling $n$ only shrinks the width by $\sqrt{2}$).

**More variability = wider interval.** A population with large $\sigma$ produces samples that spread more, which makes the sample mean wander more, which forces the interval wider. You cannot reduce this without reducing the population variability itself.

## The t-distribution

When $\sigma$ is unknown (which is almost always the case in practice), you estimate it from the sample using $S$. This adds uncertainty on top of uncertainty. The **t-distribution** accounts for this extra uncertainty by having heavier tails than the normal.

The interval becomes:

$$\bar{X} \pm t_{\alpha/2, \, n-1} \cdot \frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The critical value $t_{\alpha/2, \, n-1}$ comes from the t-distribution with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom. For small $n$, this value is noticeably larger than the normal $z$ — the interval is wider, reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$. As $n$ grows, the t-distribution converges to the normal, and the two intervals become identical.

## Key insight

> A confidence interval is a lasso thrown at a fixed target. The target does not move — your lasso does. A 95% confidence level means the lasso catches the target on 95% of throws. You can make the lasso bigger (higher confidence) or throw more rope (more data to tighten the loop), but you can never guarantee a catch — there will always be the 5% of throws that miss.

## Practice

1. A sample of 50 light bulbs has a mean lifetime of 1020 hours with a known population standard deviation of 120 hours. Compute a 95% confidence interval for the true mean lifetime. Now compute a 99% interval. How much wider is the 99% interval? Is the extra width worth the extra confidence?

2. You want to estimate the average weight of apples from an orchard. Your scale gives you a sample mean of 150 grams with a sample standard deviation of 20 grams. How many apples do you need to weigh so that the 95% confidence interval has width at most 5 grams? (Hint: set $2 \cdot 1.96 \cdot S/\sqrt{n} \leq 5$ and solve for $n$.)

3. A researcher computes a 95% confidence interval for a mean and gets $[12.3, 15.7]$. A colleague says: "There is a 95% chance the true mean is between 12.3 and 15.7." Explain why this statement is incorrect, and give the correct interpretation using the "dancing intervals" picture.
