# Diagonalization

You've seen eigenvectors — the special directions that only stretch under a transformation. Now comes the payoff: **if you switch to a coordinate system built from eigenvectors, the matrix becomes diagonal.**

A diagonal matrix is the simplest kind: it stretches along each axis independently. No rotation, no shear — just pure stretching.

## The formula

<MathBlock expression={'A = P D P^{-1}'} />

Where:
- P is the matrix of eigenvectors (columns are the eigenvectors)
- D is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
- P inverse converts from standard coordinates to eigen-coordinates

Read this right-to-left: first change to the eigenbasis (P inverse), then stretch along each axis (D), then change back to standard basis (P).

## What diagonalization buys you

When A = PDP^(-1), matrix powers become trivial:

<MathBlock expression={'A^n = P D^n P^{-1} = P \\begin{bmatrix} \\lambda_1^n & 0 \\\\ 0 & \\lambda_2^n \\end{bmatrix} P^{-1}'} />

Computing A^100 normally requires 100 matrix multiplications. Diagonalized? Just raise two numbers to the 100th power. This is why diagonalization is the engine behind:
- Solving systems of differential equations
- Computing matrix exponentials
- Google's PageRank algorithm (power iteration)
