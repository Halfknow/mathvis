# The Determinant

When a matrix transforms space, it changes areas. The unit square stretches into a parallelogram — maybe bigger, maybe smaller, maybe collapsed flat. The **determinant** measures exactly how much the area changed.

This is the geometric meaning: det(A) is the **area scaling factor** of the transformation A.

## See the area change

Watch the orange unit square. As you change the matrix, the parallelogram's area equals the absolute value of the determinant. When the determinant hits zero, the parallelogram collapses flat — the whole plane has been squashed onto a line.

<MatrixTransform2D client:visible />

## The three cases

- **det > 1**: Space expands. A unit square becomes a bigger parallelogram.
- **0 < det < 1**: Space contracts. A unit square shrinks.
- **det = 0**: Space collapses. The entire plane squashes onto a line (or a point). The transformation loses a dimension — it's **not invertible**.
- **det < 0**: The area scaling is |det|, but the space has been **flipped** (like a mirror). The orientation reversed — what was clockwise is now counterclockwise.

## The formula

For a 2x2 matrix:

<MathBlock expression={'\\det \\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\\\ c & d \\end{bmatrix} = ad - bc'} />

Where does this come from? It's the area of the parallelogram formed by the two column vectors (a,c) and (b,d). The cross-product formula for parallelogram area gives exactly ad - bc.

### Computing 3×3 determinants

For a 3×3 matrix, the determinant measures **volume** scaling. The computation uses cofactor expansion along the first row:

<MathBlock expression={'\\det \\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\\\ d & e & f \\\\ g & h & i \\end{bmatrix} = a(ei - fh) - b(di - fg) + c(dh - eg)'} />

Each term multiplies an entry from the first row by the determinant of the 2×2 "minor" matrix that remains after removing that entry's row and column. The signs alternate: +, -, +. The geometric meaning remains the same — this formula gives the signed volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three column vectors.

### Six properties of determinants

These six properties encode everything you need to know:

1. **det(I) = 1**: The identity transformation changes no areas.
2. **Row swap flips sign**: Swapping two rows multiplies the determinant by -1. Geometrically, this reverses orientation.
3. **Row scaling**: Multiplying a row by k multiplies det by k. One axis stretched by k means areas scale by k.
4. **Row addition**: Adding a multiple of one row to another leaves det unchanged. This is why Gaussian elimination preserves the determinant (up to the row swaps).
5. **det(Aᵀ) = det(A)**: A matrix and its transpose have the same determinant. Rows and columns are symmetric.
6. **det(AB) = det(A)·det(B)**: Already covered — area scalings multiply under composition.

Property 4 is the geometric reason Gaussian elimination works: shearing the grid (adding rows) preserves area, so the determinant is a reliable invariant throughout the elimination process.

## The zero determinant is special

det(A) = 0 means the transformation **squashes space flat**. A 2D plane collapses to a line (or a point). Information is lost — different input vectors end up at the same output. You can't undo this collapse, which is why:

> A matrix is invertible if and only if det(A) ≠ 0.

## In three dimensions

In 3D, the determinant measures **volume** scaling. When det = 0, the cube collapses flat into a plane — an entire dimension of information is lost.

## Connections

The determinant connects everything: it tells you whether a matrix is invertible (det ≠ 0), how areas change under transformation (|det|), whether orientation is preserved (sign of det), and it appears in the characteristic polynomial for eigenvalues. In lesson 25, the determinant of (A - λI) = 0 is exactly how you find eigenvalues. In lesson 27, det(A) = λ₁·λ₂ connects to singular values via det(A) = ±σ₁·σ₂.

Row operations (property 4) are the computational bridge to lesson 19's null space — Gaussian elimination tracks the determinant while revealing the matrix's structure.

## The product rule

Here's a beautiful property:

<MathBlock expression={'\\det(AB) = \\det(A) \\cdot \\det(B)'} />

Geometrically: if A scales areas by 3 and B scales areas by 2, then doing A then B scales areas by 6. Area scalings multiply when you compose transformations. This fact — which takes work to prove algebraically — is almost obvious from the geometric picture.
