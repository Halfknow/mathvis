# Basis and Dimension

You've seen that some sets of vectors fill more space than others. A **basis** is the sweet spot: just enough arrows to reach everywhere, with no redundancy. Not too few (you'd be stuck), not too many (you'd have echoes).

## What is a basis?

A **basis** for a space is a set of vectors that is:

1. **Linearly independent** — every arrow contributes a genuinely new direction (no redundancy)
2. **Spans the space** — together they can reach every point

Think of it as the *most efficient coordinate system* for that space. Every point can be written as a linear combination of the basis in exactly one way. No ambiguity, no waste.

## The standard basis

In 2D, the most familiar basis is:

<MathBlock expression={'\\hat{e}_1 = \\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\\\ 0 \\end{bmatrix}, \\quad \\hat{e}_2 = \\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\\\ 1 \\end{bmatrix}'} />

One arrow points east, one points north. They're independent (different directions) and they span the plane (any point is some east + some north). This is the standard basis — the coordinate axes you've used since grade school.

But it's not the *only* basis. Any two independent vectors form a basis for 2D. (1, 2) and (3, 1) work just as well — they're a different "ruler" for the same space. Every point can still be reached, but the coordinates change depending on which basis you choose.

## Dimension

The **dimension** of a space is the number of vectors in any basis for it. It's the answer to: *how many independent directions does this space have?*

- A line through the origin has dimension **1** (one basis vector)
- The plane has dimension **2** (two basis vectors)
- 3D space has dimension **3** (three basis vectors)
- A single point (the origin) has dimension **0**

The remarkable fact: **every basis for the same space has the same number of vectors**. You can't find a basis with 2 vectors for the plane and another with 3 — independence + spanning always gives exactly the same count. This is why dimension is well-defined: it doesn't depend on which basis you pick.

A basis is the minimal spanning set — enough arrows to reach everywhere, with none to spare. Dimension is how many arrows you need.

**Connections.** A basis is the minimal set of vectors that still spans the whole space — no redundancies, no gaps. The dimension of the column space is the rank (lesson 20). The dimension of the null space is the nullity. Rank + nullity = number of columns, always. Every basis for the same space has the same number of vectors — dimension is well-defined.
