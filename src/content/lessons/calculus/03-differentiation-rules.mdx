Computing every derivative from the limit definition is tedious. Fortunately, a small set of rules lets us differentiate almost any function you'll encounter.

## The power rule

If $f(x) = x^n$, then:

$$f'(x) = n \cdot x^{n-1}$$

This works for any real exponent — positive, negative, or fractional. The power rule is the workhorse of differentiation.

**Examples:**
- $(x^3)' = 3x^2$
- $(x^{-2})' = -2x^{-3}$
- $(\sqrt{x})' = (x^{1/2})' = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}$

## The sum and constant rules

$$[cf(x)]' = c \cdot f'(x)$$
$$[f(x) + g(x)]' = f'(x) + g'(x)$$

Constants multiply through, and derivatives distribute over addition. These two rules mean you can differentiate any polynomial term-by-term.

## The product rule

$$[f(x) \cdot g(x)]' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)$$

"First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first." This is essential whenever two functions are multiplied together — like $x^2 \sin(x)$.

## The chain rule

$$[f(g(x))]' = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

The chain rule handles **composed functions** — functions nested inside other functions. You differentiate the outer function, then multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

**Example:** To differentiate $\sin(x^2)$:
- Outer: $\sin(u)$, whose derivative is $\cos(u)$
- Inner: $u = x^2$, whose derivative is $2x$
- Result: $\cos(x^2) \cdot 2x$

## The quotient rule

$$\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right]' = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}$$

While useful, the quotient rule is just the product rule applied to $f(x) \cdot [g(x)]^{-1}$ combined with the chain rule. Many people prefer to rewrite quotients as products to avoid memorizing this formula.

## Key insight

> The five rules — power, sum, product, quotient, and chain — compose. Any function built from these operations can be differentiated mechanically by applying the right rule at each step.
