Probability is the mathematics of **uncertainty**. It gives us a precise language for talking about events whose outcomes are not guaranteed — coin flips, weather, election results, stock prices.

## Three perspectives

There are three ways to think about probability, each useful in different contexts:

### 1. Classical probability

If an experiment has $n$ equally likely outcomes and $k$ of them count as "success," then:

$$P(\text{success}) = \frac{k}{n}$$

A fair die has 6 equally likely faces. The probability of rolling an even number is $3/6 = 1/2$.

### 2. Frequentist probability

If you repeat an experiment many times, the fraction of times the event occurs converges to its probability:

$$P(A) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\text{number of times } A \text{ occurs}}{n}$$

Flip a coin 10,000 times and count heads — the fraction will be very close to 0.5.

### 3. Subjective probability

Probability as a **degree of belief** — how confident you are that an event will happen, based on all available evidence. This view is essential in Bayesian statistics.

## The rules of probability

All three interpretations share the same mathematical rules:

1. $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$ for any event $A$
2. $P(\text{something happens}) = 1$
3. $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B)$
4. $P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)$

These four rules are surprisingly powerful. From them, you can derive everything from Bayes' theorem to the law of large numbers.

## Key insight

> Probability doesn't tell you what *will* happen — it tells you what's *likely* to happen, and by how much. Over many trials, those likelihoods become certainties.
