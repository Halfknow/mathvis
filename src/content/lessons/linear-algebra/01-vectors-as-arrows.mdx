# Vectors as Arrows

Before a vector is a list of numbers, it is an **arrow** — pointing from
somewhere, in some direction, with some length. Almost every theorem you'll
meet later has a geometric shadow you can see, if you train yourself to look
for it. This lesson is about training that eye.

## What an arrow knows

An arrow on the page captures two pieces of information at once: a
**direction** and a **magnitude**. That's it. It does not remember where
its tail starts — and this freedom is what gives vectors their power.

### The zero vector

There is one arrow that breaks the rule. The **zero vector** has no direction at all — its tip and tail are the same point. It is the only vector that cannot point somewhere, because "somewhere" requires a direction, and the zero vector has none.

This might seem like a technicality, but the zero vector is the additive identity of the vector space: adding it to any vector leaves that vector unchanged, just as adding zero to a number does nothing. It is the "do nothing" displacement — you walk zero steps in zero directions and end up exactly where you started.

## Unit vectors

Every nonzero vector v hides a direction and a magnitude inside it. **Dividing by length** extracts the direction alone:

The unit vector û = v/‖v‖ points in the same direction as v but has length exactly 1. It's the "pure direction" extracted from v — all direction, no magnitude.

Why does this matter? Because many formulas simplify when vectors have length 1. The dot product of two unit vectors gives you cos θ directly — no division by lengths needed. Projections, reflections, and rotations all become cleaner with unit vectors.

Think of it this way: v carries *how far* and *which way*. û carries only *which way*. When you need to compare directions without being distracted by magnitude, normalize first.

### Opposite vectors

For every arrow, there is an arrow pointing the exact opposite way with the same length. This is the **negative** of the vector: -v has the same magnitude but points in the opposite direction. If v is "walk 3 east and 2 north," then -v is "walk 3 west and 2 south."

Geometrically, negating a vector rotates it 180 degrees around its tail. The direction flips, the length stays the same. Watch what happens when you click the "Opposite" preset above — the vector pivots to face the other way.

## Connections

The length ‖v‖ you just met is the **L2 norm** — the default way to measure "how long." In lesson 6, you'll see that there are other norms (L1, L∞) that measure length differently, each revealing a different geometry. The choice of norm changes what "close" means — and that choice matters in machine learning, where L1 regularization produces sparse solutions while L2 produces smooth ones.

The unit vector û = v/‖v‖ is the foundation for projection (lesson 10) and Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization (lesson 24). Every orthonormal basis is built from unit vectors.

## When coordinates enter

With a starting point fixed at the origin, the arrow's tip lands at its components:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{v} = \\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\\\ 1 \\end{bmatrix}'} />

The first number tells you how far east (positive) or west (negative). The second tells you how far north (positive) or south (negative). So the vector (3, 2) points northeast — mostly east, slightly north — while (-1, 3) points northwest — slightly west, strongly north. Both are in the upper half of the plane, but their *character* is completely different: one chases the horizon, the other climbs toward the sky.

Notice the order: **geometry first, numbers second**.
