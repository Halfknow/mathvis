# Applications and Connections

You've built the geometric intuition. Now let's see where it lives in the wild. Every concept we studied — vectors, transformations, eigenvectors, dot products — shows up in the real world doing real work. The spatial pictures you've built are not metaphors; they're the actual mechanism.

## Data science — PCA

**Principal Component Analysis** is just eigenvectors in disguise. Given a cloud of data points in high dimensions:

1. Compute the **covariance matrix** (measures how variables co-vary)
2. Find its **eigenvectors** — these are the principal components, the directions of maximum variation in the data
3. The **eigenvalues** tell you how much variation each component captures

Geometrically: PCA rotates the coordinate system so the new axes align with the natural "spread" of the data. The eigenvectors are the skeleton of the data cloud. Dimensionality reduction is just projecting onto the most important eigen-directions.

Recall the eigenvector visualizer — those special directions that don't rotate under a transformation? In PCA, those directions are the "principal components" of your data.

## Computer graphics — transformations

Every 3D object on your screen is a collection of vectors (vertices). Every frame, matrices act on them:

- **Model matrix**: positions the object in the world (translate, rotate, scale)
- **View matrix**: positions the camera (another transformation)
- **Projection matrix**: flattens 3D onto 2D (the screen)

Three matrix multiplications compose into one. The GPU does millions of these per frame. And the determinant of the projection matrix determines whether the object is visible at all (frustum culling).

## Machine learning — the dot product everywhere

Neural networks are chains of matrix multiplications interspersed with nonlinearities. At their core:

- **Linear layers**: y = Wx + b — a matrix transformation followed by a shift
- **Attention**: queries and keys are vectors; attention weights are **softmax of dot products** — "how aligned is this query with that key?"
- **Word embeddings**: words live in a vector space; similar words are nearby. "King - Man + Woman ≈ Queen" works because the vector arithmetic preserves semantic relationships.

The dot product, which you now see as a measure of alignment, is the fundamental operation underlying modern AI.

## Quantum mechanics — eigenvalues as observables

In quantum mechanics:

- States are **vectors** in a complex vector space (Hilbert space)
- Observables (energy, momentum, spin) are **Hermitian matrices** — transformations with special symmetry
- The possible measurement outcomes are the **eigenvalues** of these matrices
- After measurement, the state **collapses to an eigenvector**

The eigenvector equation Av = λv isn't just abstract algebra — it's the equation that connects the mathematical formalism to physical reality. The eigenvalues are the only numbers you can actually measure.

## Differential equations — eigenvectors as solutions

A system dx/dt = Ax (where A is a matrix) has solutions built from eigenvectors:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{x}(t) = c_1 e^{\\lambda_1 t} \\vec{v}_1 + c_2 e^{\\lambda_2 t} \\vec{v}_2'} />

Each eigenvector evolves independently, growing or decaying exponentially at a rate determined by its eigenvalue. The geometric picture: the system stretches or shrinks along the eigen-directions, and these directions don't mix.

## SVD and image compression

A 1000×1000 grayscale image is a matrix of pixel values. Its SVD has 1000 singular values. The largest few capture the most important visual structure — edges, gradients, shapes. The smallest singular values encode fine-grained noise that's often invisible to the eye.

Keeping the top 50 singular values (a truncated SVD of rank 50) typically preserves 90%+ of visual quality while reducing storage from 1,000,000 entries to 50 × (1000 + 1000 + 1) ≈ 100,050 — a 10× compression. This is how JPEG compression works in principle (though JPEG uses a different basis — the discrete cosine transform).

## PageRank as eigenvectors

Google's PageRank algorithm is an eigenvector problem in disguise. The web is a directed graph: pages link to other pages. Build a stochastic matrix M where Mᵢⱼ = 1/(out-links from j) if j links to i. The steady-state distribution π satisfies Mπ = π — π is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 1.

The eigenvalue 1 always exists for stochastic matrices. The corresponding eigenvector tells you the long-term probability of being on each page — which is exactly the "importance" ranking. The power method (repeatedly multiply by M) converges to this eigenvector, which is how Google originally computed rankings.
