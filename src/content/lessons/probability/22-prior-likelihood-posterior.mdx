Bayesian inference is a machine with three moving parts. You pour in a **prior** (what you believed before), feed it some **data** through the **likelihood** (how well each possible world predicts that data), and out comes a **posterior** (what you believe now). The machine is Bayes' theorem, and it never stops — every new batch of data turns today's posterior into tomorrow's prior.

This lesson is about the three parts and how they interact. The goal is to see, geometrically, how data reshapes belief.

## The Bayesian update

The core equation, written in the language of distributions:

$$p(\theta \mid \text{data}) = \frac{p(\text{data} \mid \theta) \cdot p(\theta)}{p(\text{data})}$$

Or more compactly, since $p(\text{data})$ is just a normalizing constant:

$$\text{posterior} \propto \text{likelihood} \times \text{prior}$$

The symbol $\propto$ means "proportional to." The posterior has the same *shape* as the product of the likelihood and the prior — the denominator just scales it so the total area is 1.

Each piece has a distinct role.

## The prior $p(\theta)$

The prior is your belief about the parameter $\theta$ *before* you see any data. It is a probability distribution over the space of possible $\theta$ values.

Picture a landscape over the number line. Hills are where you think $\theta$ is likely to be. Valleys are where you think it is not. A **wide, flat** prior says "I have almost no idea — $\theta$ could be anywhere." A **narrow, peaked** prior says "I have strong reason to believe $\theta$ is near this value."

The prior encodes everything you knew before this experiment. It can come from previous studies, from physical constraints, or from expert judgment. In the absence of any information, you can use a **non-informative prior** — a flat or nearly flat distribution that tries not to bias the result.

A common misconception: the prior is a weakness of Bayesian methods because it is "subjective." In reality, every statistical analysis makes assumptions. The Bayesian simply writes them down explicitly, as a distribution, where they can be examined, criticized, and changed.

## The likelihood $p(\text{data} \mid \theta)$

The likelihood is a function of $\theta$, not of the data. The data is already observed — it is fixed. What varies is $\theta$: for each possible value of $\theta$, how probable is the data you actually saw?

Picture the same number line, now overlaid with a second curve. This curve peaks at the value of $\theta$ that makes the data *most likely* — the **maximum likelihood estimate** (MLE). The curve falls off on either side as $\theta$ moves away from the best explanation.

The likelihood is where the data speaks. It is the bridge between your model and the evidence. If the data is strongly informative, the likelihood curve is sharply peaked — only a narrow range of $\theta$ values are consistent with what you observed. If the data is weak, the likelihood is broad and shallow.

Note: the likelihood is *not* a probability distribution over $\theta$. Its area does not integrate to 1, and it does not necessarily sum to anything meaningful. It is a **relative** measure — it tells you which values of $\theta$ are more or less compatible with the data, but it does not assign probabilities to them. That is the prior's job.

## The posterior $p(\theta \mid \text{data})$

The posterior is your updated belief — the prior reshaped by the evidence. It is a full probability distribution over $\theta$, and it is the main output of Bayesian inference.

The shape of the posterior is the product of the prior and the likelihood (normalized). This product has a clear geometric interpretation:

- Where the prior and the likelihood **both** have mass (both curves are high), the posterior peaks. The data and your prior agree: this is where $\theta$ probably lives.
- Where the prior is high but the likelihood is low, the posterior shrinks. Your prior liked this region, but the data does not support it.
- Where the likelihood is high but the prior is low, the posterior is moderate. The data points here, but your prior was skeptical.
- Where both are low, the posterior is negligible. Neither your prior nor the data find this region plausible.

The posterior is a **compromise** between the prior and the likelihood, but not a symmetric one. It is a product, so it is zero wherever *either* factor is zero. A prior that assigns exactly zero probability to some value of $\theta$ can never be overcome by any amount of data — the product is zero. This is why Bayesians are careful to use priors with broad support.

## The tug-of-war: data versus prior

The relative influence of the prior and the likelihood depends on how much data you have.

**Little data.** With only a handful of observations, the likelihood is broad and shallow. It does not strongly favor any particular $\theta$. The posterior looks mostly like the prior — gently nudged, but not reshaped. Your initial belief dominates because the data has not yet spoken loudly enough to override it.

**Moderate data.** The likelihood starts to sharpen. It pulls the posterior away from the prior and toward the MLE. The posterior sits between the two, a weighted average where the weights depend on the precision (inverse variance) of each. If the prior is tight and the data is moderate, the posterior stays close to the prior. If the prior is vague and the data is moderate, the posterior follows the data.

**Lots of data.** The likelihood becomes a narrow spike. The product of a spike and a smooth hill is essentially just the spike — the prior barely matters anymore. The posterior concentrates tightly around the MLE, and any reasonable prior would have led to the same conclusion. **The data overwhelms the prior.**

This is the reassuring property of Bayesian inference. Get enough data, and your starting beliefs wash out. Two analysts with different priors will converge to the same posterior as the data accumulates. The method is *eventually* objective, even if the starting point is subjective.

## A visual sketch

Imagine three curves drawn on the same axes, all over the parameter $\theta$:

1. **Prior** — a wide, gentle hill centered at 0.4. You started with a mild preference for values below 0.5.
2. **Likelihood** — a sharper peak centered at 0.65. The data you collected points to a higher value.
3. **Posterior** — a peak sitting at roughly 0.58, between the prior and the likelihood, but closer to the likelihood because the data was moderately informative.

Now imagine collecting ten times more data. The likelihood sharpens into a narrow needle at 0.65. The prior has not changed — still the same gentle hill at 0.4. But the posterior now sits almost exactly at 0.65, a thin spike. The prior's opinion has been overwhelmed.

## Key insight

> The posterior is the product of prior and likelihood — your initial belief multiplied by what the data says. With little data, the prior dominates. With lots of data, the likelihood dominates. Bayesian inference is not about imposing subjective opinion; it is about starting from somewhere reasonable and letting the evidence take over.

## Practice

**Exercise 1.** You have a Beta(2, 5) prior for a coin's probability of heads. You flip the coin 10 times and see 7 heads. Without computing the exact posterior, sketch (or describe) where you expect the posterior to peak relative to the prior's peak (at roughly 0.29) and the likelihood's peak (at 0.7). Will it be closer to 0.29 or 0.7?

**Exercise 2.** A colleague uses a prior that assigns probability zero to all values of $\theta$ above 0.5. The data strongly suggests $\theta \approx 0.8$. What happens to the posterior? Explain why this is a problem and what the colleague should have done differently.

**Exercise 3.** Two analysts study the same drug. Analyst A uses a tight prior centered at 0.3 (skeptical the drug works). Analyst B uses a broad, nearly flat prior. They both observe the same data, in which 45 out of 100 patients recover. Describe how their posteriors will differ, and explain what would happen if they each observed 4500 recoveries out of 10,000 patients instead.
