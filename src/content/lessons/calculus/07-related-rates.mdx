## The setup

Related rates problems always have the same structure:

1. Find an equation relating the two quantities.
2. Differentiate **both sides** with respect to time $t$ (using the chain rule).
3. Plug in the known rate and solve for the unknown rate.

The key insight that makes this work: **all variables share the same clock $t$**. The radius $r$ and the volume $V$ are not independent — they're both functions of time, tied together by the geometry. When $t$ ticks forward, every variable moves in lockstep, and the chain rule captures those coupled motions.

The chain rule is the engine here. When you differentiate an equation like $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ with respect to $t$, every $r$ becomes $r(t)$ and picks up a factor of $dr/dt$. The chain rule creates a web of connections: when one variable moves, it pulls on all the others through the geometric relationship.

## Example: the inflating balloon

A spherical balloon is inflated at a rate of $dV/dt = 100$ cm³/s. How fast is the radius increasing when $r = 5$ cm?

The relationship: $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$.

Differentiate both sides with respect to $t$:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{dV}{dt} = 4\\pi r^2 \\cdot \\frac{dr}{dt}"} />

Plug in: $100 = 4\pi(25) \cdot \frac{dr}{dt}$, so:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{dr}{dt} = \\frac{100}{100\\pi} = \\frac{1}{\\pi} \\approx 0.318 \\text{ cm/s}"} />

## Example: the sliding ladder

A 10-foot ladder leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at 1 ft/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 6 feet from the wall?

The relationship (Pythagorean theorem): $x^2 + y^2 = 100$.

Differentiate: $2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0$.

At $x = 6$: $y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8$. So:

<MathBlock expression={"12 \\cdot 1 + 16 \\cdot \\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \\implies \\frac{dy}{dt} = -\\frac{12}{16} = -0.75 \\text{ ft/s}"} />

The top slides down at 0.75 ft/s. The negative sign means $y$ is decreasing.

## The pattern

In every related rates problem, the chain rule creates a link: the rate of change of one quantity depends on the current value of the other quantity. This is why related rates problems always ask for the rate "at the moment when..." — the answer depends on the specific state of the system.

<KeyInsight>
Related rates problems are the chain rule applied to geometric relationships. Differentiate the relationship with respect to time, plug in the known rate, and solve for the unknown rate.
</KeyInsight>

## Practice

<ExerciseBlock number={1}
  prompt="A circle's radius increases at 2 cm/s. How fast is the area increasing when r = 5 cm?"
  hint="The relationship is A = πr². Differentiate both sides with respect to t."
  answer="dA/dt = 2πr · dr/dt = 2π(5)(2) = 20π ≈ 62.8 cm²/s."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={2}
  prompt="Water pours into a cone (tip pointing down) at 3 m³/min. The cone has height 10 m and base radius 5 m. How fast is the water level rising when the depth is 4 m?"
  hint="The cone formula is V = (1/3)πr²h. Use similar triangles to relate r and h: r/h = 5/10 = 1/2, so r = h/2."
  answer="Substituting r = h/2: V = (π/12)h³. Differentiating: dV/dt = (π/4)h² · dh/dt. At h = 4: 3 = (π/4)(16) · dh/dt, so dh/dt = 3/(4π) ≈ 0.239 m/min."
/>

<ExerciseBlock number={3}
  prompt="Two cars leave an intersection. Car A heads north at 60 km/h; Car B heads east at 80 km/h. How fast is the distance between them increasing after 2 hours?"
  hint="Let x = B's distance east, y = A's distance north, d = distance between them. Then d² = x² + y²."
  answer="After 2 hours: x = 160, y = 120, d = 200. Differentiating: d · dd/dt = x · dx/dt + y · dy/dt. So 200 · dd/dt = 160(80) + 120(60) = 20000. dd/dt = 100 km/h."
/>
