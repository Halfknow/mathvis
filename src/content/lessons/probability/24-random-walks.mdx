Imagine a drunkard stumbling home. Each step goes either left or right, chosen by a coin flip. The path zigzags — no direction, no plan, no memory of where it has been. And yet, if you watch a thousand such drunkards, a shape emerges. The crowd spreads outward from the starting point like an ink drop in water. Not uniformly, but as a soft bell curve widening with time.

This is the **random walk**, the simplest stochastic process and one of the most important models in all of mathematics. It captures a deep truth: individual randomness is chaotic, but collective randomness is structured.

## The setup

Start at position $S_0 = 0$. At each step, add $+1$ or $-1$ with equal probability:

$$S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$$

where each $X_i$ is independent, and $P(X_i = +1) = P(X_i = -1) = 1/2$.

The position after $n$ steps is a random variable. Its value bounces around unpredictably from one realization to the next. But its statistical properties are remarkably clean.

## Expected position and expected distance

The expected position after $n$ steps is:

$$E[S_n] = E[X_1] + \cdots + E[X_n] = 0$$

The walk has no drift. On average, you go nowhere. The coin flips cancel out. But "expected position" and "expected distance from the origin" are very different things.

The variance grows linearly:

<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(S_n) = n"} />

so the standard deviation is <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} />. The typical distance from the origin after $n$ steps is about <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} />, not $n$. After 10,000 steps, the walker is typically about 100 steps from the start — not 10,000.

### Theorem: The √n diffusion law

For a simple symmetric random walk with $n$ steps of $\pm 1$:

$$E[S_n] = 0, \qquad \text{Var}(S_n) = n, \qquad \text{SD}(S_n) = \sqrt{n}$$

Furthermore, the expected absolute distance from the origin is <MathInline expression={"E[|S_n|] \\approx \\sqrt{2n/\\pi}"} /> (exact in the Brownian limit). The walk spreads like the square root of time, not linearly. Doubling the number of steps increases the spread by only <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{2} \\approx 1.41"} />.

*Proof of the variance.* Each step $X_i$ has $E[X_i] = 0$ and $E[X_i^2] = 1$ (since $X_i = \pm 1$). By independence, <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(S_n) = \\sum \\text{Var}(X_i) = n"} />. The standard deviation is the square root: <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} />.

This is the signature of **diffusion**. The spread grows like the square root of time. It is slow. If you double the number of steps, the spread increases by only a factor of <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{2} \\approx 1.41"} />. The walk fans out, but reluctantly.

Picture it: a bundle of a hundred paths all starting at the same point. At step 10, they form a narrow cloud. At step 100, the cloud has widened. At step 10,000, the cloud is wide and soft, shaped like a bell, with most paths still clustered within a few hundred steps of the origin. A few adventurous paths have wandered far, but they are the exception.

## The scaling law

The relationship <MathInline expression={"E[|S_n|] \\approx \\sqrt{n}"} /> is a scaling law, and it appears everywhere in nature:

- **Brownian motion**: a pollen grain in water, jostled by invisible molecules. Its displacement scales as <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{t}"} />.
- **Stock prices**: in the simplest model (geometric random walk), price fluctuations over a time horizon $t$ have typical size proportional to <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{t}"} />. This is why short-term trading is noise-dominated.
- **Polymer chains**: a long molecule floating in solution folds back on itself. The end-to-end distance of a polymer with $n$ segments scales as <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} />.
- **Diffusion of heat**: a drop of dye in still water spreads outward. The radius of the colored region grows as <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{t}"} />.

All of these are random walks in disguise.

## The gambler's ruin

Consider a random walk with **absorbing barriers**. A gambler starts with $a$ dollars and plays against a casino with $b$ dollars. Each round, the gambler wins or loses $1 with equal probability. The game ends when one side goes broke.

The question: what is the probability that the gambler walks away with all the money?

### Theorem: Gambler's ruin (fair game)

For a fair game ($p = q = 1/2$) with the gambler starting at $a$ and the casino at $b$ (total $N = a + b$), the probability the gambler wins all $N$ dollars is:

$$P(\text{gambler wins}) = \frac{a}{a+b}$$

and the probability of ruin is $b/(a+b)$.

*Proof idea.* Let $p_i$ be the probability of winning (reaching $N$) starting from fortune $i$. Then <MathInline expression={"p_i = \\tfrac{1}{2}p_{i+1} + \\tfrac{1}{2}p_{i-1}"} /> (condition on the next step), with boundary conditions $p_0 = 0$ (ruin) and $p_N = 1$ (win). The solution to this difference equation is linear: $p_i = i/N$. Setting $i = a$ gives $p_a = a/(a+b)$.

If the gambler has $10 and the casino has $90, the gambler wins with probability $10/100 = 0.1$. The probability of ruin is proportional to the size of the adversary's bankroll. A random walk with finite barriers always hits a barrier eventually — there is no strategy, no lucky streak, that can hold out forever against a larger opponent.

This is why casinos do not need to cheat. They just need a bigger bankroll. The random walk does the rest.

### The unfair game

If the gambler has a slight disadvantage — say <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{win}) = 0.49"} /> and <MathInline expression={"P(\\text{loss}) = 0.51"} /> — the ruin probability against an effectively infinite casino is:

$$P(\text{ruin}) \approx 1 - \left(\frac{p}{q}\right)^a = 1 - \left(\frac{0.49}{0.51}\right)^a$$

For $a = 100$, this is <MathInline expression={"1 - (0.49/0.51)^{100} \\approx 1 - 0.018 \\approx 0.982"} /> — a 98.2% chance of ruin. Even a 1% edge, compounded over thousands of rounds, drives the ruin probability to essentially 1. The house always wins.

## The reflection principle

One of the most elegant tools for analyzing random walks is the **reflection principle**. It answers questions about the *maximum* of a walk by reflecting paths across a barrier.

### Theorem: The reflection principle

For a symmetric random walk, the number of paths from $0$ to some level $a > 0$ in $n$ steps that ever touch or exceed $a + k$ (with $k > 0$) equals the number of paths from $0$ to $a + 2k$ in $n$ steps. Equivalently, for the maximum of the walk:

<MathInline expression={"P\\!\\left(\\max_{1 \\le k \\le n} S_k \\ge a,\\; S_n = b\\right) = P(S_n = 2a - b)"} />

for $b \leq a$. Geometrically: take any path that reaches $a$ and ends at $b \leq a$. Reflect the portion of the path after the first hitting time of $a$ across the level $a$. The reflected path ends at $2a - b$. This reflection is a bijection, so the two sets of paths have equal cardinality.

The reflection principle turns "what is the probability the walk ever went above $a$?" into "what is the probability the walk ended above $a$?" — a much easier question. It is the key tool behind the ballot theorem, the distribution of the maximum of Brownian motion, and barrier option pricing in finance.

## Recurrence in one dimension

A beautiful fact about the one-dimensional random walk: the walker returns to the origin **with probability 1**. Not just once, but infinitely many times. The walk is **recurrent** — it cannot escape its starting point forever. It will always come back.

### Theorem: Polya's recurrence theorem

The simple symmetric random walk on the $d$-dimensional integer lattice is **recurrent** (returns to the origin with probability 1) for $d = 1$ and $d = 2$, and **transient** (positive probability of never returning) for $d \geq 3$.

- In $d = 1$: the walker returns to the origin infinitely often, with probability 1.
- In $d = 2$: also recurrent — a 2D random walk on the lattice returns to the origin with probability 1.
- In $d \geq 3$: transient. There is a positive probability the walk wanders off to infinity and never returns.

The transition happens because in three dimensions, there is simply too much room to get lost. The surface area of a sphere grows as <MathInline expression={"r^{d-1}"} />, so the probability of stumbling back to the origin thins out faster than the walk can compensate. A drunk man will find his way home; a drunk bird might not. (This is the origin of Sidney Port's famous quip: "A drunk man will find his way home, but a drunk bird may get lost forever.")

## From random walk to Brownian motion

If you speed up the random walk — take smaller steps, more frequently — and pass to the continuous limit, you arrive at **Brownian motion** (also called the Wiener process). This is the continuous-time stochastic process <MathInline expression={"W(t)"} /> defined by:

- $W(0) = 0$.
- **Independent increments**: $W(t) - W(s)$ is independent of the past for $s < t$.
- **Gaussian increments**: <MathInline expression={"W(t) - W(s) \\sim N(0,\\, t-s)"} /> — the increment over an interval of length $\Delta t$ is normal with variance $\Delta t$.
- **Continuous paths**: $W(t)$ is a continuous function of $t$ (almost surely), though nowhere differentiable.

Brownian motion is the scaling limit of the random walk: if <MathInline expression={"S_n"} /> is a random walk with step size <MathInline expression={"1/\\sqrt{n}"} /> run for $nt$ steps, then <MathInline expression={"S_{nt}/\\sqrt{n} \\to W(t)"} /> as $n \to \infty$. The <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} /> diffusion law becomes the exact statement <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(W(t)) = t"} />.

This is why Brownian motion appears as the continuous limit in physics (Einstein's 1905 model of Brownian motion established the existence of atoms), finance (the Black-Scholes option pricing model treats stock prices as geometric Brownian motion), and pure mathematics (it is the canonical example of a continuous martingale). The random walk is its discrete skeleton.

## Three perspectives on the random walk

1. **As a sum of independent increments.** <MathInline expression={"S_n = \\sum X_i"} /> with $X_i$ i.i.d. — this is the most elementary view. Linearity of expectation gives $E[S_n] = 0$; independence gives <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(S_n) = n"} />; the Central Limit Theorem implies <MathInline expression={"S_n/\\sqrt{n} \\to N(0,1)"} />. The walk is a textbook case of [lesson 06](/courses/probability/06-expectation-and-variance) and the CLT ([lesson 16](/courses/probability/16-central-limit-theorem)) in action.
2. **As a Markov chain.** The position $S_n$ depends only on <MathInline expression={"S_{n-1}"} /> — the next state is the current state plus or minus 1. This makes the random walk the simplest nontrivial Markov chain (see [lesson 25](/courses/probability/25-markov-chains)). The Markov property is what lets us analyze hitting times, recurrence, and gambler's ruin via difference equations.
3. **As a discrete approximation to Brownian motion.** Speed up the walk, shrink the steps, and you get the Wiener process. This is why the <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} /> scaling law, the reflection principle, and the gambler's ruin formula all have continuous-time analogs in Brownian motion — and why Brownian motion is the foundation of continuous-time finance and stochastic differential equations.

## Modern applications

**Diffusion models in generative AI.** The breakthrough behind modern image generators (DALL-E, Stable Diffusion) is the **diffusion process**: gradually add Gaussian noise to an image until it is pure static (a random walk in pixel space), then train a neural network to reverse the process. The forward noise is a random walk in the space of images; the reverse denoising recovers realistic images from pure noise. The mathematics — Langevin dynamics, score matching, stochastic differential equations — is built directly on the random walk and Brownian motion theory. The <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} /> diffusion law governs how fast the noise destroys structure, and the reverse process learns to "un-walk" the path.

**Quantitative finance.** The Black-Scholes option pricing model treats stock prices as geometric Brownian motion — a continuous-time random walk on a logarithmic scale. The model's core assumption is that log-returns are independent and normally distributed with variance proportional to the time horizon (exactly the <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{t}"} /> law). Every option price, every risk calculation, every portfolio optimization in modern finance rests on this random-walk foundation. The reflection principle directly prices barrier options (options that activate or expire when the underlying crosses a threshold).

**Reinforcement learning and Monte Carlo methods.** Random walks are the simplest example of a Markov chain, and Markov chains underpin reinforcement learning (the agent's trajectory through state space) and Monte Carlo integration (random sampling to estimate high-dimensional integrals). The gambler's ruin formula appears in bandit algorithms and betting strategies; the recurrence theorem informs the design of random-walk-based graph algorithms (PageRank, random-walk image segmentation).

<KeyInsight>
A random walk spreads like the square root of the number of steps, not linearly — this is the universal diffusion law. Individual paths are wild and unpredictable, but the envelope of a thousand paths forms a smooth bell curve widening as root-n. The reflection principle turns questions about maxima into questions about endpoints; gambler's ruin shows that a fair game still dooms the smaller bankroll; and Polya's theorem reveals that recurrence holds in dimensions 1 and 2 but fails in 3 and up. In the continuous limit, the random walk becomes Brownian motion — the foundation of modern finance and generative AI.

<ExerciseBlock number={6}
  prompt="Describe how the random walk connects to Brownian motion via the scaling limit. What happens to the step size and the time step as n -> infinity? Why does the result have continuous but nowhere-differentiable paths?"
  hint="Scale step size as 1/sqrt(n) and take n*dt = t steps. The position S_{nt}/sqrt(n) converges to W(t)."
  answer="To get Brownian motion as the limit of a random walk: take steps of size delta = 1/sqrt(n) (shrinking) at time intervals dt = 1/n (speeding up), so that after nt steps (total time t), the position is S_{nt} * (1/sqrt(n)). The variance is Var(S_{nt}) * (1/n) = (nt)*(1/n) = t, matching Brownian motion's Var(W(t)) = t. By the CLT, the increments converge to Gaussian, and Donsker's theorem (the functional CLT) makes this rigorous: S_{nt}/sqrt(n) converges in distribution to W(t). The resulting paths are continuous (infinitesimal steps shrink to zero) but nowhere differentiable: the 'velocity' (S_{t+dt} - S_t)/dt has variance (sqrt(dt))^2/dt = 1/dt -> infinity as dt -> 0, so the instantaneous slope does not converge anywhere. Brownian motion is continuous but has infinite variation on every interval — it is 'rougher' than any differentiable curve. This roughness is exactly what makes stochastic calculus (Ito calculus) necessary: ordinary calculus rules fail because the paths are too jagged."
