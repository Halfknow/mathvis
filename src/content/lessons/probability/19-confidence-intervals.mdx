A point estimate gives you a single number — your best guess. But a single number carries no information about how precise that guess is. You might estimate the average height of a population as 172 cm, but is that 172 give or take 1 cm, or 172 give or take 20 cm? The answer determines whether your estimate is useful or meaningless.

A **confidence interval** gives you a range instead of a point. It says: "based on the data, the true parameter likely lives somewhere in here." The width of that range encodes your uncertainty. Narrow interval: you know a lot. Wide interval: you know little. This lesson is about how to build those ranges, what they really mean, and the crucial distinction between the **z-interval** (when you know the population variance) and the **t-interval** (when you must estimate it from small samples).

The most important thing you will learn is the *correct interpretation* of a confidence interval — because the common-sense reading ("there is a 95% probability the true value is in this interval") is subtly but critically wrong. Getting the interpretation right is what separates a statistician from someone who has merely memorized a formula.

## The correct interpretation

A **95% confidence interval** means: if you repeated the sampling process many times, about 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true parameter.

This is subtle, and it is the most commonly misunderstood concept in statistics. The correct mental picture:

- The **parameter** (say, $\mu$) is a fixed point. It does not move. It is unknown, but it is not random.
- The **interval** is random. Each new sample produces a new interval — different center, different width.
- Over many repetitions, about 95% of those random intervals will enclose the fixed point.

The incorrect interpretation — "there is a 95% probability that the true parameter lies inside this interval" — treats the parameter as random and the interval as fixed. That reverses the correct picture. The parameter is fixed; the interval dances around it. Once you have computed a specific interval, say $[170, 174]$, the true mean $\mu$ is either in it or not — there is no probability left. The 95% refers to the *procedure* that generated the interval, not to any single interval.

### Theorem: the confidence interval for a normal mean (known variance)

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and known variance $\sigma^2$ (or $n$ large enough for the CLT). Then a $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$ is:

$$\bar{X} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of the standard normal (for 95%, $z_{0.025} \approx 1.96$).

*Proof (sketch).* By the CLT, <MathInline expression={"(\\bar{X} - \\mu)/(\\sigma/\\sqrt{n}) \\approx N(0,1)"} />. So $P(-z_{\alpha/2} \leq (\bar{X}-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n}) \leq z_{\alpha/2}) \approx 1 - \alpha$. Rearranging the inequality to isolate $\mu$ gives the interval.

## The dancing intervals

Picture the true population mean $\mu$ as a vertical line drawn on a wall. You stand across the room and throw a lasso. Each throw corresponds to a new sample, and the lasso's loop is the confidence interval. Sometimes the loop lands around the vertical line — the interval catches $\mu$. Sometimes the loop lands to the left or right — the interval misses.

With a 95% confidence level, you expect to catch the line on 95 out of every 100 throws. The 5 misses are not errors in your technique — they are the inevitable consequence of random sampling.

If you could watch 100 intervals stacked vertically on the same plot, you would see most of them crossing the true $\mu$ line, with a few strays that miss entirely. Those strays are not mistakes. They are the 5%.

## The formula for a normal mean

When the population is normal with known variance $\sigma^2$, or when the sample size is large enough for the Central Limit Theorem to kick in, the confidence interval for the mean is:

$<MathInline expression={"\\bar{X} \\pm z_{\\alpha/2} \\cdot \\frac{\\sigma}{\\sqrt{n}}"} />$

The pieces have clear geometric meanings:

- <MathInline expression={"\\bar{X}"} /> is the center of the interval — your point estimate.
- <MathInline expression={"\\sigma/\\sqrt{n}"} /> is the **standard error** — how much the sample mean typically wanders from the true mean (this is the $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ from the CLT).
- <MathInline expression={"z_{\\alpha/2}"} /> is the multiplier that controls the confidence level. For 95%, <MathInline expression={"z_{0.025} \\approx 1.96"} />. For 99%, <MathInline expression={"z_{0.005} \\approx 2.576"} />.

The interval extends symmetrically from the estimate by an amount proportional to the standard error. The more your estimate typically wanders (large <MathInline expression={"\\sigma/\\sqrt{n}"} />), the wider the interval needs to be to catch the true value with the specified probability.

## Width, confidence, and sample size

Three forces control the width of the interval:

**Higher confidence = wider interval.** Moving from 95% to 99% confidence replaces $z = 1.96$ with $z = 2.576$. You demand that more of the lassos catch the line, so each lasso must be bigger. There is no free lunch: more confidence costs more width.

**More data = narrower interval.** The standard error <MathInline expression={"\\sigma/\\sqrt{n}"} /> shrinks as $n$ grows. The sample mean becomes a more precise estimator, and the interval tightens around it. To halve the width of the interval, you need four times as much data (because the <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{n}"} /> in the denominator means doubling $n$ only shrinks the width by <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{2}"} />).

**More variability = wider interval.** A population with large $\sigma$ produces samples that spread more, which makes the sample mean wander more, which forces the interval wider. You cannot reduce this without reducing the population variability itself.

### Sample size determination

The width formula lets you *solve for* the sample size you need. If you want the margin of error <MathInline expression={"E = z_{\\alpha/2} \\cdot \\sigma/\\sqrt{n}"} /> to be at most $E_0$, solve for $n$:

$<MathInline expression={"n \\geq \\left(\\frac{z_{\\alpha/2} \\cdot \\sigma}{E_0}\\right)^2"} />$

This is the calculation behind every clinical trial (how many patients to enroll), every political poll (how many voters to survey), and every engineering tolerance study. The $\sigma^2$ in the numerator is why higher-variability populations need larger samples; the $E_0^2$ in the denominator is why tighter precision demands quadratically more data.

## The t-distribution: small samples and unknown variance

In practice, you almost never know the population variance $\sigma^2$. You must estimate it from the sample using $S$. This adds a second layer of uncertainty — you are not only uncertain about $\mu$, you are also uncertain about $\sigma$. The **t-distribution** accounts for this extra uncertainty by having heavier tails than the normal. It was discovered by William Sealy Gosset in 1908, who published under the pen name "Student" because his employer (the Guinness brewery) forbade employees from publishing.

The interval becomes:

$<MathInline expression={"\\bar{X} \\pm t_{\\alpha/2, \\, n-1} \\cdot \\frac{S}{\\sqrt{n}}"} />$

The critical value <MathInline expression={"t_{\\alpha/2, \\, n-1}"} /> comes from the **t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom**. For small $n$, this value is noticeably larger than the normal $z$ — the interval is wider, reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$. As $n$ grows, the t-distribution converges to the normal, and the two intervals become identical.

### Theorem: the t-distribution and the t-interval

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with both parameters unknown. Define:

$$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}}$$

Then $T$ follows a **t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom**, denoted $t_{n-1}$. A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$ is:

$$\bar{X} \pm t_{\alpha/2, \, n-1} \cdot \frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}$$

*Why the heavier tails?* The z-statistic $(\bar{X}-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})$ has only one source of randomness: $\bar{X}$. The t-statistic $(\bar{X}-\mu)/(S/\sqrt{n})$ has *two*: $\bar{X}$ in the numerator and $S$ in the denominator. When $S$ happens to be small (underestimating $\sigma$), the t-statistic blows up — producing more extreme values than the normal would predict. The t-distribution's heavier tails absorb this extra variability.

### The shape of the t-distribution

The t-distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom is symmetric and bell-shaped, like the normal, but with heavier tails. Its density is:

$<MathInline expression={"f(t) = \\frac{\\Gamma\\!\\left(\\frac{k+1}{2}\\right)}{\\sqrt{k\\pi}\\;\\Gamma\\!\\left(\\frac{k}{2}\\right)} \\left(1 + \\frac{t^2}{k}\\right)^{-(k+1)/2}"} />$

As $k \to \infty$, the t-distribution converges to the standard normal. A rule of thumb: for $k \geq 30$, the t and normal are nearly indistinguishable, which is why "n >= 30" is the threshold for using the z-interval as an approximation. For small $k$, the difference is substantial:

| Degrees of freedom | $t_{0.025}$ (95% CI) | vs $z_{0.025} = 1.96$ |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 12.71 | 6.5x wider |
| 5 | 2.571 | 31% wider |
| 10 | 2.228 | 14% wider |
| 30 | 2.042 | 4% wider |
| $\infty$ | 1.960 | (normal) |

With only 1 degree of freedom (a sample of 2), the 95% CI is *six and a half times* wider than the normal-based interval — a dramatic correction that honestly reflects how little you know from two data points.

## Three perspectives on confidence intervals

1. **As a dancing lasso (the frequentist view):** the parameter is a fixed target; the interval is a random lasso thrown at it. The confidence level is the long-run fraction of lassos that catch the target. This is the correct frequentist interpretation, and it is what the math actually guarantees.
2. **As inverted hypothesis tests:** a 95% confidence interval for $\mu$ is exactly the set of values $\mu_0$ that would NOT be rejected by a two-sided hypothesis test at the 5% level. Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests ([lesson 20](/courses/probability/20-hypothesis-testing)) are two sides of the same coin — the interval is the collection of "plausible" parameter values, where plausibility is defined by the test.
3. **As a quantification of sampling uncertainty:** the width of the interval tells you how much the sample mean wanders, which is $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$. The interval is a point estimate plus or minus a measure of its own reliability. This is the practical use: the interval tells you not just your best guess but how much to trust it.

## Modern applications

**A/B testing.** Every tech company runs A/B tests to compare features, layouts, and algorithms. The confidence interval on the difference in conversion rates between version A and version B tells you not just which version is better but *how much* better, and whether the difference is large enough to matter. A common failure mode: a tiny, statistically significant difference with a narrow CI — "significant" but practically useless. The CI protects against this by showing the magnitude.

**Clinical trials.** Drug approval hinges on confidence intervals. A new drug is approved if its effect size's confidence interval is entirely above the clinically meaningful threshold (not just above zero). The width of the CI determines the sample size needed — this is why trials are powered to detect a specific effect size with a specific precision. Sample size determination (the formula above) is how pharmaceutical companies decide how many patients to enroll.

**Polling and election forecasting.** A political poll reports a "margin of error" of $\pm 3\%$, which is the half-width of a 95% CI: $1.96 \cdot \sqrt{0.25/1000} \approx 0.031$. Election forecasters (FiveThirtyEight, etc.) build CIs for each state's vote share and combine them via simulation. The CI is what makes a forecast "90% confident" vs "lean" — the width encodes the uncertainty.

**Quality control and Six Sigma.** Manufacturing processes are monitored with confidence intervals on key parameters (dimensions, weights, defect rates). "Six Sigma" quality means the process mean is at least 6 standard deviations from the specification limit, so that defects are extremely rare (a few per million). The CI on the process mean determines whether the process is "in control."

A confidence interval is a lasso thrown at a fixed target. The target does not move — your lasso does. A 95% confidence level means the lasso catches the target on 95% of throws (over repeated sampling), NOT that there is a 95% probability the true value is in any single interval. When the population variance is unknown (almost always), use the t-distribution (heavier tails, wider intervals for small samples), which converges to the normal as n grows. The width sigma/sqrt(n) ties the interval directly to the CLT and determines the sample size needed for any desired precision.

## Connections

This lesson is the direct continuation of [lesson 18](/courses/probability/18-maximum-likelihood-estimation): the confidence interval is built from the MLE's asymptotic normality — <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta}_{\\text{MLE}} \\approx N(\\theta,\\, 1/(nI(\\theta)))"} /> gives the interval <MathInline expression={"\\hat{\\theta} \\pm 1.96/\\sqrt{nI(\\theta)}"} />. The Fisher information $I(\theta)$ determines the width. For the normal mean, $I(\mu) = 1/\sigma^2$, recovering the familiar $\bar{X} \pm 1.96\sigma/\sqrt{n}$. The **Central Limit Theorem** ([lesson 16](/courses/probability/16-central-limit-theorem)) is what justifies the normal approximation for large $n$, and the **sampling theory** of [lesson 17](/courses/probability/17-sampling-and-point-estimation) provides the standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.

The forward link is to **hypothesis testing** ([lesson 20](/courses/probability/20-hypothesis-testing)): a 95% CI is exactly the set of parameter values that would not be rejected by a two-sided test at the 5% level. The two are mathematically equivalent — if the CI contains $\mu_0$, you fail to reject $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$; if it does not, you reject. This duality means every concept in this lesson (z, t, standard error, sample size) maps directly to the corresponding concept in hypothesis testing (test statistic, critical value, power).

Cross-disciplinarily, the confidence interval is the frequentist answer to the question "what is the plausible range for the parameter?" The Bayesian answer is the **credible interval** ([lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-conditional-probability-and-bayes)), which CAN be interpreted as "95% probability the parameter is in this interval" — because the Bayesian treats the parameter as random (having a posterior distribution). The tension between these two interpretations is one of the great debates in statistics. In modern practice, most working scientists report confidence intervals alongside (or instead of) p-values, because the interval conveys both the estimate and its uncertainty — more informative than a single accept/reject verdict.

Step 5: Multiply by -1 (flipping inequalities again): P(X_bar - z_alpha/2*sigma/sqrt(n) <= mu <= X_bar + z_alpha/2*sigma/sqrt(n)) = 1 - alpha. Step 6: This says that the random interval [X_bar - z*sigma/sqrt(n), X_bar + z*sigma/sqrt(n)] contains the fixed parameter mu with probability 1 - alpha. This is the confidence interval. The rearrangement is valid because we only applied operations that preserve the truth of inequalities: multiplying by a positive number, and adding/subtracting the same quantity. The key subtlety: after the rearrangement, the random quantity is the INTERVAL (it depends on X_bar), and the fixed quantity is mu. The probability statement is about the interval, not about mu. This is why the correct interpretation is '95% of intervals constructed this way contain mu,' not '95% probability that mu is in this interval.' The interval is random; mu is fixed. The derivation makes this explicit."
