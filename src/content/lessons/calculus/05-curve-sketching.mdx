If someone tells you a function's derivative, you can sketch the original function. You don't need the formula — just the **sign** of $f'$ tells you almost everything about the shape. Add the sign of $f''$ and you know the curvature too. This is the diagnostic heart of calculus: the derivatives are an X-ray of the function's behavior.

## The derivative reads the landscape

Think of $f(x)$ as a hilly landscape and $f'(x)$ as a topographic map:

- $f'(x) > 0$: the terrain slopes **uphill** — the function is increasing.
- $f'(x) < 0$: the terrain slopes **downhill** — the function is decreasing.
- $f'(x) = 0$: the terrain is **flat** — you've found a peak, a valley, or a plateau.

Trace $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$ from left to right: the curve rises (green positive), peaks at $x = -1$ (green crosses zero), falls through the origin (green negative), bottoms out at $x = 1$ (green crosses zero again), then rises forever.

Points where $f'(x) = 0$ (or where $f'$ fails to exist) are called **critical points**. They are the *only* candidates for local maxima and minima — but a critical point is not automatically an extremum. The classification requires looking at how $f'$ changes sign around the point.

## The second derivative and concavity

While $f'$ tells you direction, $f''(x)$ tells you about **curvature** — how the slope itself is changing:

- $f''(x) > 0$: the curve is **concave up** — it holds water, like a bowl. The slope is increasing.
- $f''(x) < 0$: the curve is **concave down** — it spills water, like a dome. The slope is decreasing.
- $f''(x) = 0$: a possible **inflection point** — where the concavity switches.

For $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$, we have $f''(x) = 6x$. The inflection point is at $x = 0$ — the exact moment the curve switches from concave down (dome) to concave up (bowl).

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{concave up} \\iff f''(x) > 0, \\qquad \\text{concave down} \\iff f''(x) < 0"} />

## Theorem: First Derivative Test

Suppose $c$ is a critical point of $f$ (so $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist) and $f$ is continuous at $c$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{If } f' \\text{ changes sign from } + \\text{ to } - \\text{ at } c, \\text{ then } f(c) \\text{ is a local maximum.}"} />
<MathBlock expression={"\\text{If } f' \\text{ changes sign from } - \\text{ to } + \\text{ at } c, \\text{ then } f(c) \\text{ is a local minimum.}"} />
<MathBlock expression={"\\text{If } f' \\text{ does not change sign at } c, \\text{ then } f(c) \\text{ is neither.}"} />

The intuition is purely geometric: if the function was climbing (positive slope) and starts falling (negative slope), the critical point is a peak. If it was falling and starts climbing, the critical point is a valley. The First Derivative Test is robust — it works even when the second derivative is zero or undefined, which is why it never fails to classify a sign-change.

## Theorem: Second Derivative Test

Suppose $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''$ is continuous near $c$.

<MathBlock expression={"f''(c) > 0 \\implies f(c) \\text{ is a local minimum (concave up = bowl)"} />
<MathBlock expression={"f''(c) < 0 \\implies f(c) \\text{ is a local maximum (concave down = dome)"} />
<MathBlock expression={"f''(c) = 0 \\implies \\text{the test is inconclusive."} />

When $f''(c) = 0$, *anything* can happen. Consider $f(x) = x^4$, which has $f'(0) = 0$ and $f''(0) = 0$ yet a clear minimum at the origin. Or $f(x) = -x^4$, same derivatives but a maximum. Or $f(x) = x^3$, where the origin is neither. When the second derivative test fails, fall back to the **First Derivative Test** by checking the sign of $f'$ on either side of $c$.

## The curve sketching checklist

Think of curve sketching as a **diagnostic flowchart** — a systematic procedure that extracts the complete shape from just a few pieces of information. Professional mathematicians run through this checklist mentally before plotting anything:

1. **Domain** — Where is $f$ defined? Note excluded points, asymptotes, and endpoints.
2. **Intercepts** — Where does $f$ cross the axes? ($x$-intercepts: solve $f(x)=0$; $y$-intercept: evaluate $f(0)$.)
3. **Symmetry** — Is $f$ even ($f(-x) = f(x)$, $y$-axis symmetry), odd ($f(-x) = -f(x)$, origin symmetry), or periodic?
4. **Asymptotes** — Vertical (where $f \to \pm\infty$), horizontal (<MathInline expression={"\\lim_{x \\to \\pm\\infty} f(x)"} />), or oblique (slanted).
5. **Intervals of increase/decrease** — Solve $f'(x) > 0$ and $f'(x) < 0$. Identify critical points where $f'(x) = 0$.
6. **Local extrema** — Apply the First or Second Derivative Test at each critical point.
7. **Concavity and inflection points** — Solve $f''(x) = 0$; test the sign of $f''$ on either side.

A computer can plot any function instantly — but understanding these diagnostic steps is what makes you a mathematician, not a button-pusher. The point of curve sketching isn't to produce the picture; it's to train yourself to read a function's shape directly from its derivatives, the way a doctor reads an X-ray.

## Three ways to read the second derivative

$f''$ carries three layers of meaning, each useful in a different context:

1. **Geometric (curvature):** the sign of $f''$ tells you bowl vs. dome — whether the curve bends toward or away from you.
2. **Physical (acceleration):** if $f$ is position, $f'$ is velocity and $f''$ is acceleration. A positive $f''$ means you're speeding up in the positive direction.
3. **Optimization (classification):** at a critical point, $f''$ immediately classifies it as a max or min — no sign chart needed.

## Modern applications

Curve-sketching logic is the foundation of several modern techniques:

- **Optimism in machine learning:** training a neural network means navigating a loss landscape. Inflection points and saddle points (where the gradient is zero but it's neither a min nor a max) are the *central obstacle* to efficient training — most "critical points" in high-dimensional loss surfaces are saddles, not minima.
- **Physics — phase transitions:** the second derivative of the free energy with respect to temperature is the heat capacity. An inflection point (a discontinuity or zero in $f''$) signals a phase transition — the moment a material changes state.
- **Economics — diminishing returns:** marginal cost is the first derivative of total cost; its sign and curvature tell you whether producing more is getting cheaper or more expensive per unit.

<KeyInsight>
The first derivative tells you direction (up/down/flat). The second derivative tells you curvature (bowl/dome/switching). Together they give you the complete shape of the function — and at a critical point, the sign change of $f'$ classifies the extremum, while $f''$ offers a faster test that occasionally fails.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson connects [differentiation rules](/courses/calculus/03-differentiation-rules) to the **shape** of functions. The Mean Value Theorem is the hidden engine: it guarantees that if $f'(x) > 0$ on an interval, then $f$ is strictly increasing there — the sign of the derivative *rigorously* determines direction, not just as intuition. In the [next lesson on optimization](/courses/calculus/06-optimization), we'll use these same critical points and classification tests to find the *best* value of a function, not just classify its shape.

The concavity analysis generalizes in two directions. In multivariable calculus, the **Hessian matrix** of second partial derivatives plays the role of $f''$ — its eigenvalues classify critical points of surfaces as minima, maxima, or saddle points. And in numerical methods, Newton's method for finding roots uses the second derivative (curvature) to decide how big a step to take, exactly the way $f''$ refines our picture of a curve.
