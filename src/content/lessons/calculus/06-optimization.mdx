You want the best outcome — the most profit, the least cost, the shortest path. Calculus gives you a systematic way to find it: **set the derivative to zero and check**.

## The idea

A smooth function's peaks and valleys are where the tangent line is flat — where $f'(x) = 0$. These points are called **critical points**. Not every critical point is a maximum or minimum, but every maximum and minimum (in the interior) is a critical point.

- $f'' < 0$ at the critical point → **local maximum** (dome shape, marked red)
- $f'' > 0$ at the critical point → **local minimum** (bowl shape, marked green)

## The shortest path: a deeper example

Imagine you need to travel from point $A$ on land to point $B$ in the water. You run faster on land than you swim in water. What's the fastest path?

You might think a straight line — but a straight line spends too much time swimming slowly. You might think: run to the shore closest to $B$, then swim. But that path is unnecessarily long. Calculus finds the sweet spot: the exact crossing point where the tradeoff between distance and speed is optimal. This is the same principle behind Fermat's principle of least time in optics — light itself "chooses" the fastest path, bending as it passes from air into water.

Today, this same idea powers **gradient descent**, the engine behind machine learning. A neural network's "loss" is a surface in high dimensions, and training means rolling downhill to find the lowest point — a minimum. The derivative points downhill; the algorithm follows it. Every time a model improves its predictions, it's solving an optimization problem.

## The optimization recipe

1. **Model**: express the quantity to optimize as a function $f(x)$.
2. **Differentiate**: compute $f'(x)$.
3. **Find critical points**: solve $f'(x) = 0$.
4. **Classify**: use the second derivative test or the sign-change test.
5. **Check boundaries**: the global optimum might be at the edge of the domain.

## A classic example

A farmer has 100 meters of fence and wants to enclose the largest possible rectangular area against a wall (no fence needed on the wall side).

Let $x$ = width, $y$ = height. The constraint is $2y + x = 100$, so $y = (100 - x)/2$.

Area: $A(x) = x \cdot y = x(100 - x)/2 = 50x - x^2/2$.

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = 50 - x = 0 \\implies x = 50"} />
<MathBlock expression={"A''(x) = -1 < 0 \\implies \\text{local max"} />

The optimal rectangle is 50m wide and 25m tall, giving $A = 1250$ m². The second derivative is negative everywhere — the parabola opens downward, so this is indeed the global maximum.

<KeyInsight>
To find the optimum, set the derivative to zero and verify the concavity. A negative second derivative confirms a maximum (dome); a positive second derivative confirms a minimum (bowl).
</KeyInsight>
