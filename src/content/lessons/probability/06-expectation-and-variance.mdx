Imagine a probability distribution as a physical object — a bar chart cut from sheet metal. The bars have different heights because some outcomes are heavier than others. If you tried to balance this metal chart on your fingertip, there is exactly one point where it would rest level. That point is the **expectation**.

Now look at how far the metal extends to either side of that balance point. A distribution that is tall and narrow has most of its mass clustered near the center — it barely reaches outward. A distribution that is short and wide has mass spread far from center — it reaches far in both directions. That reach is the **variance**. Between them, expectation and variance capture the two most important things about any distribution: where it sits, and how it spreads.

## Expectation as center of mass

The **expected value** (also called the mean, or expectation) of a discrete random variable is the probability-weighted average of all possible outcomes:

$$E[X] = \sum_i x_i \cdot p_i$$

For a continuous random variable, the sum becomes an integral:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X] = \\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} x \\cdot f(x)\\,dx"} />$

The geometric picture: multiply each bar's height (probability) by its position (value), and sum them up. You are computing the center of mass of the distribution.

### Theorem: Expectation of an indicator

For any event $A$ with indicator <MathInline expression={"\\mathbf{1}_A"} />,

$<MathInline expression={"E[\\mathbf{1}_A] = P(A)"} />$

*Proof.* <MathInline expression={"\\mathbf{1}_A"} /> takes only values 0 and 1, so <MathInline expression={"E[\\mathbf{1}_A] = 0 \\cdot P(A^c) + 1 \\cdot P(A) = P(A)"} />.

This innocent identity — the expectation of an indicator is the probability of the event — is one of the most useful facts in probability. It lets us replace probability computations with expectation computations, where we can deploy linearity.

**A fair die.** Each face from 1 to 6 has probability $1/6$. The expectation is:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X] = \\frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = 3.5"} />$

The balance point sits at 3.5 — a value you can never actually roll. The expectation is not "the most likely outcome." It is where the distribution balances. Think of it as the fulcrum, not the peak.

## Linearity of expectation — the most important property

Expectation has a property so useful it deserves its own name: **linearity**.

### Theorem: Linearity of expectation

For any random variables $X, Y$ and constants $a, b$:

$$E[aX + bY] = a\,E[X] + b\,E[Y]$$

This holds **always** — with no assumption about independence, correlation, or any relationship between $X$ and $Y$ whatsoever.

Scaling a random variable scales its center of mass. Shifting it shifts the balance point. That part is intuitive. The deeper surprise is that the expectation of a sum is always the sum of expectations, even when the variables are tangled together. Linearity does not care about dependence.

This is the single most-used theorem in probability. Independence is a strong and often unrealistic assumption, but linearity never needs it. You can decompose a complicated random variable into simpler pieces, take expectations separately, and add them back up.

**Example: aces in a poker hand.** You draw 5 cards from a standard deck. What is the expected number of aces?

Define an indicator variable for each card: $I_k = 1$ if the $k$-th card is an ace, and $0$ otherwise. The probability that any single card is an ace is $4/52 = 1/13$. So $E[I_k] = 1/13$ for each of the 5 cards.

The total number of aces is $X = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 + I_4 + I_5$. By linearity:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X] = 5 \\cdot \\frac{1}{13} = \\frac{5}{13} \\approx 0.385"} />$

Notice: we did not need to know how the cards depend on each other. (If the first card is an ace, the second card is less likely to be one — but that does not matter for the expectation of the sum.) Linearity cuts through the dependence. This indicator trick — write a count as a sum of indicators, then apply linearity — solves a huge class of problems that would be brutal by direct computation.

## Variance as spread

The expectation tells you where the distribution balances. It says nothing about how the mass is arranged around that point. Two distributions can share the same balance point but look completely different — one tall and narrow, the other short and wide.

The **variance** measures that spread:

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X) = E\\!\\left[(X - E[X])^2\\right]"} />$

It is the average squared distance from the center of mass. Every outcome contributes its squared deviation, weighted by its probability. Outliers pull hard because squaring amplifies large distances.

### The computational formula

There is a computationally convenient equivalent form:

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - \\left(E[X]\\right)^2"} />$

In words: "the mean of the squares, minus the square of the mean." This form is often faster to compute because you only need $E[X]$ and $E[X^2]$, rather than computing every individual deviation.

### Theorem: Variance under scaling and shifting

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\\,\\text{Var}(X)"} />$

Shifting by a constant does nothing to the spread (the distribution just translates). Scaling multiplies variance by the *square* of the scale factor — because variance is a squared quantity. This is why standard deviation, not variance, is the natural unit of spread.

Note the contrast with expectation, which is linear: $E[aX+b] = aE[X]+b$. Variance is *not* linear. This asymmetry matters constantly.

**A fair die, revisited.** We already know $E[X] = 3.5$. Computing $E[X^2]$:

$<MathInline expression={"E[X^2] = \\frac{1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + 4^2 + 5^2 + 6^2}{6} = \\frac{91}{6} \\approx 15.17"} />$

So the variance is:

$<MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X) = \\frac{91}{6} - \\left(\\frac{7}{2}\\right)^2 = \\frac{91}{6} - \\frac{49}{4} = \\frac{35}{12} \\approx 2.917"} />$

**Same mean, different spread.** Consider a random variable that is always exactly 3.5 — a "deterministic" variable. Its mean is 3.5 and its variance is 0 (there is no spread at all). Now consider a fair die: same mean, but the variance is about 2.917. Same balance point, completely different shape. The variance is what distinguishes them.

## Variance as moment of inertia

If expectation is the center of mass, variance is the **moment of inertia** — the resistance of the distribution to rotation around its center. This is not a loose analogy; it is exact. A point mass $p_i$ at position $x_i$ contributes $p_i(x_i - \mu)^2$ to the moment of inertia about the center, which is precisely its contribution to the variance.

A tall narrow distribution (small variance) is like a tight spinning top — all the mass near the axis, easy to spin. A short wide distribution (large variance) is like a heavy rim — mass flung far from the axis, hard to get moving. The same physics governs both. Engineers and probabilists are doing the same calculation under different names.

## Standard deviation

Variance is measured in **squared units**. If $X$ represents a length in meters, then <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X)"} /> has units of square meters — an area, not a length. That makes it hard to compare directly with the original quantity.

The **standard deviation** fixes this by taking the square root:

$<MathInline expression={"\\sigma = \\sqrt{\\text{Var}(X)}"} />$

If $X$ is in meters, $\sigma$ is back in meters. It answers the question: "how far does a typical outcome land from the center of mass?" — in the same units as the data.

For the fair die: <MathInline expression={"\\sigma = \\sqrt{35/12} \\approx 1.71"} />. A typical roll lands about 1.7 units away from 3.5. That feels right — the die ranges from 1 to 6, and most rolls are within 2 of the center.

## Moments — the fingerprints of a distribution

The $k$-th **moment** of $X$ is $E[X^k]$. These moments form a sequence that fingerprints the distribution:

- **First moment** $E[X]$ — the mean, the center of mass.
- **Second moment** $E[X^2]$ — together with the first moment, gives the variance via $E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$.
- **Higher moments** $E[X^3], E[X^4], \ldots$ — measure skewness (asymmetry) and kurtosis (tail heaviness).

The variance is therefore called the *second central moment* — the second moment measured around the mean. Under mild conditions, the entire sequence of moments uniquely determines the distribution, which is why moment-based methods (method of moments, moment generating functions) are so powerful in statistics.

## Jensen's inequality — convexity and expectation

A subtle and far-reaching theorem connects expectation with the shape of functions.

### Theorem: Jensen's inequality

If $f$ is a **convex** function (curving upward, like $x^2$ or $e^x$) and $X$ is any random variable with finite expectation, then:

$$E[f(X)] \geq f(E[X])$$

If $f$ is **concave** (curving downward, like <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{x}"} /> or $\log x$), the inequality flips:

$$E[f(X)] \leq f(E[X])$$

Equality holds only when $f$ is linear on the range of $X$ or when $X$ is deterministic.

The intuition is geometric. For a convex function, the chord connecting two points on the curve lies *above* the curve. When you take an expectation, you are averaging points on the curve (the $f(x_i)$ values), which lands on the chord — above the curve at the average point $f(E[X])$. Convexity means "the average of the function is at least the function of the average."

**Why this matters.** Jensen's inequality explains countless phenomena:

- **The variance is non-negative:** with $f(x) = x^2$ (convex), $E[X^2] \geq (E[X])^2$, so <MathInline expression={"\\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \\geq 0"} />. Variance cannot be negative — and Jensen is why.
- **Arithmetic mean ≥ geometric mean:** with $f(x) = -\log x$ (concave), $E[-\log X] \leq -\log(E[X])$, which rearranges to the AM-GM inequality.
- **Risk and insurance:** a concave utility function $u$ (risk aversion) gives $E[u(X)] \leq u(E[X])$. A risk-averse person prefers the certain amount $E[X]$ over the gamble $X$, even with the same expected value. This is the foundation of insurance and portfolio theory.
- **Information theory:** the gap $E[f(X)] - f(E[X])$ for convex $f$ appears as the definition of divergence measures in information theory and machine learning (e.g., the evidence lower bound in variational inference).

Jensen's inequality is the reason convexity is the most important shape in optimization, statistics, and machine learning. Whenever you take an expectation *outside* a nonlinear function, convexity decides which way the inequality goes.

## Three perspectives on expectation and variance

1. **Physical:** expectation is center of mass; variance is moment of inertia; standard deviation is radius of gyration. The whole theory of random variables is literally classical mechanics in disguise.
2. **Computational:** linearity of expectation lets you decompose and conquer — break a complex variable into simple pieces (often indicators), take expectations separately, and add. Variance is harder (it is not linear) but the computational formula $E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$ makes it a two-moment calculation.
3. **Geometric (Jensen):** convex functions bend upward, so $E[f(X)] \geq f(E[X])$; concave functions bend downward, so the reverse. Curvature interacts with randomness in a deterministic, predictable way.

## Modern applications

**Machine learning loss functions.** Every ML training objective is an expectation — the expected loss over the data distribution. Mean squared error is <MathInline expression={"E[(Y - \\hat{Y})^2]"} />; the bias-variance tradeoff decomposes it using <MathInline expression={"E[X^2] = \\text{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2"} />. Cross-entropy is an expectation of a log-probability. Optimization of these expectations (via stochastic gradient descent) is the engine of modern deep learning.

**Finance: risk and return.** Portfolio theory is built on expectation (expected return) and variance (risk). The Sharpe ratio, Markowitz efficient frontier, and option pricing all reduce to manipulating these two quantities. Jensen's inequality, applied to the concave utility of wealth, is the formal justification for why rational investors diversify.

**Concentration and generalization.** How confidently can you estimate $E[X]$ from a sample? Concentration inequalities (Chebyshev, Chernoff) bound the probability that a sample mean strays far from the true mean, and every one of them is a statement about variance. These bounds are what guarantee that machine learning models generalize from training data to unseen data — covered in lessons 14 and 17.

<KeyInsight>
Expectation is the balance point — where the distribution would rest on your fingertip. Variance is the reach — how far the mass extends from that point, the rotational moment of inertia of the distribution. Linearity of expectation ($E[aX+bY] = aE[X]+bE[Y]$) holds with no independence assumption and is the most-used tool in probability, while Jensen's inequality ($E[f(X)] \geq f(E[X])$ for convex $f$) explains why variance is non-negative, why risk-averse people insure, and why convexity dominates optimization.
