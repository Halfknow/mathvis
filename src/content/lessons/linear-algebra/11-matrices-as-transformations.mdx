# Matrices as Transformations

Forget "rows and columns of numbers" for a moment. A matrix is a **machine that moves points**. Feed it a vector, and it spits out a new vector. Every point in space gets relocated according to the same rule — and the result is that the entire grid of space gets warped, stretched, rotated, or sheared.

This is the single most important perspective shift in linear algebra: **a matrix is a transformation of space**.

## How a matrix moves space

A 2x2 matrix tells you where two special vectors land: where **e₁ = (1,0)** goes and where **e₂ = (0,1)** goes. The first column is the new home of e₁, the second column is the new home of e₂.

<MathBlock expression={'\\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\\\ c & d \\end{bmatrix} \\begin{bmatrix} x \\\\ y \\end{bmatrix} = x \\begin{bmatrix} a \\\\ c \\end{bmatrix} + y \\begin{bmatrix} b \\\\ d \\end{bmatrix} = \\begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\\\ cx + dy \\end{bmatrix}'} />

Read this geometrically: the output is x times "where e₁ went" plus y times "where e₂ went." The entire transformation is determined by what happens to just two vectors.

## Visual catalogue

Every 2x2 matrix produces one of these spatial effects. Try each preset above and watch:

- **Rotation**: Click "Rotation 90°" — the grid turns but keeps its shape. Distances and angles are preserved. The determinant stays at 1 because the area doesn't change. Try "Rotation 45°" for a subtler turn.

- **Scaling**: Click "Scale 2x" — the grid stretches uniformly outward. Everything doubles in size. The determinant jumps to 4 because the unit square's area quadruples.

- **Shear**: Click "Shear" — the grid slides sideways, turning squares into parallelograms. The y-axis tilts to the right. Notice the determinant is still 1: the parallelogram has the same area as the original square.

- **Reflection**: Click "Reflect x" — the grid flips across the x-axis. The determinant drops to **-1**. The negative sign means the orientation has flipped: if you drew a letter "F" on the grid, it would now read backwards.

- **Projection**: Click "Projection" — the entire plane collapses onto the x-axis. Every point's y-coordinate becomes zero. The determinant is zero: the unit square has been squashed flat, losing all its area.

- **Collapse**: Click "Collapse" — the grid folds in on itself, collapsing to something less than a line. The determinant drops to zero and the grid loses a dimension.

## Reading the determinant

The determinant is the most information-dense number associated with a matrix. It tells you four things at once:

- **|det| = area scaling factor**: The absolute value tells you how much the transformation stretches or shrinks areas. |det| = 2 means areas double; |det| = 0.5 means areas halve.
- **det > 0 means orientation preserved**: A letter "F" drawn on the grid still reads as "F" — the transformation hasn't flipped the plane.
- **det < 0 means orientation flipped**: The "F" is now mirror-imaged. The transformation has turned the plane inside out.
- **det = 0 means dimension collapsed**: Information has been lost. The transformation is not invertible — you cannot undo it, because multiple input points have been smashed into the same output point.

## The key property: linearity

Matrix transformations are **linear**: they preserve the structure of vector arithmetic. In geometric terms:

- **Lines remain lines** (they don't curve)
- **The origin stays fixed** (it doesn't shift)
- **Grid lines remain evenly spaced** (they don't bunch up or spread out unevenly)

Linearity means the transformation of any vector is completely determined by where the basis vectors go. If you know the fate of e₁ and e₂, you know the fate of everything. This is why a 2×2 matrix — just 4 numbers — can describe an infinite transformation. Every point's new location is a weighted combination of where the two basis vectors landed.
