Can you approximate any smooth function with a polynomial? Yes -- and the recipe is: match derivatives at a single point. Taylor's idea is to encode everything about a function at a single point into its derivatives. The first derivative gives the slope, the second gives the curvature, the third gives the rate of change of curvature, and so on. More derivatives means a better approximation -- each additional term captures a finer detail of how the function bends.

## The Taylor polynomial

The $n$-th degree Taylor polynomial of $f$ centered at $a$ is:

<MathBlock expression={"T_n(x) = \\sum_{k=0}^{n} \\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!} (x-a)^k"} />

Each term matches an additional derivative at $x = a$:
- $k = 0$: matches the **value** $f(a)$
- $k = 1$: matches the **slope** $f'(a)$
- $k = 2$: matches the **curvature** $f''(a)$
- Higher $k$: match higher-order bending behavior

Start with $f(x) = \sin(x)$ at center $= 0$:
- $n = 1$: a straight line through the origin — matches the slope.
- $n = 3$: a cubic that hugs one full arch of sine.
- $n = 9$: the polynomial and sine are visually indistinguishable over $[-4, 4]$.

Now try $f(x) = \ln(1+x)$. The red dashed lines mark the radius of convergence ($R = 1$). Beyond $x = 1$, no amount of Taylor terms will help — the series simply doesn't converge there.

## The error: Taylor's Remainder Theorem

How good is the approximation? The remainder term tells us:

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) - T_n(x) = \\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}"} />

for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$. The error is bounded by the $(n+1)$-th derivative at an unknown point, divided by $(n+1)!$. Since factorials grow astronomically fast, the error usually shrinks quickly.

## Maclaurin series (Taylor at $a = 0$)

Most commonly, we center at $a = 0$:

| Function | Maclaurin series |
|----------|-----------------|
| $e^x$ | $1 + x + x^2/2! + x^3/3! + \cdots$ |
| $\sin x$ | $x - x^3/3! + x^5/5! - \cdots$ |
| $\cos x$ | $1 - x^2/2! + x^4/4! - \cdots$ |
| $\ln(1+x)$ | $x - x^2/2 + x^3/3 - \cdots$ |

<KeyInsight>
A Taylor polynomial matches a function's value, slope, curvature, and higher-order bends at a single point. As the degree increases, the polynomial hugs the function over a wider interval — but only within the radius of convergence.
</KeyInsight>
