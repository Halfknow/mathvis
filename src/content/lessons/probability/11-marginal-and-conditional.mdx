You have a joint distribution — a heat map or a surface showing the probability landscape of two variables together. But often you want to ask a simpler question. What does $X$ look like on its own? Or: given that $Y$ takes a specific value, what does $X$ look like now?

These two questions produce two different kinds of distributions. The **marginal** collapses the landscape onto one wall. The **conditional** slices through it at a fixed value and rescales what remains. One ignores the other variable entirely. The other uses it as a lens. Mastering both operations is the key to every reasoning task involving multiple variables — from Bayesian inference to feature engineering.

## Marginal distributions: projecting onto one axis

Imagine the joint density $f(x, y)$ as a 3D surface. Now imagine a light source far to one side, casting a shadow of the entire surface onto the $xz$-wall (the wall where $Y = 0$). That shadow is the **marginal distribution** of $X$.

To compute it, you integrate out (or "sum out") the other variable:

$<MathInline expression={"f_X(x) = \\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f(x, y)\\,dy \\qquad \\text{(continuous)}"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"p_X(x) = \\sum_j p(x, y_j) \\qquad \\text{(discrete)}"} />$

Geometrically: for each value of $x$, you sweep along the $y$-axis and accumulate all the probability mass at that $x$-coordinate. The result is a single curve $f_X(x)$ — a one-dimensional landscape that tells you everything about $X$ alone, and nothing about how $X$ relates to $Y$.

The word "marginal" comes from the literal margin of a table. If you write a joint PMF as a grid, the row totals written in the right margin give you $p_X$, and the column totals written in the bottom margin give you $p_Y$. The margins of the table *are* the marginal distributions.

### Theorem: Marginals are valid distributions

If $f(x, y)$ is a valid joint PDF, then the marginal $f_X(x) = \int f(x, y)\,dy$ is a valid PDF: it is non-negative, and it integrates to 1.

*Proof.* Non-negativity is immediate: $f \geq 0$ implies its integral is $\geq 0$. For normalization, swap the order of integration:

$<MathInline expression={"\\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f_X(x)\\,dx = \\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} \\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f(x, y)\\,dy\\,dx = 1"} />$

The last step uses the joint normalization condition. So the shadow of a valid landscape is itself a valid landscape — in one fewer dimension.

### The loss of information

Here is the catch. Many different joint distributions can produce the same marginals. Two very different landscapes — one with a diagonal ridge, one with a circular blob — can cast the same shadow on each wall. The marginal tells you about each variable alone, but it destroys all information about the *relationship* between them.

If the joint distribution is a photograph of two people, the marginals are two separate portraits. You can learn each person's face, but you cannot tell whether they are standing side by side or back to back. **You cannot recover the joint from the marginals alone** — unless the variables are independent (lesson 13). This is the deepest asymmetry in this subject: projection is easy and always possible, but reconstruction is impossible without extra information.

## Conditional distributions: slicing the landscape

Now a different operation. Instead of projecting the entire surface onto a wall, you slice it at one fixed value of $Y$. You pick $Y = y_0$, and you look only at the cross-section $f(x, y_0)$.

But a cross-section is not yet a distribution. Its total area (or sum) is almost certainly not 1 — it is some fraction of the total probability, namely $f_Y(y_0)$ (or $p_Y(y_0)$ for discrete). To turn this slice into a valid distribution, you normalize it — divide by that total.

$<MathInline expression={"f_{X \\mid Y}(x \\mid y) = \\frac{f(x, y)}{f_Y(y)} \\qquad \\text{(continuous)}"} />$

$<MathInline expression={"p_{X \\mid Y}(x \\mid y) = \\frac{p(x, y)}{p_Y(y)} \\qquad \\text{(discrete)}"} />$

Read the bar as "given." This is the density of $X$, *given that* $Y = y$. It is a genuine probability distribution in its own right: it is non-negative, and it integrates to 1 over $x$ (the $f_Y(y)$ in the denominator is exactly the normalizer that makes this true).

### Theorem: Conditional distributions are valid distributions

For any $y$ with $f_Y(y) > 0$, the conditional <MathInline expression={"f_{X|Y}(x|y) = f(x,y)/f_Y(y)"} /> is a valid PDF in $x$.

*Proof.* Non-negativity is immediate. For normalization: <MathInline expression={"\\int f_{X|Y}(x|y)\\,dx = \\frac{1}{f_Y(y)} \\int f(x,y)\\,dx = \\frac{f_Y(y)}{f_Y(y)} = 1"} />, where we used the definition of the marginal $f_Y(y) = \int f(x,y)\,dx$. So the rescaled slice is a genuine distribution — the geometric meaning of the normalizing denominator.

### The visual

Go back to the heat map. Pick one row — say the row where $Y = y_0$. The brightness values across that row are the unnormalized slice. Now rescale the entire row so that its total brightness becomes 1. The pattern of relative brightness within that row — the ups and downs across $x$ — is the conditional distribution $p(x \mid y_0)$.

Different rows may look very different. Row $Y = 1$ might show mass concentrated at large $x$-values. Row $Y = 3$ might show mass concentrated at small $x$-values. If the rows all look the same (after normalization), then knowing $Y$ tells you nothing about $X$ — the variables are independent. If the rows look different, knowing $Y$ reshapes your picture of $X$. This is the operational test for independence in the next lesson.

## Bayes' theorem for distributions

The marginal and conditional are linked by the most important equation in inference. Combining the definition of conditional with the symmetry $f(x, y) = f(y, x)$, we get **Bayes' theorem for densities**:

$<MathInline expression={"f_{Y \\mid X}(y \\mid x) = \\frac{f_{X \\mid Y}(x \\mid y) \\, f_Y(y)}{f_X(x)}"} />$

This is the same Bayes' theorem from [lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-bayes-theorem), lifted from events to distributions. It tells you how to *flip* the direction of conditioning: if you know the conditional of $X$ given $Y$, and the marginal of $Y$, you can recover the reverse conditional of $Y$ given $X$.

The denominator <MathInline expression={"f_X(x) = \\int f_{X|Y}(x|y) f_Y(y)\\,dy"} /> is the marginal of $X$, computed by integrating out $y$. This is the "evidence" term — the normalizer that makes the result a valid distribution. Every Bayesian update, from spam filtering to medical diagnosis to posterior inference in machine learning, is an application of this single equation.

## A worked example

Two fair coins: outcomes are HH, HT, TH, TT, each with probability $1/4$. Let $X$ be the total number of heads and $Y$ be the number of heads on the first flip. The joint PMF:

| | Y = 0 | Y = 1 | $p_X(x)$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| **X = 0** | 1/4 (TT) | 0 | 1/4 |
| **X = 1** | 1/4 (TH) | 1/4 (HT) | 1/2 |
| **X = 2** | 0 | 1/4 (HH) | 1/4 |
| **$p_Y(y)$** | 1/2 | 1/2 | 1 |

**Marginal of $X$** (sum across each row): $p_X(0) = 1/4 + 0 = 1/4$, $p_X(1) = 1/4 + 1/4 = 1/2$, $p_X(2) = 0 + 1/4 = 1/4$. These sum to 1, as they must. **Marginal of $Y$** (sum down each column): $p_Y(0) = 1/2$, $p_Y(1) = 1/2$.

**Conditional $X \mid Y = 1$**: take the $Y = 1$ column: $p(0, 1) = 0$, $p(1, 1) = 1/4$, $p(2, 1) = 1/4$. Divide by $p_Y(1) = 1/2$:

$<MathInline expression={"p_{X \\mid Y}(0 \\mid 1) = 0, \\quad p_{X \\mid Y}(1 \\mid 1) = \\tfrac{1}{2}, \\quad p_{X \\mid Y}(2 \\mid 1) = \\tfrac{1}{2}"} />$

Given that the first flip is heads, the total number of heads is either 1 or 2, with equal probability. It cannot be 0. The slice removed a possibility and rescaled the rest.

**Conditional $X \mid Y = 0$**: take the $Y = 0$ column: $p(0, 0) = 1/4$, $p(1, 0) = 1/4$, $p(2, 0) = 0$. Divide by $p_Y(0) = 1/2$:

$<MathInline expression={"p_{X \\mid Y}(0 \\mid 0) = \\tfrac{1}{2}, \\quad p_{X \\mid Y}(1 \\mid 0) = \\tfrac{1}{2}, \\quad p_{X \\mid Y}(2 \\mid 0) = 0"} />$

Given the first flip is tails, total heads is 0 or 1, never 2. The two conditional distributions look completely different — knowing $Y$ reshapes what you expect from $X$. This is the fingerprint of dependence.

<FormulaReadout formulas={[
  { label: "边缘分布 (积分/求和消除)", expression: "f_X(x) = \\int f(x,y)\\,dy, \\quad p_X(x) = \\sum_j p(x, y_j)" },
  { label: "条件分布 (切片再归一化)", expression: "f_{X|Y}(x|y) = \\frac{f(x,y)}{f_Y(y)}, \\quad p_{X|Y}(x|y) = \\frac{p(x,y)}{p_Y(y)}" },
  { label: "贝叶斯定理 (分布形式)", expression: "f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \\frac{f_{X|Y}(x|y)\\,f_Y(y)}{f_X(x)}" },
  { label: "联合 = 边缘 × 条件", expression: "f(x,y) = f_{X|Y}(x|y)\\, f_Y(y) = f_{Y|X}(y|x)\\, f_X(x)" },
]} />

## Three perspectives on marginal and conditional distributions

1. **Geometric (projection vs. slicing):** the marginal is the shadow of the joint surface on one wall (project, losing a dimension); the conditional is a cross-section at fixed $Y$, rescaled to have area 1 (slice, then normalize). These are the two fundamental operations on a 3D landscape.
2. **Information-theoretic:** the marginal tells you about one variable with *zero* information about the other; the conditional tells you about one variable *using everything you know* about the other. The difference between the conditional and the marginal — how much the conditional shifts when you learn $Y$ — is precisely what "information" measures (the mutual information, covered in advanced treatments).
3. **Bayesian (inversion):** Bayes' theorem lets you flip the direction of conditioning. The conditional of $X$ given $Y$ plus the marginal of $Y$ together determine the conditional of $Y$ given $X$. This symmetry is the engine of Bayesian inference — you specify a likelihood and a prior, and recover a posterior.

## Modern applications

**Bayesian inference and posterior updating.** The entire Bayesian framework is built on the conditional distribution. You have a parameter $\theta$ (say, the true click-through rate of an ad) with a *prior* distribution $p(\theta)$. You observe data $x$. The *likelihood* is $p(x \mid \theta)$ — the conditional of the data given the parameter. Bayes' theorem flips this into the *posterior* $p(\theta \mid x) = p(x \mid \theta) p(\theta) / p(x)$: the conditional of the parameter given the data. Every Bayesian update — from A/B testing to medical diagnosis to hyperparameter tuning — is this single flip. The marginal $p(x) = \int p(x \mid \theta) p(\theta)\,d\theta$ (the "evidence") is the normalizer that makes the posterior a valid distribution.

**Conditional models in machine learning.** Supervised learning is, at its core, the problem of estimating a conditional distribution. Given features $X$ (an image), what is the distribution of the label $Y$ (the object in the image)? A classifier outputs $p(Y \mid X)$; a regression model outputs the parameters of $p(Y \mid X)$. The whole training loop — minimizing cross-entropy or mean squared error — is maximum likelihood estimation of the conditional distribution. Generative models like GANs and diffusion models go the other direction: they learn to sample from $p(X)$ itself, the marginal of the data.

**Markov chains and time series.** A Markov chain is defined by its *transition kernel* — the conditional distribution <MathInline expression={"p(X_{t+1} \\mid X_t)"} />. The chain's long-run behavior is captured by the *stationary distribution*, which is a marginal. The interplay between conditionals (transitions) and marginals (stationary distributions) is the heart of Markov chain theory, MCMC sampling, and PageRank.

<KeyInsight>
The marginal projects the joint landscape onto one axis — it sums or integrates out the other variable, producing a valid 1D distribution that forgets everything about the relationship. The conditional slices the landscape at a fixed value and rescales — it shows you one variable through the lens of the other, preserving the shape of the dependence. Bayes' theorem links them: the conditional of $X$ given $Y$ plus the marginal of $Y$ determines the reverse conditional, which is the engine of Bayesian inference.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson takes the **joint distributions** of [lesson 10](/courses/probability/10-joint-distributions) and shows how to extract single-variable information from them. The marginalization operation (summing or integrating out a variable) is the direct multivariate analog of the normalization conditions from [lesson 06](/courses/probability/06-expectation-and-variance). Bayes' theorem for distributions is the lifting of the **event-level Bayes' theorem** from [lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-bayes-theorem) to the random-variable setting — same equation, richer context.

Forward, the conditional distribution is the gateway to [lesson 12](/courses/probability/12-covariance-and-correlation): the covariance measures *on average* how much the conditional shifts as $Y$ changes, reducing a whole family of conditionals to a single number. [Lesson 13](/courses/probability/13-independence) identifies the special case where the conditional equals the marginal — where learning $Y$ changes nothing — and gives it the name *independence*. The Bayesian flip (prior + likelihood = posterior) introduced here becomes the computational backbone of all of statistical inference in lessons 17-20. Cross-disciplinarily, the projection/slicing duality of marginals and conditionals is the same duality that appears in database theory (marginalization is a GROUP-BY aggregation), in image processing (projections in computed tomography), and in quantum mechanics (partial trace of a density matrix).
