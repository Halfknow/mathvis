# Inner Product Spaces

The dot product gave us length, angle, and orthogonality. But the dot product is just one example of an **inner product** — a generalization that works for functions, sequences, and any vector space.

## What makes an inner product?

An inner product is any operation that takes two vectors and returns a number, satisfying:
1. **Symmetry**: a . b = b . a
2. **Linearity**: a . (b + c) = a . b + a . c
3. **Positive definiteness**: a . a ≥ 0, with equality only when a = 0

The familiar dot product satisfies all three. But so does:

<MathBlock expression={'\\langle f, g \\rangle = \\int_0^1 f(x) \\, g(x) \\, dx'} />

This is the **L2 inner product** for functions. It measures how much two functions "overlap" — just as the dot product measures how much two arrows align.

## Orthogonal functions

Two functions are orthogonal when their inner product is zero:

<MathBlock expression={'\\langle f, g \\rangle = 0 \\quad \\iff \\quad f \\perp g'} />

This means the integral of their product is zero — the positive and negative areas cancel out perfectly.

The Fourier basis (1, sin x, cos x, sin 2x, cos 2x, …) is an orthogonal basis under this inner product. Fourier analysis is just Gram-Schmidt applied to function space.

## The same geometry, everywhere

Once you have an inner product, you get:
- **Length**: the norm of f is the square root of its self-inner-product
- **Angle**: cos θ = inner product divided by norms
- **Projection**: decompose functions into orthogonal components
- **Best approximation**: find the closest function in a subspace (least squares for functions)

Every geometric idea from the arrow world transfers to the function world.

<KeyInsight>
The inner product generalizes the dot product to any vector space — functions, sequences, polynomials. It gives length, angle, and orthogonality, and with them: projection, decomposition, and best approximation.
</KeyInsight>

**Connections.** The L^2 inner product <f,g> = integral of f(x)g(x)dx generalizes the dot product to functions. Two functions are "orthogonal" when their inner product is zero — just like perpendicular vectors. Fourier series is orthogonal expansion: f(x) = sum of <f, phi_n> * phi_n where phi_n are sines and cosines. This is Parseval's identity for functions: ||f||^2 = sum of |<f, phi_n>|^2.
