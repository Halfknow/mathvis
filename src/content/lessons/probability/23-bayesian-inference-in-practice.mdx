Bayesian updating sounds elegant in theory: multiply prior by likelihood, normalize, done. In practice, the normalization step requires computing an integral — and for most real models, that integral has no closed form. For centuries, this computational wall kept Bayesian methods in the realm of theory.

There are two ways through that wall. The first is **conjugate priors** — when prior and likelihood are matched, the integral solves itself, and updating becomes pure arithmetic. The second is **computation** — Markov Chain Monte Carlo and variational inference, which let you approximate any posterior, no matter how complex the model. This lesson covers both: the elegant special case, and the general engine that powers modern Bayesian inference.

## Conjugate priors: the idea

A prior is **conjugate** to a likelihood if the posterior belongs to the same distribution family as the prior.

<MathInline expression={"\\text{Prior: } p(\\theta) \\in \\mathcal{F} \\quad + \\quad \\text{Likelihood: } p(\\text{data} \\mid \\theta) \\quad \\Longrightarrow \\quad \\text{Posterior: } p(\\theta \\mid \\text{data}) \\in \\mathcal{F}"} />

The family <MathInline expression={"\\mathcal{F}"} /> is preserved. Only the parameters change.

Why does this matter? Because it means you can update your beliefs **analytically** — with algebra, not with numerical integration. Each new data point adjusts the parameters of the distribution, and you can do it sequentially, one observation at a time, without ever going back to the raw data.

Conjugate pairs exist for every distribution in the exponential family (which we develop below):

| Likelihood | Conjugate Prior | Posterior Update |
|---|---|---|
| Binomial (k heads in n flips) | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(\\alpha, \\beta)"} /> | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(\\alpha+k, \\, \\beta+n-k)"} /> |
| Poisson (counts) | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Gamma}(a, b)"} /> | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Gamma}(a+\\sum x_i, \\, b+n)"} /> |
| Normal (mean, known var) | <MathInline expression={"N(\\mu_0, \\sigma_0^2)"} /> | precision-weighted update |
| Normal (variance, known mean) | Inverse-Gamma | Inverse-Gamma |
| Exponential (rate) | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Gamma}(a, b)"} /> | <MathInline expression={"\\text{Gamma}(a+n, \\, b+\\sum x_i)"} /> |
| Multinomial (categories) | Dirichlet | Dirichlet, add counts |

The Beta-Binomial pair is the most intuitive entry point, so that is where we start.

## The Beta distribution

The **Beta distribution** is a family of distributions defined on $[0, 1]$. It is the natural prior for any unknown probability — a coin's fairness, a conversion rate, a success probability.

Its density is:

<MathInline expression={"f(\\theta) = \\frac{\\Gamma(\\alpha + \\beta)}{\\Gamma(\\alpha)\\Gamma(\\beta)} \\theta^{\\alpha - 1} (1 - \\theta)^{\\beta - 1}"} />

Do not let the gamma functions intimidate you. The shape is controlled entirely by two parameters, $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and the geometry is intuitive:

- The **mean** is $\alpha / (\alpha + \beta)$. This is where the peak tends to sit.
- The **concentration** is $\alpha + \beta$. Larger values mean a tighter, more peaked distribution — more certainty.
- When $\alpha = \beta = 1$, you get the **flat** uniform distribution on $[0, 1]$ — complete ignorance.
- When $\alpha = \beta$ and both are large, you get a symmetric bell centered at 0.5, increasingly narrow.
- When $\alpha > \beta$, the mass shifts right. When $\beta > \alpha$, it shifts left.

Think of $\alpha$ as "imaginary heads" and $\beta$ as "imaginary tails." A Beta(3, 3) prior says: "before I saw any data, it is as if I had flipped 6 coins and got 3 heads — I mildly believe the coin is fair, but I am not certain." The more imaginary flips you start with, the stronger your prior conviction.

## The Beta-Binomial update

Here is the beautiful part. Suppose your prior for a coin's probability of heads $\theta$ is <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(\\alpha, \\beta)"} />. You flip the coin $n$ times and observe $k$ heads. The posterior is:

<MathInline expression={"\\theta \\mid \\text{data} \\sim \\text{Beta}(\\alpha + k,\\; \\beta + n - k)"} />

### Theorem: Beta-Binomial conjugacy

If <MathInline expression={"\\theta \\sim \\text{Beta}(\\alpha, \\beta)"} /> and <MathInline expression={"k \\mid \\theta \\sim \\text{Binomial}(n, \\theta)"} />, then:

$$\theta \mid k \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\; \beta + n - k)$$

*Proof sketch.* The Binomial likelihood is <MathInline expression={"\\binom{n}{k} \\theta^k (1-\\theta)^{n-k}"} />. The Beta prior is proportional to <MathInline expression={"\\theta^{\\alpha-1}(1-\\theta)^{\\beta-1}"} />. Multiplying: the posterior is proportional to <MathInline expression={"\\theta^{\\alpha+k-1}(1-\\theta)^{\\beta+n-k-1}"} />, which is exactly the kernel of <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(\\alpha+k, \\beta+n-k)"} />. The normalizing constant is absorbed automatically. The parameters simply add.

That is it. Add the observed heads to $\alpha$. Add the observed tails to $\beta$. The posterior is a Beta distribution with the updated parameters.

The update is **additive** and **sequential**. Each new flip either increments $\alpha$ (if it is a head) or $\beta$ (if it is a tail). You do not need to store the full history — the running sums $\alpha + k$ and $\beta + (n - k)$ are **sufficient statistics**.

## Watching the distribution evolve

Start with a **flat prior**: <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(1, 1)"} />. This is a horizontal line at height 1 across the entire $[0, 1]$ interval. You are saying: "the coin's probability of heads could be anything, and I have no preference."

**Flip 1: heads.** The posterior becomes <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(2, 1)"} />. The distribution tilts to the right — a gentle upward slope from left to right. The peak is at the right edge (the mode is 1). The data says "heads," and the prior had nothing to say, so the posterior follows the data strongly. But this is just one flip. The distribution is still very wide.

**Flip 2: heads.** The posterior becomes <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(3, 1)"} />. The tilt sharpens. More mass piles up near 1. The coin has shown two heads in a row — the distribution is beginning to suspect this is a biased coin, but it is still very uncertain.

**Flip 3: tails.** The posterior becomes <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(3, 2)"} />. Now something interesting happens. The distribution shifts left — the single tail pulls mass away from 1 and toward the center. The peak moves to around 0.75. The shape is no longer a monotone slope; it is a genuine hill with a mode somewhere in the interior.

**Flip 4: heads.** <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(4, 2)"} />. The hill shifts right again, peaking near 0.8.

**Continue flipping...** Each flip nudges the hill. Heads push it right and sharpen it. Tails push it left and sharpen it. After 100 flips with 58 heads, the posterior is <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(59, 43)"} /> — a narrow bell centered at about 0.578. The width of the bell has shrunk dramatically from the flat line you started with.

The pattern is clear: **the distribution sharpens as data accumulates.** Uncertainty shrinks. The peak converges toward the true proportion. The shape evolves from a flat sheet of ignorance into a tight spike of knowledge.

## The exponential family: why conjugacy works

Conjugate priors are not lucky accidents. They exist for a systematic reason: every distribution in the **exponential family** has a conjugate prior derived by matching the likelihood's functional form.

### Theorem: The exponential family

A distribution belongs to the (natural) **exponential family** if its density can be written:

$$p(x \mid \eta) = h(x)\,\exp\!\big(\eta \cdot T(x) - A(\eta)\big)$$

where $\eta$ is the **natural parameter**, $T(x)$ is the **sufficient statistic**, $A(\eta)$ is the **log-partition function** (a normalizer), and $h(x)$ is the base measure.

The sufficient statistic $T(x)$ is remarkable: it is a *summary of the data* that contains everything you need to know about $\eta$. For the Binomial, $T(x) = \sum x_i$ (the total count of heads). For the Normal with known variance, $T(x) = \sum x_i$. For the Poisson, $T(x) = \sum x_i$. You can throw away the raw data and keep only $T(x)$ without losing any information about the parameter — this is the **sufficiency principle**.

The conjugate prior for an exponential family likelihood has the form:

<MathInline expression={"p(\\eta) \\propto \\exp\\big(\\nu \\cdot \\eta - \\tau A(\\eta)\\big)"} />

with hyperparameters $(\nu, \tau)$ that play the role of **prior pseudo-statistics**: $\nu$ is a prior guess at the sufficient statistic, and $\tau$ is the prior sample size (pseudo-count). When data arrives, the posterior updates to $(\nu + \sum T(x_i),\, \tau + n)$ — literally adding the data's sufficient statistic to the prior's. This is *why* every conjugate pair exhibits the "add to the parameter" pattern: the math forces it.

The exponential family includes: Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Geometric, Normal, Exponential, Gamma, Multinomial, Dirichlet, and many more. Almost every model in introductory statistics is in this family — which is why conjugate priors cover almost every common case.

## Credible intervals vs confidence intervals

Once you have the posterior, you can extract a **credible interval** — the Bayesian analog of a confidence interval.

For a 95% credible interval, you find the range $[a, b]$ such that:

<MathInline expression={"P(a \\leq \\theta \\leq b \\mid \\text{data}) = 0.95"} />

This is an area under the posterior curve. You shade the central 95% of the posterior distribution, and the boundaries of that shaded region are your interval.

The interpretation is the one most people intuitively want: **"there is a 95% probability that the true $\theta$ lies in this interval."** This is a valid statement for Bayesians because $\theta$ has a distribution. For frequentists, this statement is meaningless — $\theta$ is a fixed constant, not a random variable. This is the sharpest concrete difference between the two schools, developed in [lesson 21](/courses/probability/21-two-schools-of-thought).

For the Beta posterior, the credible interval can be computed exactly using the quantile function of the Beta distribution. For <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(59, 43)"} />, the 95% credible interval is approximately $[0.484, 0.670]$. You can report: "given the data and a flat prior, there is a 95% probability that the coin's probability of heads is between 0.484 and 0.670."

### Highest posterior density intervals

There is a subtlety. The standard credible interval splits the remaining 5% equally: 2.5% in each tail. But for asymmetric posteriors (like <MathInline expression={"\\text{Beta}(3, 1)"} /> after just a few flips), there is a more natural choice: the **highest posterior density** (HPD) interval. This is the narrowest interval that contains 95% of the posterior mass.

For symmetric posteriors, the HPD interval and the equal-tailed interval are the same. For skewed posteriors, the HPD interval is shorter and shifted toward the peak. It is the tightest summary of where the parameter probably lives.

## When conjugacy breaks: the computational engine

Conjugate priors are elegant but limited. They exist only for the exponential family, and only for single-parameter or carefully structured multi-parameter models. Real models — hierarchical models, mixture models, Bayesian neural networks with millions of parameters — have posteriors that no conjugate prior can tame. For these, the normalization integral is intractable, and Bayesian inference needs computational engines.

### MCMC: Markov Chain Monte Carlo

The breakthrough that made modern Bayesian inference practical is **Markov Chain Monte Carlo**. The idea is profound: instead of computing the posterior analytically, construct a Markov chain whose **stationary distribution** is the posterior, then run the chain and collect samples. After enough steps, the samples approximate the posterior — no integration required.

The most foundational MCMC algorithm is **Metropolis-Hastings**:

1. Start at some parameter value <MathInline expression={"\\theta^{(0)}"} />.
2. Propose a new value $\theta^*$ from a proposal distribution (e.g., a Gaussian centered at the current value).
3. Compute the **acceptance ratio**: <MathInline expression={"r = \\frac{p(\\text{data} \\mid \\theta^*)\\, p(\\theta^*)}{p(\\text{data} \\mid \\theta^{(t)})\\, p(\\theta^{(t)})}"} />.
4. Accept the proposal with probability $\min(1, r)$. If accepted, <MathInline expression={"\\theta^{(t+1)} = \\theta^*"} />; otherwise stay put.
5. Repeat for thousands of iterations.

The magic: the acceptance ratio involves only the **unnormalized** posterior (likelihood times prior) — the nasty normalizing constant <MathInline expression={"p(\\text{data})"} /> cancels in the ratio. This is why MCMC works: it sidesteps the integral that was the computational wall.

### Theorem: MCMC convergence

Under mild conditions (the chain is irreducible, aperiodic, and has the posterior as its stationary distribution — guaranteed by detailed balance), the empirical distribution of MCMC samples converges to the posterior:

$$\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^N f(\theta^{(t)}) \;\xrightarrow{N \to \infty}\; E_{\text{posterior}}[f(\theta)]$$

This is the **ergodic theorem** for Markov chains (see [lesson 25](/courses/probability/25-markov-chains)): time averages converge to ensemble averages. Any posterior expectation — the posterior mean, a credible interval, the probability of a hypothesis — can be estimated by averaging over MCMC samples. This is why MCMC is the universal engine of modern Bayesian inference: it reduces integration to sampling.

Modern variants — Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) — exploit gradient information to propose much more efficient moves, making MCMC practical for high-dimensional models. These power the probabilistic programming languages Stan, PyMC, and NumPyro.

### Variational inference: the scalable alternative

When the model is too large for MCMC (millions of parameters, massive datasets), **variational inference (VI)** offers a faster alternative. Instead of sampling from the posterior, VI turns inference into **optimization**:

1. Choose a tractable family of distributions $q_\phi(\theta)$ parameterized by $\phi$ (e.g., a mean-field Gaussian — independent across dimensions).
2. Find the member of the family closest to the true posterior by minimizing the KL divergence <MathInline expression={"\\text{KL}(q_\\phi \\| p(\\theta \\mid \\text{data}))"} />.
3. Equivalently, maximize the **evidence lower bound (ELBO)**:

<MathInline expression={"\\text{ELBO}(\\phi) = E_{q_\\phi}[\\log p(\\text{data}, \\theta)] - E_{q_\\phi}[\\log q_\\phi(\\theta)]"} />

The ELBO is a lower bound on <MathInline expression={"\\log p(\\text{data})"} /> (the log evidence), and maximizing it minimizes the KL divergence. The first term rewards fitting the data (likelihood); the second term penalizes $q$ for being too far from the prior (a complexity penalty).

VI trades accuracy for speed: the approximate posterior $q_\phi$ will never be exactly the true posterior (because the tractable family rarely contains it), but it can be fit with stochastic gradient descent and scales to enormous models. This is the engine behind variational autoencoders and large-scale Bayesian deep learning.

## Three perspectives on Bayesian computation

1. **As exact arithmetic (conjugate case).** For exponential-family models with conjugate priors, Bayesian inference reduces to updating parameters by adding sufficient statistics. No integrals, no approximation. This is the cleanest expression of Bayesian updating and the right mental model for understanding what Bayesian inference *means*. Limitation: only works for specially matched prior-likelihood pairs.
2. **As sampling (MCMC).** For models without conjugacy, MCMC samples from the posterior by constructing a Markov chain with the right stationary distribution. The samples are the posterior — you compute any quantity you need by averaging over them. Limitation: slow to converge for complex or high-dimensional models, and diagnosing convergence is subtle.
3. **As optimization (variational inference).** VI approximates the posterior with a tractable family, fit by optimizing the ELBO. Faster than MCMC and scales to massive models, but biased — the approximation is never exact, and choosing the variational family is an art. This is the workhorse of large-scale Bayesian deep learning.

## Modern applications

**Probabilistic programming (Stan, PyMC, NumPyro).** These frameworks let you specify a Bayesian model in a few lines of code and automatically fit it with HMC or NUTS. A hierarchical regression, a mixture model, a pharmacokinetic model — all become posterior samples with a single `fit()` call. The MCMC engine runs under the hood, and diagnostics (R-hat, effective sample size) tell you when the chain has converged. This has democratized Bayesian inference: you no longer need to derive a sampler by hand.

**Variational autoencoders (VAEs).** In generative AI, VAEs perform variational inference on the latent variables of a generative model. The encoder network outputs the parameters of $q_\phi(z \mid x)$ (the approximate posterior over the latent code), and training maximizes the ELBO. The prior $p(z)$ is typically a standard normal. VAEs are probabilistic graphical models trained with variational inference — the same machinery as this lesson, applied inside a neural network.

**Bayesian deep learning.** Putting prior distributions over neural network weights and computing the posterior (via variational inference or Laplace approximation) gives **uncertainty estimates** on predictions — critical for safety in self-driving cars, medical diagnosis, and scientific applications where knowing what the model does *not* know matters as much as what it does. Bayesian neural networks treat every weight as a distribution rather than a point estimate.

<KeyInsight>
Bayesian inference has two computational regimes. For exponential-family models with conjugate priors, updating is exact arithmetic — the Beta-Binomial update (add heads to alpha, tails to beta) is the purest illustration: counting with uncertainty. For everything else, Markov Chain Monte Carlo samples from the posterior by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target (sidestepping the intractable normalizing integral), and variational inference turns inference into optimization for scalability. Together these three methods — conjugacy, MCMC, and VI — let Bayesians fit any model they can write down.
</KeyInsight>

The confidence interval [2.1, 5.4] means: the procedure that generated this interval (compute a CI from fresh data) covers the true theta in 95% of repeated samples. Here theta is a fixed unknown constant, and this specific interval is just two numbers — either theta is in it (probability 1) or not (probability 0). The 95% is about the procedure, not about this interval. So only the credible interval licenses the '95% probability' statement. Numerical agreement is common in large samples (when the likelihood dominates and the Bernstein-von Mises theorem makes the posterior approximately normal, centered at the MLE), but the interpretations remain fundamentally distinct."
