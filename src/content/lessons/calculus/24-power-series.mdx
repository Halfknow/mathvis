A power series is a function in disguise. It looks like an infinite polynomial, but inside its radius of convergence, it behaves like any other function -- you can differentiate and integrate it term by term, add and multiply it like a polynomial, and evaluate it to any desired precision. The series defines the function completely within that disk of convergence.

<MathBlock expression={"\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + \\cdots"} />

For each value of $x$, you get a different series. Some converge, some diverge. The set of $x$-values where it converges is the **interval of convergence**.

## The radius of convergence

There exists a number $R$ (possibly 0 or $\infty$) such that:

- $|x - a| < R$: the series **converges absolutely**
- $|x - a| > R$: the series **diverges**
- $|x - a| = R$: **check manually** (the ratio/root test is inconclusive at the boundary)

The interval $(a - R, a + R)$ is the convergence interval. $R$ is the **radius of convergence**, found by:

<MathBlock expression={"R = \\frac{1}{\\lim_{n \\to \\infty} |c_{n+1}/c_n|}"} />

or equivalently by the root test.

## Example: The geometric power series

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{1}{1-x} = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} x^n = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \\cdots \\quad \\text{for } |x| < 1"} />

This is just the geometric series formula with $r = x$. The radius of convergence is $R = 1$. At the boundary $x = \pm 1$, the series diverges.

## Operations on power series

**Differentiation**: Inside the radius of convergence, you can differentiate term-by-term:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}\\left[\\sum c_n x^n\\right] = \\sum n c_n x^{n-1}"} />

**Integration**: Similarly, integrate term-by-term:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int \\left[\\sum c_n x^n\\right] dx = \\sum \\frac{c_n}{n+1} x^{n+1} + C"} />

**Addition and multiplication**: Power series add and multiply like polynomials, within the common interval of convergence.

## Why power series matter

Many important functions are defined by power series:
- $e^x = \sum x^n / n!$ — the exponential function
- $\sin x = \sum (-1)^n x^{2n+1} / (2n+1)!$ — sine
- $\cos x = \sum (-1)^n x^{2n} / (2n)!$ — cosine

These definitions let you compute function values to any desired precision.

<KeyInsight>
A power series is a polynomial with infinitely many terms. It converges inside a radius R centered at a, and defines a function there. You can differentiate and integrate term-by-term within this interval.
</KeyInsight>
