# Matrices Encode Transformations

Last lesson, you saw what a linear transformation looks like: the grid bends, but lines stay lines and the origin stays put. Now the key question: **how do you write down a transformation?**

The answer is deceptively simple. You only need to record where the two basis vectors land. That's it. Four numbers — two for where e1 goes, two for where e2 goes — and you've captured the entire transformation.

## The columns are the destinations

A 2x2 matrix has this structure:

<MathBlock expression={'A = \\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\\\ c & d \\end{bmatrix} = \\bigl[ \\, A\\hat{e}_1 \\;\\;\\; A\\hat{e}_2 \\, \\bigr]'} />

The first column (a, c) is where e1 = (1, 0) lands. The second column (b, d) is where e2 = (0, 1) lands. Once you know where the basis vectors go, every other vector's destination is determined by linearity.

## Watch the basis arrows move

Drag the matrix sliders below. The blue arrow is e1's destination (column 1). The green arrow is e2's destination (column 2). The whole grid follows them — because the grid *is* built from e1 and e2.

## Reading a matrix geometrically

Every matrix becomes readable once you learn to see its columns as arrows:

| Matrix | What the columns tell you | Geometric effect |
|--------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| [[2, 0], [0, 2]] | Both basis arrows doubled in length | Uniform scaling by 2 |
| [[0, -1], [1, 0]] | e1 goes up, e2 goes left | 90° counterclockwise rotation |
| [[1, 1], [0, 1]] | e1 stays, e2 shifts right | Shear (parallelogram tilt) |
| [[1, 0], [0, 0]] | e1 stays, e2 vanishes | Collapse onto x-axis |

Try typing these into the presets above and watch what happens.
