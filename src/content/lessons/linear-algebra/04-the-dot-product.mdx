# The Dot Product

We know how to build vectors (add them, scale them) and move them around. Now let's measure the *relationship* between two vectors. The **dot product** answers one question: **how much do these two arrows point in the same direction?**

## The formula

Take two arrows. The dot product is:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 = \\lVert\\vec{a}\\rVert \\, \\lVert\\vec{b}\\rVert \\, \\cos\\theta'} />

where θ is the angle between them. Match up the components, multiply pairs, add them up.

The sign tells you everything:
- **Positive** (θ < 90°): arrows point roughly the same way — they're aligned
- **Zero** (θ = 90°): arrows are perpendicular — completely unaligned
- **Negative** (θ > 90°): arrows point away from each other — anti-aligned

## Projection — the geometric meaning

The dot product equals the length of **a** times the projection of **b** onto **a**. Think of shining a light perpendicular to **a**: the shadow **b** casts onto **a**'s line is its projection. The dot product scales that shadow by the length of **a**.

This means: **a** · **b** answers "how much of **b** lies in the direction of **a**?" — weighted by **a**'s length.

The dot product works identically in 3D — project one arrow onto another and measure the shadow. The yellow dashed line shows the projection of **b** onto **a**.

## Three ways to read the dot product

The dot product has three equivalent definitions, and each one is the "right" one in a different context:

1. **Geometric**: a·b = ‖a‖‖b‖cos θ — the product of magnitudes times the cosine of the angle between them. This tells you about alignment.

2. **Algebraic**: a·b = a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ — the sum of component-wise products. This is what you compute.

3. **Projection**: a·b = ‖a‖ × (component of b along a) — the length of a times the projection of b onto a. This measures how much of b lives in a's direction.

All three give the same number. The geometric definition reveals the angle, the algebraic definition is what you compute, and the projection definition is the one that powers everything in machine learning.

## The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

One of the most important inequalities in all of mathematics:

The absolute value of the dot product never exceeds the product of lengths: |a·b| ≤ ‖a‖‖b‖, with equality if and only if a and b point in the same (or opposite) direction.

Geometrically: since cos θ ranges from -1 to 1, the dot product a·b = ‖a‖‖b‖cos θ is bounded by ‖a‖‖b‖. The maximum alignment is parallel, the minimum is anti-parallel.

This inequality is the reason why correlation coefficients are between -1 and 1, why Heisenberg's uncertainty principle holds, and why you can bound the error in approximations.

## Orthogonality as a test

The dot product is the **algebraic test for perpendicularity**: a·b = 0 if and only if a and b are perpendicular (or one is zero). No angles needed — just compute a sum and check if it's zero.

This is why "orthogonal" and "dot product equals zero" are interchangeable. In higher dimensions, where you can't just look at an angle, the dot product is the *only* practical way to check perpendicularity.

## Connections

The dot product is the foundation for everything in module 5: projection (lesson 10) is built from dot products, Gram-Schmidt (lesson 24) repeatedly computes dot products to subtract projections, and PCA (lesson 29) measures variance through dot products. The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality underpins all of these — it guarantees that projections are always smaller than the original vector.

In machine learning, attention mechanisms in transformers compute dot products between query and key vectors. "How aligned is this query with that key?" is literally a dot product question — the same geometric operation you see here.
