Imagine you are at a restaurant. The menu has 3 appetizers, 4 main courses, and 2 desserts. How many different complete meals can you build?

You could try listing them all — appetizer 1 with main 1 with dessert 1, then appetizer 1 with main 1 with dessert 2, and so on. That works, but it gets painful fast. Add a fourth course with 5 options and suddenly you are staring at 120 combinations. What we need is a *system* — a way to count without missing anything and without double-counting.

This lesson is about that system. Counting is the foundation underneath every probability calculation. Before you can ask "how likely?" you must first ask "how many?"

## The multiplication principle

Here is the key insight behind all of counting: **when choices are independent, multiply.**

Think of it as a tree. At the first branch, you pick an appetizer — 3 ways to go. At the second branch, you pick a main — 4 ways to go from *each* of those 3 branches. At the third branch, you pick a dessert — 2 ways from *each* of those. The branches split and split again.

$$3 \times 4 \times 2 = 24 \text{ meals}$$

The tree picture is what makes this click. You are not adding branches — you are multiplying because each new choice multiplies the number of paths so far. If you add a fourth course with 5 options, the tree grows by a factor of 5:

$$3 \times 4 \times 2 \times 5 = 120 \text{ meals}$$

This is the **multiplication principle**: if a task has $r$ stages, and stage $i$ has $n_i$ choices regardless of what was picked before, then the total number of outcomes is $n_1 \times n_2 \times \cdots \times n_r$.

The "regardless of what was picked before" part matters. If the dessert options changed depending on which main you ordered, the simple multiplication breaks down — you would need to draw the tree and count branches case by case.

## Permutations: when order matters

Five runners cross the finish line. Gold, silver, bronze go to the top three. How many different ways can the medals be awarded?

Picture three empty slots on a podium:

$$\underline{\quad} \quad \underline{\quad} \quad \underline{\quad}$$

For the first slot (gold), any of the 5 runners could be there. Once gold is decided, 4 runners remain for silver. Once silver is decided, 3 runners remain for bronze.

$$5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60 \text{ medal arrangements}$$

This is a **permutation** — an arrangement where order matters. Getting gold is not the same as getting bronze, so the arrangement "Alice, Bob, Carol" is different from "Carol, Alice, Bob."

The general pattern: arranging $k$ objects selected from $n$ total gives:

$$P(n, k) = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times (n-k+1) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$$

When $k = n$ — arranging all $n$ objects — this simplifies to $n!$ (n factorial). Five runners in all five positions: $5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120$ orderings.

The factorial $n!$ is the "multiplier that keeps shrinking" — each slot has one fewer option than the one before it, because one more object has already been placed.

## Combinations: when order does not matter

Now a different question. You have 5 friends and need to choose 2 for a team. Alice-and-Bob is the same team as Bob-and-Alice — order does not matter.

Start with what we know. The number of *ordered* pairs from 5 people is:

$$5 \times 4 = 20$$

But every pair of people was counted twice — once as Alice-then-Bob, once as Bob-then-Alice. The 20 ordered pairs overcount by a factor of 2. So divide:

$$\frac{20}{2} = 10 \text{ teams}$$

This is the core idea behind **combinations**. Start with permutations, then divide away the overcounting.

For choosing $k$ objects from $n$ when order does not matter, each group of $k$ objects was counted $k!$ times (once for each possible ordering of that group). So divide:

$$C(n, k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k! \times (n-k)!}$$

The notation $\binom{n}{k}$ is read "n choose k." It counts the number of ways to grab $k$ items from a pile of $n$, where the grab-bag is what matters, not the sequence in which you grabbed them.

Why does $k!$ appear in the denominator? Because $k$ objects can be arranged in $k!$ ways. The permutation counted every one of those arrangements separately. Dividing by $k!$ collapses them back into a single group.

## Connecting counting to probability

Classical probability — the $k/n$ ratio from the previous lesson — requires you to count both the favorable outcomes $k$ and the total outcomes $n$. That is where all of this leads.

A standard deck has 52 cards. What is the probability of drawing a heart?

Total outcomes: 52. Favorable outcomes: 13 (one for each heart). The probability is $13/52 = 1/4$.

What about drawing 2 hearts from a full deck? Now you need combinations.

Total ways to choose 2 cards from 52:

$$\binom{52}{2} = \frac{52!}{2! \times 50!} = \frac{52 \times 51}{2} = 1326$$

Favorable ways (both are hearts, chosen from 13):

$$\binom{13}{2} = \frac{13!}{2! \times 11!} = \frac{13 \times 12}{2} = 78$$

$$P(\text{two hearts}) = \frac{78}{1326} = \frac{1}{17}$$

The counting is the hard part. Once you have the numerator and denominator, the probability is just division.

## Key insight

> Counting is not about memorizing formulas — it is about seeing the structure. Permutations count sequences (the slots shrink). Combinations count groups (each group was overcounted by every possible reordering). The multiplication principle is the engine underneath both.

## Practice

1. A password must be exactly 4 characters long. Each character is a lowercase letter (a–z). How many possible passwords are there? What if the password must contain 4 *different* letters?

2. A committee of 3 people is chosen from a group of 8. Two of the 8 are siblings. How many committees include both siblings? How many exclude at least one of them?

3. A lock has 3 dials, each showing digits 0–9. The lock opens with one specific combination. Someone tries a random combination — what is the probability it opens? What changes if no two dials can show the same digit?
