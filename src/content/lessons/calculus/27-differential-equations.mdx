A differential equation says: "at every point $(x, y)$, here's the slope of the solution curve." Your job is to find the function whose derivative matches that rule. Picture a landscape of arrows: at each point $(x, y)$, the equation tells you which direction to walk. Solution curves are the paths that follow the arrows -- they flow through the field like leaves in a river, each one tracing out a valid solution.

## The slope field

For a first-order ODE $dy/dx = f(x, y)$, the **slope field** (or direction field) draws a small tick mark at every grid point showing the required slope. The solution curves must follow these tick marks — they flow along the field like leaves in a river.

Try each equation:
- $dy/dx = y$: solution curves grow exponentially. The ticks all point upward on the right (positive $y$ grows) and downward on the left (negative $y$ decays).
- $dy/dx = -x/y$: solution curves are circles centered at the origin. The field rotates around the center.
- $dy/dx = x + y$: curves that start negative get pulled positive by the $+y$ term, then accelerate upward.

## Separable equations

The simplest type: $dy/dx = g(x) \cdot h(y)$. Separate variables and integrate:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int \\frac{dy}{h(y)} = \\int g(x)\\, dx + C"} />

### Example: $dy/dx = xy$

Separate: $\frac{dy}{y} = x\, dx$. Integrate: $\ln|y| = x^2/2 + C$. Solve: $y = Ae^{x^2/2}$.

## First-order linear ODEs

The form $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)$ can always be solved using an **integrating factor**:

<MathBlock expression={"\\mu(x) = e^{\\int P(x)\\, dx}"} />

Multiply through by $\mu(x)$ and the left side becomes a perfect derivative:

<MathBlock expression={"\\frac{d}{dx}[\\mu(x) \\cdot y] = \\mu(x) \\cdot Q(x)"} />

Integrate both sides to get the solution.

## Why differential equations matter

DEs are the language of nature:
- **Newton's law**: $F = ma$ is a second-order ODE for position.
- **Population growth**: $dP/dt = kP$ gives exponential growth.
- **Cooling**: $dT/dt = -k(T - T_{env})$ gives Newton's law of cooling.
- **Circuits**: $L \cdot dI/dt + RI = V$ governs R-L circuits.
