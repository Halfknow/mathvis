# The Cross Product

In 2D, the dot product measures alignment. In 3D, there's a second operation: the **cross product** takes two vectors and produces a third one, perpendicular to both.

## The geometric picture

Given two 3D vectors **a** and **b**, the cross product **a** x **b** is a vector that:
- Points perpendicular to both **a** and **b** (along the normal to the parallelogram they form)
- Has length equal to the area of that parallelogram
- Follows the right-hand rule: curl from **a** to **b**, and your thumb points along **a** x **b**

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b} = \\lVert\\vec{a}\\rVert \\, \\lVert\\vec{b}\\rVert \\, \\sin\\theta \\, \\hat{n}'} />

### The right-hand rule

Point your index finger along **a**, curl your fingers toward **b**, and your thumb points along **a** x **b**. This convention is what gives the cross product its direction — there are two perpendicular directions (up and down from the parallelogram's plane), and the right-hand rule picks one. Swap the order of **a** and **b**, and the cross product flips to the other side: **a** x **b** = -(**b** x **a**).

Key properties:
- **a** x **a** = **0** (parallel vectors span no area)
- **a** x **b** = -(**b** x **a**) (order flips the direction)
- |**a** x **b**| = 0 when **a** and **b** are parallel

### Why perpendicular?

The cross product must be perpendicular to both inputs because it encodes the *area* of the parallelogram, not the *shape*. Two pairs of vectors that span the same parallelogram (same area, same plane) give the same cross product. The length captures "how much area" and the direction captures "which plane." Only a perpendicular direction can encode the plane uniquely — any direction in the plane would depend on the specific vectors, not just the area they span.

## Computation

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b} = \\begin{vmatrix} \\hat{i} & \\hat{j} & \\hat{k} \\\\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\\\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\end{vmatrix}'} />

This determinant formula is a memory device — it gives the three components of the cross product.

## The triple product

There's a deeper connection between the cross product and determinants. Take three vectors **a**, **b**, **c** and form:

<MathBlock expression={'\\vec{a} \\cdot (\\vec{b} \\times \\vec{c}) = \\begin{vmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\\\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \\\\ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \\end{vmatrix}'} />

This **scalar triple product** gives the signed volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three vectors. It is exactly the determinant of the 3x3 matrix whose rows are **a**, **b**, **c**.

The geometric story: the cross product **b** x **c** gives you the "area direction" of the base parallelogram (perpendicular arrow with length = area). Then dotting with **a** projects **a** onto that perpendicular direction, giving the height. Area times height = volume.

If the triple product is zero, the three vectors lie in the same plane — they are linearly dependent.

<KeyInsight>
The cross product builds a perpendicular arrow whose length equals the parallelogram area. The triple product a · (b × c) connects it to the determinant — the signed volume of the parallelepiped. Together, they link area, volume, and orientation in one formula.
</KeyInsight>

**Connections.** The cross product a x b is a determinant in disguise: the 3x3 matrix [[i,j,k],[a1,a2,a3],[b1,b2,b3]] has determinant equal to a x b. The scalar triple product a . (b x c) = det[a b c] gives the signed volume of the parallelepiped — it's positive if (a,b,c) form a right-handed system. Surface normals in computer graphics are computed via cross products of edge vectors.
