# QR Decomposition

You've seen Gram-Schmidt straighten a basis into an orthogonal one — subtracting projections one at a time until every vector stands perpendicular. That process is elegant, but it lives in the world of "vectors and operations."

Here's the deeper view: **Gram-Schmidt is a matrix factorization in disguise.** Every matrix A with independent columns can be factored as A = QR, where Q is the orthonormal basis Gram-Schmidt produces and R records exactly how to rebuild the original columns from Q. The payoff is enormous — this single equation gives a numerically stable way to solve linear systems, compute least squares, and find eigenvalues.

## The factorization

Take a matrix A with independent columns. Write:

<MathBlock expression={'A = QR'} />

Two pieces:

- **Q** has orthonormal columns — these are the unit vectors Gram-Schmidt produces. Think of Q as the "clean directions."
- **R** is upper triangular — these are the coefficients that rebuild A's columns from Q's columns. Think of R as the "recipe."

Why is R upper triangular? Because Gram-Schmidt builds each new orthogonal direction using only the *previous* vectors. The second column of A depends on q1 and q2 but not q3. The third column depends on q1, q2, and q3 but not q4. This one-way dependency is exactly what makes R upper triangular — zeros below the diagonal.

## How R captures Gram-Schmidt

The k-th column of A is a linear combination of the first k columns of Q:

<MathBlock expression={'\\mathbf{a}_k = r_{1k}\\,\\mathbf{q}_1 + r_{2k}\\,\\mathbf{q}_2 + \\cdots + r_{kk}\\,\\mathbf{q}_k'} />

The coefficients are dot products. Since Q's columns are orthonormal, taking the dot product of both sides with <MathInline expression={'\\mathbf{q}_i'} /> picks out exactly one term:

<MathBlock expression={'r_{ik} = \\mathbf{q}_i \\cdot \\mathbf{a}_k'} />

In matrix form this is beautifully compact:

<MathBlock expression={'R = Q^T A'} />

The upper-triangular structure of R is a direct consequence of Gram-Schmidt's sequential construction. When you compute <MathInline expression={'r_{ik} = \\mathbf{q}_i \\cdot \\mathbf{a}_k'} /> for i > k, you get zero — because <MathInline expression={'\\mathbf{a}_k'} /> has already been cleaned of any component along <MathInline expression={'\\mathbf{q}_i'} />. The zeros below the diagonal aren't coincidental; they're the signature of an orthogonalization process.

## Solving Ax = b via QR

Here is where QR earns its keep. To solve Ax = b:

**Step 1: Compute QR.** Factor A into Q and R.

**Step 2: Transform the system.** Substitute A = QR:

<MathBlock expression={'QR\\mathbf{x} = \\mathbf{b}'} />

Multiply both sides by <MathInline expression={'Q^T'} />. Because Q has orthonormal columns, <MathInline expression={'Q^T Q = I'} />, so the left side simplifies:

<MathBlock expression={'R\\mathbf{x} = Q^T \\mathbf{b}'} />

**Step 3: Back-substitute.** R is upper triangular, so solve from the bottom row up. No matrix inversion. No Gaussian elimination. Just dot products and back-substitution.

This is faster and more stable than computing <MathInline expression={'A^{-1}'} />. For least squares problems (when A is tall and thin), the same approach gives the normal equations in their most stable form: <MathInline expression={'R\\mathbf{x} = Q^T \\mathbf{b}'} />.

## Numerical stability

Classical Gram-Schmidt — the version that subtracts all projections at once — loses orthogonality in finite-precision arithmetic. The rounding errors accumulate, and the computed Q drifts away from being truly orthogonal. For a 10x10 matrix, the loss can be visible. For a 100x100 matrix, it's catastrophic.

**Modified Gram-Schmidt** fixes this by subtracting projections one at a time, updating after each step. It's the same mathematics but reorganized to minimize rounding error.

In practice, numerical software goes further. **Householder reflections** — the method used by LAPACK, NumPy, and MATLAB — doesn't build Q column by column at all. Instead it reflects the entire matrix through a sequence of carefully chosen mirrors, sweeping A into upper-triangular form. The result is the same A = QR, but the orthogonality of Q is preserved to machine precision.

The key takeaway: when you call `numpy.linalg.lstsq` or `numpy.linalg.solve`, QR decomposition is doing the heavy lifting internally. You don't see it, but it's there — making sure your answer has as many correct digits as possible.

## Connections

QR decomposition connects the abstract Gram-Schmidt process to practical computation. It's the bridge between "here's how to orthogonalize vectors by hand" and "here's how a computer solves linear systems without losing accuracy."

QR is also the backbone of the **QR algorithm** for computing eigenvalues — widely regarded as one of the most important numerical algorithms in linear algebra. The idea is deceptively simple: factor A = QR, then form A' = RQ, then repeat. The sequence converges to a matrix whose eigenvalues sit on the diagonal. Without QR decomposition, there would be no reliable way to compute eigenvalues of large matrices.
