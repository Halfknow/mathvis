The definite integral gives the area under a single curve. What about the area **between** two curves? Simple: subtract. The area between curves is just "top minus bottom" integrated across the interval. At each x-position, measure the vertical gap between the two functions, then let the integral accumulate all those gaps. But be careful when curves cross -- the top and bottom swap roles, and you must split the integral at each crossing.

## The formula

If $f(x) \geq g(x)$ on $[a, b]$, then the area between them is:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Area} = \\int_a^b [f(x) - g(x)]\\, dx"} />

The integrand $f(x) - g(x)$ is the vertical gap between the curves at each point. Integrate the gap and you get the total area between them.

Switch to "sin vs cos" and notice how the curves cross. Between intersections, sin is above cos; outside those crossings, the relationship flips. The formula only works when you know which function is on top.

## When curves cross

If the curves cross within $[a, b]$, you must split the integral at each crossing point:

<MathBlock expression={"\\text{Area} = \\int_a^c [f(x) - g(x)]\\, dx + \\int_c^b [g(x) - f(x)]\\, dx"} />

The absolute value is implicit: always subtract the lower from the upper.

## Finding the bounds

The bounds $a$ and $b$ are often the intersection points of the two curves. To find them, solve $f(x) = g(x)$.

For $y = \sin(x)$ and $y = \cos(x)$: $\sin(x) = \cos(x)$ when $x = \pi/4 + n\pi$.
