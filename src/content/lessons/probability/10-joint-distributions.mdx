So far we have studied one random variable at a time — a single landscape of probability, a single curve. But the real world rarely deals in isolation. Height and weight arrive together. A stock's price today and tomorrow are entangled. Two dice land simultaneously. When two random variables share the same probability space, we need a map that shows them *both* at once.

That map is the **joint distribution**. It tells you the probability of every possible *pair* of outcomes — not just how $X$ behaves on its own, and not just how $Y$ behaves on its own, but how they behave *together*. This is the leap from one dimension to two, and it is the gateway to every multivariate idea in statistics and machine learning.

## The discrete case: a table of pairs

Suppose you roll two dice. Call the first die $X$ and the second $Y$. Each die has six faces, so there are $6 \times 6 = 36$ possible pairs. The **joint probability mass function** (joint PMF) assigns a probability to each cell in a grid:

$<MathInline expression={"p(x_i, y_j) = P(X = x_i \\text{ and } Y = y_j)"} />$

For two fair dice, every cell gets $1/36$. The table is flat — no cell is brighter than any other. But now suppose the dice are loaded, or suppose $X$ and $Y$ are not dice at all but something entangled — say, daily temperature and ice cream sales. Now some cells glow bright (hot day, lots of ice cream) and others are nearly dark (hot day, zero ice cream). The pattern of brightness across the grid *is* the joint distribution.

### Theorem: Valid joint PMFs

A function $p(x_i, y_j)$ is a valid joint PMF if and only if it satisfies two conditions:

1. **Non-negativity:** $p(x_i, y_j) \geq 0$ for every pair $(x_i, y_j)$.
2. **Normalization:** the total mass over all pairs is exactly 1.

$$\sum_i \sum_j p(x_i, y_j) = 1$$

The intuition is the probability axiom "total probability is 1," applied to the sample space of all *pairs* of outcomes. Every probability mass function, no matter how many variables, must be a non-negative thing that sums to one.

### A fully worked joint PMF table

Let $X$ be the number of heads in two coin flips, and let $Y$ be the number of heads on the first flip alone. The four equally likely outcomes are HH, HT, TH, TT (each with probability $1/4$). The joint PMF:

| | Y = 0 | Y = 1 | $p_X(x)$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| **X = 0** | 1/4 (TT) | 0 | 1/4 |
| **X = 1** | 1/4 (TH) | 1/4 (HT) | 1/2 |
| **X = 2** | 0 | 1/4 (HH) | 1/4 |
| **$p_Y(y)$** | 1/2 | 1/2 | 1 |

Check normalization: $1/4 + 0 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 0 + 1/4 = 1$. Good. The zeros tell you something structural: if the first flip is heads ($Y = 1$), then $X$ cannot be 0 (you already have one head). And if the first flip is tails ($Y = 0$), then $X$ cannot be 2. The pattern of zeros and non-zeros in the grid is the fingerprint of the relationship between $X$ and $Y$. The row and column totals in the margins are the **marginal distributions**, which we will study in detail in the next lesson.

## The continuous case: a surface over the plane

When $X$ and $Y$ are continuous, the table becomes a smooth surface. The **joint probability density function** (joint PDF) $f(x, y)$ is a landscape stretched over the $xy$-plane. There are hills where pairs are likely and valleys where pairs are rare.

$$P(a \leq X \leq b, \; c \leq Y \leq d) = \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x, y)\,dx\,dy$$

The probability of a rectangular region is the **volume** under the surface — just as the probability for one continuous variable was the *area* under its curve. We have gone from a 2D picture (one variable) to a 3D picture (two variables). The total volume under the entire surface is 1.

### The support region

A key new idea in the continuous case is the **support** — the region of the $xy$-plane where the density is positive. For a joint PDF, the support can be a rectangle (like $0 \leq x \leq 1$, $0 \leq y \leq 1$), but it can also be a triangle, a disk, or any other shape. The shape of the support already tells you a great deal about how the variables relate.

Consider $f(x, y) = 2$ on the triangle $0 \leq y \leq x \leq 1$, and zero elsewhere. The support is the region *below* the line $y = x$. This triangular support encodes a relationship: $Y$ can never exceed $X$. The variables are entangled purely through the geometry of where they are allowed to live, before we even look at the height of the density. Verifying normalization:

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^x 2\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1 2x\,dx = \left[x^2\right]_0^1 = 1$$

Good — this is a valid joint PDF.

### Theorem: Valid joint PDFs

A function $f(x, y)$ is a valid joint PDF if and only if:

1. **Non-negativity:** $f(x, y) \geq 0$ for all $(x, y)$.
2. **Normalization:** the total integral over the entire plane is exactly 1.

$<MathInline expression={"\\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} \\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f(x, y)\\,dx\\,dy = 1"} />$

This is the continuous analog of the PMF normalization condition. The density can be zero outside the support, but wherever it is positive, its total volume must be 1.

## The geometry of concentration

Where the mass concentrates tells you how $X$ and $Y$ relate. If the bright ridge runs from lower-left to upper-right, large values of $X$ tend to accompany large values of $Y$. If the ridge runs from upper-left to lower-right, large $X$ pairs with small $Y$. If the bright spot sits in a circular blob near the center, the two variables have little to do with each other.

This ridge direction is the visual signature of dependence. In the next lessons we will give it precise names — covariance, correlation, conditional distributions. But the raw geometry comes first. Before you compute anything, look at the landscape. Where is the mass?

## The multivariate normal and the covariance matrix

The most important joint distribution in all of statistics is the **multivariate normal** (or multivariate Gaussian). For two variables, its density is a smooth bell-shaped hill over the $xy$-plane:

$<MathInline expression={"f(\\mathbf{x}) = \\frac{1}{2\\pi\\,|\\Sigma|^{1/2}} \\exp\\!\\left(-\\tfrac{1}{2}(\\mathbf{x} - \\boldsymbol{\\mu})^\\top \\Sigma^{-1} (\\mathbf{x} - \\boldsymbol{\\mu})\\right)"} />$

where <MathInline expression={"\\mathbf{x} = (x, y)^\\top"} />, <MathInline expression={"\\boldsymbol{\\mu} = (\\mu_X, \\mu_Y)^\\top"} /> is the vector of means, and $\Sigma$ is the **covariance matrix**:

$<MathInline expression={"\\Sigma = \\begin{pmatrix} \\text{Var}(X) & \\text{Cov}(X, Y) \\\\ \\text{Cov}(X, Y) & \\text{Var}(Y) \\end{pmatrix}"} />$

The level curves of this density are **ellipses**. The shape of those ellipses — their orientation, eccentricity, and size — is completely determined by $\Sigma$. This matrix is the central object in all of multivariate statistics, and we will devote the next two lessons to unpacking its entries: the variances on the diagonal and the covariance off the diagonal. For now, the key takeaway is this: the joint distribution of two correlated normal variables is an elliptical hill, and that ellipse *is* the geometric picture of the covariance matrix.

## Three perspectives on joint distributions

1. **Tabular (discrete):** the joint PMF is a grid of probabilities. Each cell is $P(X = x_i, Y = y_j)$, the cells sum to 1, and the pattern of zeros and bright spots encodes the relationship. This is the cleanest mental model — start here.
2. **Geometric (continuous):** the joint PDF is a surface over the $xy$-plane, and probabilities are volumes under that surface. The **support region** (where the density is positive) and the **ridges** (where mass concentrates) are the two geometric features that matter most.
3. **Algebraic (the covariance matrix):** for the multivariate normal, the entire shape of the joint distribution is captured by a single matrix $\Sigma$. This is the viewpoint that scales to high dimensions and powers modern statistics and machine learning.

## Modern applications

**Gaussian mixture models (GMMs).** Not every joint distribution is a single bell. A Gaussian mixture model expresses a joint distribution as a weighted sum of several multivariate Gaussians, each with its own mean and covariance matrix: <MathInline expression={"f(\\mathbf{x}) = \\sum_k \\pi_k \\, \\mathcal{N}(\\mathbf{x}; \\boldsymbol{\\mu}_k, \\Sigma_k)"} />. This produces a landscape with multiple hills — perfect for modeling data that clusters into several groups (different customer segments, different cell types in genomics, different topics in a document). The parameters are fit by the **EM algorithm** (Expectation-Maximization), which alternates between guessing which cluster each point belongs to (the E-step) and refitting the Gaussians (the M-step). GMMs are the backbone of unsupervised clustering, speaker identification, and density estimation.

**Multivariate statistics and PCA.** The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is the raw material of every dimensionality-reduction technique. **Principal Component Analysis** (PCA) diagonalizes $\Sigma$ — its eigenvectors point along the principal axes of the data cloud, and its eigenvalues measure the spread along those axes. The first principal component is the direction of maximum variance, which is exactly the long axis of the joint distribution's ellipse. This is how we compress high-dimensional data, denoise signals, and find structure in genetics, finance, and image processing.

**Joint distributions in generative AI.** Modern generative models — variational autoencoders (VAEs), normalizing flows, diffusion models — are all, at their core, ways of learning and sampling from complex high-dimensional joint distributions. A diffusion model learns the joint distribution of an image and its successive noise-corrupted versions; the reverse process samples from this joint to generate new images. The multivariate Gaussian appears at every step as both the noise source and the variational approximation.

<KeyInsight>
A joint distribution is a landscape of pairs — a heat map (discrete) or a surface (continuous) over the $xy$-plane, with total mass 1. Its two defining features are the support region (where pairs are even possible) and the ridges of concentration (where pairs are likely). For the multivariate normal, this entire landscape is encoded in the covariance matrix $\Sigma$, whose diagonal entries are variances and whose off-diagonal entry is the covariance — the geometric blueprint of the elliptical hill.
</KeyInsight>
