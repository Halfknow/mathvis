You have a default assumption about the world — a drug does not work, a coin is fair, a factory produces within specifications. Then you collect data. The data might be perfectly consistent with your assumption, or it might look surprising under that assumption. How surprising does the data need to be before you abandon the default?

**Hypothesis testing** gives you a framework for making that decision. It is a probabilistic version of proof by contradiction: assume the default, check whether the data makes that assumption look absurd, and if it does, reject the default. The catch is that "absurd" is not binary — it is a matter of degree, and you must choose where to draw the line.

This lesson formalizes that framework and confronts its sharpest difficulties: the two types of error (false positives and false negatives), the widely misunderstood p-value, the concept of statistical power, and the **multiple testing problem** — the fact that if you run enough tests, some will appear significant purely by chance. We close with the frequentist-vs-Bayesian critique, the great philosophical debate that has shaped statistics for a century.

## Null and alternative hypotheses

The **null hypothesis** $H_0$ is the default assumption — the status quo, the boring explanation, the "nothing is going on" scenario. Examples: the mean is 100, the coin is fair, the drug has no effect.

The **alternative hypothesis** $H_1$ is what you suspect might be true instead. Examples: the mean is not 100, the coin is biased, the drug does have an effect.

The asymmetry is deliberate. $H_0$ gets the benefit of the doubt. You need evidence — strong evidence — to overthrow it. Think of $H_0$ as the defendant in a trial: innocent until proven guilty.

## The test statistic and the p-value

You compute a **test statistic** from your data — a number that measures how far the data falls from what $H_0$ predicts. The further the test statistic from zero (or from whatever value $H_0$ implies), the more surprising the data is under $H_0$.

The **p-value** is the probability of seeing data at least as extreme as what you observed, assuming $H_0$ is true:

$<MathInline expression={"p\\text{-value} = P(\\text{data this extreme or more} \\mid H_0)"} />$

A small p-value means: "if $H_0$ were true, data this extreme would be rare." That is evidence against $H_0$.

A large p-value means: "data like this is perfectly normal under $H_0$." That is not evidence *for* $H_0$ — it just means you lack the evidence to reject it. **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.**

### Theorem: definition and distribution of the p-value

Under $H_0$, if the test statistic $T$ has CDF $F_0$, the p-value $p = P_{H_0}(T \geq t_{\text{obs}})$ (for a one-sided test) is uniformly distributed on $[0, 1]$. That is, $p \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ when $H_0$ is true.

*Intuition.* If $H_0$ is true, the p-value is a random draw from the uniform distribution. So a p-value below 0.05 happens exactly 5% of the time — by construction. This is why the 5% threshold gives a 5% false-positive rate: under $H_0$, 5% of p-values fall below 0.05 purely by chance.

### The misinterpretation of the p-value

The p-value is the most misunderstood quantity in statistics. The common (wrong) interpretations:

- **Wrong:** "The p-value is the probability that $H_0$ is true." (It is not — $H_0$ is either true or false; there is no probability.)
- **Wrong:** "A p-value of 0.04 means there is a 96% chance the alternative is true." (It does not — the p-value is computed *assuming* $H_0$ is true; it says nothing about the probability of $H_1$.)
- **Wrong:** "A p-value of 0.20 means $H_0$ is probably true." (It does not — it just means the data is not surprising under $H_0$, which could also happen if $H_1$ is true but the effect is small or the sample is small.)

The **correct** interpretation: the p-value is the probability of observing data *at least as extreme* as what you got, *if $H_0$ were true*. It is a statement about the data under a hypothetical, not a statement about the truth of the hypothesis.

## Drawing the line: significance level

You choose a **significance level** $\alpha$ *before* looking at the data. Common values: $\alpha = 0.05$ (5%) or $\alpha = 0.01$ (1%). The rule is simple:

- If <MathInline expression={"p\\text{-value} \\leq \\alpha"} />: reject $H_0$. The data is too extreme to be compatible with the null.
- If <MathInline expression={"p\\text{-value} > \\alpha"} />: fail to reject $H_0$. The data is not extreme enough to overthrow the default.

The significance level is the threshold for how much surprise you are willing to tolerate before changing your mind. A strict threshold ($\alpha = 0.01$) demands overwhelming evidence. A lenient threshold ($\alpha = 0.10$) is more easily convinced.

## Two kinds of error

Every decision under uncertainty can be wrong. There are exactly two ways to make a mistake:

**Type I error (false positive):** You reject $H_0$ when it is actually true. You convict an innocent defendant. You declare the drug effective when it is not. The probability of this error is $\alpha$, the significance level. You control it directly by choosing the threshold.

**Type II error (false negative):** You fail to reject $H_0$ when $H_1$ is actually true. You let a guilty defendant go free. You miss a truly effective drug. The probability of this error is $\beta$.

The **power** of a test is $1 - \beta$ — the probability of correctly detecting a real effect. High power means the test is sensitive enough to catch the signal when it is there.

### Theorem: the relationship between alpha, beta, and power

For a test of $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$ vs $H_1: \theta = \theta_1$ at significance level $\alpha$:

- $P(\text{Type I error}) = P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ true}) = \alpha$ (controlled by the critical value).
- $P(\text{Type II error}) = P(\text{fail to reject } H_0 \mid H_1 \text{ true}) = \beta$ (depends on $\theta_1$, $n$, and $\alpha$).
- **Power** $= 1 - \beta = P(\text{reject } H_0 \mid H_1 \text{ true})$.

*The fundamental tradeoff.* For fixed sample size $n$, decreasing $\alpha$ (stricter significance) increases $\beta$ (more missed detections) and vice versa. The only way to reduce both errors simultaneously is to increase $n$.

## The tradeoff

Here is the fundamental tension: reducing one type of error increases the other.

Picture two overlapping bell curves on the same axis. The left curve is the sampling distribution under $H_0$. The right curve is the sampling distribution under $H_1$. The **critical value** is the boundary between "fail to reject" and "reject" — a vertical line on the number line.

- $\alpha$ is the area in the right tail of the $H_0$ curve, beyond the critical value. It is the probability of a false alarm.
- $\beta$ is the area in the left tail of the $H_1$ curve, before the critical value. It is the probability of a missed detection.

Now slide the critical value to the right. $\alpha$ shrinks (fewer false alarms) but $\beta$ grows (more missed detections). You become harder to convince, but you also miss more real effects. Slide it to the left and the reverse happens.

The only way to reduce both errors simultaneously is to collect more data. More data pulls the two curves apart — $H_0$ and $H_1$ distributions separate from each other (because the standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ shrinks) — so the overlap shrinks and both tails get smaller.

## A concrete example

You suspect a coin is biased. Your null hypothesis: $H_0: p = 0.5$ (fair coin). You flip it 100 times and observe 62 heads.

Under $H_0$, the number of heads follows a binomial distribution with mean 50 and standard deviation 5. The observed 62 is 2.4 standard deviations above the mean. The p-value is the probability of seeing 62 or more heads under a fair coin — about 0.011.

At $\alpha = 0.05$, you reject $H_0$: the coin appears biased. At $\alpha = 0.01$, you would fail to reject — the evidence is strong but not overwhelming.

Notice how the threshold matters. The data is the same. The p-value is the same. The conclusion changes depending on how strict you decided to be before seeing the data.

## The multiple testing problem

Here is a subtle and dangerous trap. If you run a single test at $\alpha = 0.05$, the probability of a false positive is 5%. But if you run *many* tests, the probability that *at least one* of them produces a false positive grows rapidly.

Suppose you run $m$ independent tests, all under true null hypotheses. The probability that at least one is falsely significant is:

$<MathInline expression={"P(\\text{at least one false positive}) = 1 - (1 - \\alpha)^m"} />$

For $\alpha = 0.05$: with $m = 10$ tests, this is $1 - 0.95^{10} = 0.40$ — a 40% chance of at least one false positive. With $m = 100$ tests, it is $1 - 0.95^{100} = 0.994$ — a 99.4% chance. Run enough tests and you are *guaranteed* to find "significance" purely by chance.

This is the **multiple testing problem**, and it is rampant in modern science. Genomic studies test thousands of genes simultaneously. A/B testing platforms run hundreds of experiments. Neuroimaging compares activity at tens of thousands of brain voxels. Without correction, all of these fields would drown in false positives.

### Theorem: The Bonferroni correction

To control the **family-wise error rate** (the probability of at least one false positive among all $m$ tests) at level $\alpha$, test each individual hypothesis at level $\alpha/m$. That is, reject test $i$ only if $p_i \leq \alpha/m$.

*Proof (union bound).* By the union bound, $P(\text{at least one false positive}) \leq \sum_{i=1}^m P(\text{test } i \text{ false positive}) = \sum_{i=1}^m (\alpha/m) = \alpha$. So testing each at $\alpha/m$ guarantees the family-wise error rate is at most $\alpha$.

*Tradeoff.* The Bonferroni correction is very conservative — it controls the worst case. With $m = 1000$ tests at $\alpha = 0.05$, each test requires $p < 0.00005$, which makes it very hard to detect real effects (low power). Modern alternatives like the **Benjamini-Hochberg** procedure control the **false discovery rate** (the expected proportion of false positives among the rejected hypotheses) instead of the family-wise error rate, which is less conservative and more powerful for large-scale testing.

## Three perspectives on hypothesis testing

1. **As probabilistic proof by contradiction:** assume $H_0$, derive what the data should look like, and check whether the observed data is too extreme to be plausible. If it is, reject $H_0$. This is the logic of the scientific method formalized: you never *prove* $H_1$; you only show that $H_0$ is implausible.
2. **As decision theory under uncertainty:** every test is a decision with two possible errors (Type I and Type II). The significance level $\alpha$ controls the cost of false positives; the power $1-\beta$ controls the cost of false negatives. The choice of $\alpha$ reflects the relative seriousness of the two errors — in medical screening, you tolerate more false positives (low threshold) to avoid missing real disease; in criminal justice, you tolerate more false negatives (high threshold) to avoid convicting the innocent.
3. **As the dual of confidence intervals:** a 95% CI is exactly the set of parameter values that would not be rejected by a two-sided test at 5%. The two are the same inference expressed differently — the test gives a binary verdict, the interval gives the continuous picture of which values are plausible.

## Modern applications

**A/B testing in tech.** Every major tech company (Google, Netflix, Meta) runs thousands of A/B tests per year. Each test is a hypothesis test: does version B convert better than version A? The significance level controls the false-positive rate (shipping a feature that doesn't actually help), and the power controls the false-negative rate (failing to ship a feature that would have helped). The multiple testing problem is acute — with thousands of tests, some will appear significant by chance. Sophisticated platforms use sequential testing (stop early if the result is clear) and false-discovery-rate control to manage this.

**Clinical trials and drug approval.** The FDA requires Phase III clinical trials to demonstrate efficacy at $\alpha = 0.025$ (two-sided, or 0.025 one-sided) with adequate power (typically 80-90%). The stringent significance level reflects the cost of approving an ineffective or harmful drug. Power analysis determines the sample size: to detect a clinically meaningful effect with 90% power at $\alpha = 0.025$, you need a specific number of patients, computed from the expected effect size and variability. This is why clinical trials enroll thousands of patients.

**Genomics and high-throughput biology.** A gene expression study might test 20,000 genes for differential expression. Without correction, at $\alpha = 0.05$, you would expect 1000 false positives. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure (controlling false discovery rate) is the standard tool — it allows some false positives but controls the proportion, enabling discovery in high-dimensional data where Bonferroni would be too conservative.

**The replication crisis.** A growing concern across science (psychology, medicine, economics) is that many published findings fail to replicate. The causes include: p-hacking (running many analyses and reporting only the significant one), publication bias (journals publish only significant results, creating a distorted literature), and underpowered studies (small samples that detect large effects but miss small ones). The multiple testing problem is central: when researchers have many "degrees of freedom" in how to analyze the data, the effective number of tests is large, and false positives proliferate. Preregistration and the open science movement are direct responses.

## Connections

This lesson completes the inference trilogy with [lesson 18](/courses/probability/18-maximum-likelihood-estimation) (MLE) and [lesson 19](/courses/probability/19-confidence-intervals). The three great hypothesis tests — the Wald test, the score test, and the likelihood ratio test — are all built on the MLE's asymptotic properties. The duality with confidence intervals means every concept here maps to a concept there: the significance level $\alpha$ corresponds to the confidence level $1-\alpha$; the test statistic corresponds to the interval's center and width; rejection of $H_0$ corresponds to the CI excluding the null value. The **Central Limit Theorem** ([lesson 16](/courses/probability/16-central-limit-theorem)) provides the sampling distributions (normal for the z-test, t for small samples) that underlie all the tests in this lesson.

The backward link is to **Bayes' theorem** ([lesson 04](/courses/probability/04-conditional-probability-and-bayes)): hypothesis testing is the frequentist approach to the question "is this hypothesis true given the data?" The Bayesian approach computes $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$ directly, using a prior. The frequentist refuses to put a probability on a hypothesis, which is why the p-value is so awkward — it answers a different question ("how likely is the data given the hypothesis?") than the one we actually want to ask ("how likely is the hypothesis given the data?"). The **Bayes factor** is the Bayesian alternative to the p-value, and the debate between the two frameworks continues to shape statistical practice.

Cross-disciplinarily, hypothesis testing is the mathematical formalization of the scientific method: formulate a null hypothesis (the conservative default), collect data, and reject the null only if the data is sufficiently surprising. This structure — assume the default, look for evidence against it — appears in every empirical science, in quality control (six-sigma testing), in the legal standard ("beyond reasonable doubt" is a low-alpha test), and in machine learning (significance testing for comparing model performance). The concepts of Type I and Type II error, power, and multiple testing correction are the shared vocabulary of all quantitative research. The replication crisis is, at its core, a crisis of hypothesis testing — a reminder that the framework's guarantees hold only when its assumptions (pre-specified hypotheses, adequate power, honest reporting) are respected.

  hint="The 95% CI is x_bar +/- 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n). The two-sided test of H_0: mu = mu_0 rejects when |x_bar - mu_0|/(sigma/sqrt(n)) > 1.96, i.e., when mu_0 is outside the CI. So the CI contains exactly the values of mu_0 that are NOT rejected."
  answer="The 95% CI for mu is [x_bar - 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n), x_bar + 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n)]. The two-sided z-test of H_0: mu = mu_0 at alpha = 0.05 rejects when the test statistic |z| = |x_bar - mu_0|/(sigma/sqrt(n)) > 1.96, i.e., when |x_bar - mu_0| > 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n). Rearranging: the test rejects when mu_0 < x_bar - 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n) OR mu_0 > x_bar + 1.96*sigma/sqrt(n), i.e., when mu_0 is OUTSIDE the 95% CI. Equivalently, the test FAILS TO REJECT when mu_0 is INSIDE the 95% CI. So: the 95% CI is exactly the set of mu_0 values that would not be rejected by the two-sided test at 5%. Statement of the test in terms of the CI: to test H_0: mu = mu_0 at 5%, compute the 95% CI and check whether mu_0 is inside it. If yes, fail to reject; if no, reject. Construction of the CI from the test: the 95% CI is the set of all mu_0 for which the test fails to reject. Why this is useful: (1) It means you can read off the hypothesis test from the CI without recomputing — if the CI excludes mu_0, the test rejects; if it includes mu_0, it doesn't. (2) The CI is more informative than the test: it tells you not just whether to reject (binary) but the RANGE of plausible values (continuous). A narrow CI that excludes mu_0 is strong evidence against H_0; a wide CI that barely excludes mu_0 is weak evidence. (3) The duality extends to all tests: the t-test corresponds to the t-interval, the chi-squared test to the chi-squared CI, etc. Every confidence interval has a corresponding hypothesis test, and vice versa. This is why modern statistical practice emphasizes reporting confidence intervals over p-values — the interval contains strictly more information."
