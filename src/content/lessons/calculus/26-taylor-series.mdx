Take the [Taylor polynomial](/courses/calculus/25-taylor-polynomials) and never stop adding terms. When the degree $n$ runs off to infinity, you get the **Taylor series** — a function written as an infinite polynomial. The deep question is: *does this infinite polynomial actually equal the original function?* The answer is yes precisely when the **Lagrange remainder** $R_n(x)$ tends to $0$ as $n \to \infty$. For functions like $e^x$, $\sin x$, and $\cos x$, the remainder vanishes everywhere and the series reconstructs the function exactly, for every $x$. Such functions are called **analytic** — they are completely determined by their local behavior (their derivatives) at a single point. This is one of the most remarkable ideas in mathematics: local information, propagated infinitely, recovers the whole function.

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x - a)^n"} />

This is a [power series](/courses/calculus/24-power-series). Inside its radius of convergence, *if* the remainder vanishes, it converges to $f(x)$ — the function and its infinite polynomial representation are one and the same object.

## The convergence condition: when does the series equal the function?

The Taylor series is a power series, so it has a radius of convergence $R$ (found by the [ratio test](/courses/calculus/23-convergence-tests)). But converging is not enough — the series might converge to something *other* than $f(x)$. The exact condition:

## Theorem: When a Taylor series converges to its function

The Taylor series of $f$ centered at $a$ converges to $f(x)$ at a point $x$ within its radius of convergence if and only if the remainder vanishes:

<MathBlock expression={"\\lim_{n \\to \\infty} R_n(x) = 0"} />

Recall the [Lagrange form](/courses/calculus/25-taylor-polynomials) of the remainder: <MathInline expression={"R_n(x) = \\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}"} /> for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$. If the derivatives <MathInline expression={"f^{(n+1)}"} /> do not grow faster than $(n+1)!$ kills them off, the remainder vanishes and the series converges to $f$.

**For $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$:** all derivatives stay bounded (by exponentials for $e^x$, by $1$ for $\sin$ and $\cos$), and $(n+1)!$ grows faster than anything. The remainder vanishes for *all* $x$ — the Taylor series converge everywhere, and equal the function everywhere.

**For $\ln(1+x)$:** the remainder vanishes only for $|x| < 1$. Beyond $x = 1$, the series diverges (it cannot represent a function that is undefined at $x = -1$ and has a singularity there). The radius of convergence is set by the nearest singularity in the complex plane.

## The common Taylor series — a reference table

These series are worth memorizing. All are centered at $a = 0$ (Maclaurin series), and the first three converge for all $x$:

| Function | Taylor series | Convergence |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| $e^x$ | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \\frac{x^2}{2!} + \\frac{x^3}{3!} + \\cdots"} /> | all $x$ ($R = \infty$) |
| $\sin x$ | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = x - \\frac{x^3}{3!} + \\frac{x^5}{5!} - \\cdots"} /> | all $x$ ($R = \infty$) |
| $\cos x$ | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} = 1 - \\frac{x^2}{2!} + \\frac{x^4}{4!} - \\cdots"} /> | all $x$ ($R = \infty$) |
| $\ln(1+x)$ | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=1}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n} = x - \\frac{x^2}{2} + \\frac{x^3}{3} - \\cdots"} /> | $-1 < x \le 1$ |
| <MathInline expression={"\\frac{1}{1-x}"} /> | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} x^n = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \\cdots"} /> | $|x| < 1$ ($R = 1$) |
| $\arctan x$ | <MathInline expression={"\\displaystyle\\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} = x - \\frac{x^3}{3} + \\frac{x^5}{5} - \\cdots"} /> | $|x| \le 1$ |

Notice the three "converges everywhere" series ($e^x$, $\sin$, $\cos$) all have **factorials in the denominator**, which is what makes the remainder vanish for all $x$. The two series with $R = 1$ have only $n$ (not $n!$) in the denominator — slower decay, smaller radius.

## The binomial series

Newton's generalization of the binomial theorem to arbitrary real exponents:

## Theorem: The Binomial Series

For any real number $\alpha$ and $|x| < 1$:

<MathBlock expression={"(1 + x)^{\\alpha} = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\binom{\\alpha}{n} x^n = 1 + \\alpha x + \\frac{\\alpha(\\alpha-1)}{2!} x^2 + \\frac{\\alpha(\\alpha-1)(\\alpha-2)}{3!} x^3 + \\cdots"} />

where <MathInline expression={"\\binom{\\alpha}{n} = \\frac{\\alpha(\\alpha-1)\\cdots(\\alpha-n+1)}{n!}"} />. The radius of convergence is $R = 1$ (usually). When $\alpha$ is a positive integer, the coefficients <MathInline expression={"\\binom{\\alpha}{n}"} /> become zero for $n > \alpha$ and the series **terminates** — recovering the ordinary binomial theorem, giving a polynomial rather than an infinite series. For non-integer $\alpha$ (like $\alpha = 1/2$ for square roots), the series is genuinely infinite and converges for $|x| < 1$.

## Manipulating Taylor series — finding new series from old

You rarely compute a Taylor series from scratch by taking $n$ derivatives. Instead, you start from the known series above and **manipulate** them using the term-by-term operations validated in [Lesson 24](/courses/calculus/24-power-series):

**Substitution.** Replace $x$ with a function of $x$. From $e^x = \sum x^n/n!$, substitute $x \to -x^2$:

<MathBlock expression={"e^{-x^2} = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-x^2)^n}{n!} = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{n!} = 1 - x^2 + \\frac{x^4}{2} - \\cdots"} />

This is the Gaussian function central to probability and the normal distribution — and its series came for free, no derivatives needed.

**Differentiation.** Differentiate <MathInline expression={"\\sin x = \\sum (-1)^n x^{2n+1}/(2n+1)!"} /> term by term to get <MathInline expression={"\\cos x = \\sum (-1)^n x^{2n}/(2n)!"} /> — confirming the cosine series from the sine series.

**Integration.** Integrate $1/(1+x) = \sum (-1)^n x^n$ term by term to get <MathInline expression={"\\ln(1+x) = \\sum (-1)^n x^{n+1}/(n+1)"} /> — deriving the log series from the geometric series.

**Multiplication.** Multiply series like polynomials (Cauchy product). For example, <MathInline expression={"e^x \\cdot e^x = e^{2x}"} /> gives a nontrivial identity by multiplying $\sum x^n/n!$ with itself.

All of these operations are valid *inside the radius of convergence* of the resulting series, and they are the everyday algebra of analytic functions.

## Euler's identity from Taylor series — the payoff

The Taylor series for $e^x$, $\sin x$, and $\cos x$ are:

<MathBlock expression={"e^x = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{x^n}{n!}, \\quad \\sin x = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}, \\quad \\cos x = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}"} />

Now substitute $x = i\theta$ (where $i^2 = -1$) into the exponential series and separate even and odd powers:

<MathBlock expression={"e^{i\\theta} = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(i\\theta)^n}{n!} = \\underbrace{\\sum_{k=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^k \\theta^{2k}}{(2k)!}}_{\\cos\\theta} + i\\underbrace{\\sum_{k=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{(-1)^k \\theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!}}_{\\sin\\theta} = \\cos\\theta + i\\sin\\theta"} />

This is **Euler's formula** <MathInline expression={"e^{i\\theta} = \\cos\\theta + i\\sin\\theta"} />. Setting $\theta = \pi$: <MathInline expression={"e^{i\\pi} = -1"} />. Five fundamental constants ($e, i, \pi, 1, 0$ via the $+0$) connected by one equation — all derived from the Taylor series of three elementary functions. This single identity underlies signal processing (Fourier analysis), quantum mechanics (wavefunctions), and electrical engineering (phasors).

## Analytic functions — and a warning

A function is **analytic** at $a$ if its Taylor series at $a$ converges to the function in some neighborhood of $a$. Polynomials, exponentials, trig functions, and logarithms are all analytic on their domains — and so is any function built from them by addition, multiplication, composition, differentiation, integration, and (where defined) inversion.

**Not all smooth functions are analytic.** The classic counterexample:

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) = \\begin{cases} e^{-1/x^2} & x \\neq 0 \\\\ 0 & x = 0 \\end{cases}"} />

This function is infinitely differentiable — *smooth* — and all its derivatives at $0$ are zero. Its Taylor series is therefore identically $0$, which matches $f$ at $x = 0$ but *not* at any $x \neq 0$ (where <MathInline expression={"f(x) = e^{-1/x^2} > 0"} />). The Taylor series converges, but to the wrong function. The remainder does not vanish. This example shows that "infinitely differentiable" is strictly weaker than "analytic" — the Taylor series machinery requires the function to be determined by its local derivatives, which not every smooth function is.

## The many faces of Taylor series

- **Computation:** every math library evaluates transcendental functions by summing Taylor series (with argument reduction and Chebyshev tuning for speed). The factorial denominators make $e^x$, $\sin$, $\cos$ converge spectacularly fast.
- **Signal processing:** Fourier analysis decomposes signals into trigonometric series — and Euler's formula <MathInline expression={"e^{i\\theta} = \\cos\\theta + i\\sin\\theta"} /> turns Fourier series into power series in <MathInline expression={"e^{i\\theta}"} />, the foundation of the FFT.
- **Quantum mechanics:** wavefunctions are exponentials <MathInline expression={"e^{iHt/\\hbar}"} /> of the Hamiltonian; perturbation theory expands them in Taylor series in a small coupling constant.
- **Differential equations:** the Frobenius method assumes the solution is a Taylor series and solves for the coefficients — this is how most special functions (Bessel, Legendre, hypergeometric) are defined.

## Modern applications

In **machine learning and optimization**, Taylor series underpin **second-order optimization**. The full Taylor expansion of a loss $L(w)$ around the current weights is <MathInline expression={"L(w + \\Delta w) = L(w) + \\nabla L \\cdot \\Delta w + \\tfrac{1}{2} \\Delta w^\\top H \\Delta w + \\cdots"} />. Newton's method truncates at the quadratic term and minimizes it exactly: <MathInline expression={"\\Delta w = -H^{-1} \\nabla L"} />. The Hessian $H$ is the second-order Taylor coefficient. Quasi-Newton methods (BFGS, L-BFGS) build low-rank approximations to $H$ from gradient differences — implicitly reconstructing the Taylor series from observable quantities. **Second-order optimization** is literally "use the Taylor series through degree 2."

In **physics**, **small-angle approximations** are first-order Taylor truncations: $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ linearizes the pendulum, $\cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2$ gives the relativistic correction. Higher-order Taylor terms produce the nonlinear corrections that distinguish real pendulums from textbook ones. In **general relativity**, the Schwarzschild metric's corrections to Newtonian gravity are the higher-order Taylor terms of the full metric; the perihelion precession of Mercury is a third-order Taylor effect.

In **numerical analysis**, finite-difference schemes for derivatives are derived by Taylor expansion, and their accuracy order is literally "the degree of the first nonzero omitted Taylor term." A "second-order accurate" method is one whose leading error is the third-order Taylor remainder.

In **neural network initialization**, the variance propagation analysis that motivates Xavier/Glorot and He initialization is a first-order Taylor expansion of the activation function around zero, tracking how signal variance passes through each layer. The Taylor series is the analytical backbone of deep learning stability theory.

<KeyInsight>
The Taylor series is the infinite-degree Taylor polynomial: f(x) = Σ f⁽ⁿ⁾(a)/n! · (x−a)ⁿ. It is a power series, and it converges to f(x) precisely when the Lagrange remainder R_n(x) → 0 — which happens when the derivatives f^(n) do not outgrow the factorial (n+1)!. The series for eˣ, sin x, cos x converge everywhere (derivatives bounded, factorials win); ln(1+x) converges only for |x| < 1 (singularity at x = −1 limits the radius). The standard table — eˣ, sin, cos, ln(1+x), 1/(1−x), (1+x)^α (binomial) — is the alphabet of analytic functions, and you find new series by substituting, differentiating, integrating, and multiplying the old ones (all valid inside the radius). Euler's formula e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ falls out of the Taylor series, and with it most of Fourier analysis, quantum mechanics, and signal processing. Analytic functions — those whose Taylor series converge to them — are the workhorses of science; the smooth-but-not-analytic counterexample e^(−1/x²) shows that 'infinitely differentiable' is strictly weaker than 'representable by a Taylor series.'
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson is the capstone of the series module: it takes the [Taylor polynomials](/courses/calculus/25-taylor-polynomials) of the previous lesson to their infinite limit, and it rests on the [power series](/courses/calculus/24-power-series) machinery (radius of convergence, term-by-term operations) and the [convergence tests](/courses/calculus/23-convergence-tests) that compute that radius. The Lagrange remainder, introduced for the finite Taylor polynomial, becomes the exact criterion for whether the infinite series equals the function. The next lesson, [differential equations](/courses/calculus/27-differential-equations), will use Taylor series (via the Frobenius method) to solve ODEs that have no closed-form solution — power series *are* the solutions for many important equations. The binomial series generalizes the binomial theorem you learned in algebra, and Euler's formula is the gateway to complex analysis.

Across disciplines, Taylor series are the universal language of approximation and linearization: Newton's method and second-order optimization in ML, small-angle and perturbation expansions in physics, finite-difference schemes and special-function evaluation in numerical analysis, and Fourier analysis (via Euler's formula) in every quantitative field. To master Taylor series is to master the most powerful approximation tool ever invented — the engine that turns local information (derivatives) into global knowledge (the whole function).
