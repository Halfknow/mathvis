# Matrices as Transformations

Forget "rows and columns of numbers" for a moment. A matrix is a **machine that moves points**. Feed it a vector, and it spits out a new vector. Every point in space gets relocated according to the same rule — and the result is that the entire grid of space gets warped, stretched, rotated, or sheared.

This is the single most important perspective shift in linear algebra: **a matrix is a transformation of space**.

## How a matrix moves space

A 2x2 matrix tells you where two special vectors land: where **e₁ = (1,0)** goes and where **e₂ = (0,1)** goes. The first column is the new home of e₁, the second column is the new home of e₂.

<MathBlock expression={'\\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\\\ c & d \\end{bmatrix} \\begin{bmatrix} x \\\\ y \\end{bmatrix} = x \\begin{bmatrix} a \\\\ c \\end{bmatrix} + y \\begin{bmatrix} b \\\\ d \\end{bmatrix} = \\begin{bmatrix} ax + by \\\\ cx + dy \\end{bmatrix}'} />

Read this geometrically: the output is x times "where e₁ went" plus y times "where e₂ went." The entire transformation is determined by what happens to just two vectors.

## Visual catalogue

Every 2x2 matrix produces one of these spatial effects:

- **Rotation**: The grid turns but keeps its shape. Distances and angles preserved.
  Example: rotation by 90° is the matrix with columns (0, 1) and (-1, 0).

- **Scaling**: The grid stretches or compresses along the axes.
  Example: 2I doubles everything — columns are (2, 0) and (0, 2).

- **Shear**: The grid slides sideways, turning squares into parallelograms.
  Example: columns (1, 0) and (1, 1) — the y-axis tilts to the right.

- **Reflection**: The grid flips across a line.
  Example: columns (1, 0) and (0, -1) — mirror across the x-axis.

- **Projection**: The grid collapses — the whole plane squashes onto a line (or a point).

<KeyInsight>
A matrix is a transformation of space. Its columns tell you where the basis vectors land — and every other vector follows the same linear rule.
</KeyInsight>

## The key property: linearity

Matrix transformations are **linear**: they preserve the structure of vector arithmetic. In geometric terms:

- **Lines remain lines** (they don't curve)
- **The origin stays fixed** (it doesn't shift)
- **Grid lines remain evenly spaced** (they don't bunch up or spread out unevenly)

This is why the entire transformation is captured by just two column vectors. If you know where the grid's anchor points go, you know where everything goes.
