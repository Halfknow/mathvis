You want the best outcome — the most profit, the least cost, the shortest path. Calculus gives you a systematic way to find it: **set the derivative to zero and check**. That single equation, $f'(x) = 0$, encodes the geometric fact that at a peak or valley the tangent line is perfectly flat. Optimization is the art of turning "what's the best?" into "where is the slope zero?"

## The idea

A smooth function's peaks and valleys are where the tangent line is flat — where $f'(x) = 0$. These points are called **critical points**. The optimality condition is the cornerstone of all of optimization:

<MathBlock expression={"f'(x) = 0 \\quad \\Longleftrightarrow \\quad \\text{the tangent line is flat (a candidate extremum)"} />

Not every critical point is a maximum or minimum, but every interior maximum and minimum is a critical point. (Endpoints and corners can also be extrema without satisfying $f'(x) = 0$.)

## Local vs. global extrema

It is crucial to distinguish two flavors of "best":

- A **local (relative) extremum** is the best value *in a neighborhood*. The function may do better elsewhere.
- A **global (absolute) extremum** is the best value *on the entire domain*. There is at most one global maximum value and one global minimum value.

Every global extremum is either a local extremum or occurs at an endpoint (boundary) of the domain. This is why the optimization recipe below includes the final "check the boundaries" step — the global winner is often hiding at the edge, not at a critical point.

## Theorem: The Extreme Value Theorem

If $f$ is **continuous** on a **closed** (and bounded) interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on $[a, b]$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\exists\\, c_{\\max}, c_{\\min} \\in [a,b] \\;\\text{ such that }\\; f(c_{\\min}) \\le f(x) \\le f(c_{\\max}) \\;\\forall x \\in [a,b]"} />

Both hypotheses matter. Drop continuity — for example a jump discontinuity — and the function can "skip over" the value that should be the extremum. Drop closedness (an open interval) and the extremum can escape: $f(x) = x$ on $(0, 1)$ has no max or min, because the values $0$ and $1$ are never attained. The theorem guarantees *existence*; the optimization recipe below tells you *how to find* them — check all critical points plus both endpoints, and the largest/smallest value wins.

## The shortest path: a deeper example

Imagine you need to travel from point $A$ on land to point $B$ in the water. You run faster on land than you swim in water. What's the fastest path?

You might think a straight line — but a straight line spends too much time swimming slowly. You might think: run to the shore closest to $B$, then swim. But that path is unnecessarily long. Calculus finds the sweet spot: the exact crossing point where the tradeoff between distance and speed is optimal. This is the same principle behind Fermat's principle of least time in optics — light itself "chooses" the fastest path, bending as it passes from air into water.

Today, this same idea powers **gradient descent**, the engine behind machine learning. A neural network's "loss" is a surface in high dimensions, and training means rolling downhill to find the lowest point — a minimum. The derivative points downhill; the algorithm follows it. Every time a model improves its predictions, it's solving an optimization problem.

## The optimization recipe

1. **Model**: express the quantity to optimize as a function $f(x)$. (This is the hardest step — translating the word problem into math.)
2. **Differentiate**: compute $f'(x)$.
3. **Find critical points**: solve $f'(x) = 0$ (and note where $f'$ does not exist).
4. **Classify**: use the second derivative test or the sign-change test.
5. **Check boundaries**: if the domain is closed, evaluate $f$ at the endpoints too — the global optimum might be at the edge.

The final step is what the Extreme Value Theorem buys you: on a closed interval you are *guaranteed* a global max and min, and they are among the critical points and endpoints. Compare all candidates and pick the winner.

## A classic example

A farmer has 100 meters of fence and wants to enclose the largest possible rectangular area against a wall (no fence needed on the wall side).

Let $x$ = width, $y$ = height. The constraint is $2y + x = 100$, so $y = (100 - x)/2$.

Area: $A(x) = x \cdot y = x(100 - x)/2 = 50x - x^2/2$.

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = 50 - x = 0 \\implies x = 50"} />
<MathBlock expression={"A''(x) = -1 < 0 \\implies \\text{local maximum"} />

The optimal rectangle is 50m wide and 25m tall, giving $A = 1250$ m². The second derivative is negative everywhere — the parabola opens downward, so this local maximum is also the global maximum. (On the boundary $x = 0$ or $x = 100$, the area is zero, confirming the interior critical point wins.)

## Gradient descent — optimization in many dimensions

In machine learning, we minimize a loss function $L(\theta)$ where $\theta$ is a vector of parameters (often millions of them). The derivative generalizes to the **gradient** $\nabla L$ — a vector pointing in the direction of steepest ascent. Gradient descent takes a step in the *opposite* direction:

<MathBlock expression={"\\theta_{\\text{new}} = \\theta - \\alpha \\cdot \\nabla L(\\theta)"} />

where $\alpha$ is the learning rate (step size). This is the multivariable analogue of "set $f'(x) = 0$ and solve" — except in millions of dimensions, solving $\nabla L = 0$ directly is impossible, so we instead *walk downhill* following the gradient until we reach a (local) minimum. Every neural network trained today runs this loop millions of times.

The optimality condition $f'(x) = 0$ is exactly the condition $\nabla L = 0$ at the bottom of a valley: the gradient vanishes because the tangent plane is flat. The second derivative test generalizes too — to the **Hessian** matrix of second partial derivatives, whose eigenvalues classify critical points as minima, maxima, or saddle points.

## When the second derivative test fails

The test gives no information when $f''(c) = 0$ at a critical point. Three failure cases to watch for:

- **Inflection with a flat tangent:** $f(x) = x^3$ at $x = 0$. Here $f'(0) = 0$ and $f''(0) = 0$, but it's neither a max nor a min — the function keeps increasing right through the origin.
- **Higher-order minimum:** $f(x) = x^4$ at $x = 0$. $f''(0) = 0$ but it's a genuine minimum; you need the fourth derivative (or the First Derivative Test) to see it.
- **Endpoints:** the test only classifies interior critical points. A global max can sit at a boundary where $f'$ is not zero.

When the second derivative test is inconclusive, fall back to the **First Derivative Test** (check the sign of $f'$ on either side) or evaluate $f$ at the point and compare to neighbors directly.

<KeyInsight>
To find the optimum, set the derivative to zero and verify the concavity. A negative second derivative confirms a maximum (dome); a positive second derivative confirms a minimum (bowl). On a closed interval, the Extreme Value Theorem guarantees a global winner exists — find it by comparing all critical points and endpoints. The condition $f'(x)=0$ is the geometric statement "the tangent is flat."
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson applies the [curve-sketching toolkit](/courses/calculus/05-curve-sketching) to a single purpose: finding the best value. The classification tests (First and Second Derivative Test) are borrowed directly, and the concept of critical points is identical. The Extreme Value Theorem sits beside the **Intermediate Value Theorem** from [limits and continuity](/courses/calculus/02-limits-and-continuity) — both guarantee that continuous functions on closed intervals behave the way geometric intuition says they should.

Optimization is the bridge to machine learning. The update rule <MathInline expression={"\\theta_{\\text{new}} = \\theta - \\alpha \\nabla L"} /> is the multivariable cousin of "set $f'(x)=0$," and the entire field of deep learning is built on navigating high-dimensional optimization landscapes. We will return to this in multivariable calculus, where the gradient and Hessian replace $f'$ and $f''$.
