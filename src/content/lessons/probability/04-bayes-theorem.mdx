Bayes' theorem is about **flipping your perspective**. A doctor knows: if you have the disease, the test comes back positive 99% of the time. That is $P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease})$. But what you, the patient, actually need to know is the reverse: given that the test came back positive, what is the chance I actually have the disease? That is $P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})$.

These two quantities are *not* the same, and confusing them leads to profoundly wrong conclusions. In the scenario above — a disease that affects 1 in 100 people, a test that is 99% sensitive and 95% specific — a positive result means you have roughly a **16.6% chance** of being sick. Not 99%. Not even close. Bayes' theorem is the bridge that gets you from the doctor's perspective to the patient's.

## The formula

Bayes' theorem in its simplest form:

$$P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}$$

Each piece has a name and a geometric meaning:

- **Prior** $P(H)$ — your belief in the hypothesis *before* you see any evidence. This is the base rate. It shrinks the world before you even look at the data. If the disease is rare (1%), the prior is tiny, and it takes extraordinary evidence to overcome it.
- **Likelihood** $P(E \mid H)$ — how well the hypothesis *predicts* the evidence. If the disease is present, how likely is a positive test? This is the direction the doctor knows.
- **Posterior** $P(H \mid E)$ — your updated belief *after* seeing the evidence. This is what you actually want.
- **Evidence** $P(E)$ — the total probability of seeing this evidence under *all* possible hypotheses. It acts as a normalizing constant, making sure the posterior is a valid probability.

The evidence term can be expanded using the law of total probability. For two mutually exclusive hypotheses $H$ and $\neg H$:

$$P(E) = P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H) + P(E \mid \neg H) \cdot P(\neg H)$$

This says: the chance of seeing the evidence is the sum over every way the evidence could appear — weighted by how likely each path is.

## The visual picture

Imagine a rectangle representing all possible outcomes. Divide it into **horizontal strips** — one strip for each hypothesis. The height of each strip is proportional to the prior probability $P(H_i)$. A rare hypothesis gets a thin strip. A common one gets a wide strip.

Now draw a **vertical line** through each strip, dividing it into two regions: the evidence $E$ occurs on the left, and $\neg E$ on the right. The position of the vertical line is determined by the likelihood $P(E \mid H_i)$ — if the hypothesis strongly predicts the evidence, the left portion of that strip is large.

Bayes' theorem asks: look at the entire left column (all the $E$-regions stacked up). What fraction of that column comes from the $H_1$-strip?

$$P(H_1 \mid E) = \frac{\text{area of } H_1\text{-strip's } E\text{-region}}{\text{total area of the entire } E\text{-column}}$$

When the prior for $H_1$ is tiny, its strip is thin. Even if nearly all of that thin strip falls in the $E$-column, the absolute area is small compared to the $E$-contributions from the fat $\neg H_1$-strip. That is why a rare disease with an accurate test still yields a surprisingly low posterior.

## Worked example: the medical test

A disease affects **1%** of the population. A test has:

- **Sensitivity** (true positive rate): 99% — if you are sick, the test says positive with probability 0.99
- **Specificity** (true negative rate): 95% — if you are healthy, the test says negative with probability 0.95

You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

### Step 1: Set up the priors

$$P(\text{disease}) = 0.01, \quad P(\text{healthy}) = 0.99$$

The healthy strip is 99 times wider than the sick strip. This is the base rate at work.

### Step 2: The likelihoods

$$P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease}) = 0.99$$

$$P(\text{positive} \mid \text{healthy}) = 1 - 0.95 = 0.05$$

### Step 3: The 10,000-person table

Imagine 10,000 people tested:

| | Sick (100) | Healthy (9,900) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Test +** | 99 | 495 | 594 |
| **Test -** | 1 | 9,405 | 9,406 |

Look at the top row. There are 594 positive results in total. Of those, only 99 come from actually sick people. The other 495 are **false positives** — healthy people who happened to test positive.

### Step 4: Apply Bayes

$$P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive}) = \frac{99}{594} = \frac{99}{99 + 495} \approx 0.166$$

**About 16.6%.** Despite a test that seems highly accurate, a positive result means you probably do *not* have the disease. The reason: the healthy population is so large that even a 5% false positive rate generates far more false positives (495) than the true positives (99) from the tiny sick population.

This is not a paradox. It is the prior doing its job. A rare hypothesis needs strong evidence to become probable.

## The Monty Hall problem

You are on a game show. Three doors. Behind one door is a car; behind the other two, goats. You pick door 1. The host, Monty, opens door 3 to reveal a goat. He asks: do you want to switch to door 2?

Most people feel the remaining two doors should be equally likely. They are wrong. **You should switch.**

### Bayes' thinking

Your prior for each door: $P(\text{car behind } i) = 1/3$ for $i = 1, 2, 3$.

Monty's action is **evidence**. He always opens a goat door, and he chooses which door to open based on where the car actually is. Let $E$ be the event "Monty opens door 3."

- If the car is behind door 1 (your pick), Monty opens door 2 or door 3 at random. So $P(E \mid \text{door 1}) = 1/2$.
- If the car is behind door 2, Monty *must* open door 3. So $P(E \mid \text{door 2}) = 1$.
- If the car is behind door 3, Monty cannot open it. So $P(E \mid \text{door 3}) = 0$.

The total probability of the evidence:

$$P(E) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1 + \frac{1}{3} \cdot 0 = \frac{1}{2}$$

Now apply Bayes:

$$P(\text{door 1} \mid E) = \frac{(1/2)(1/3)}{1/2} = \frac{1}{3}$$

$$P(\text{door 2} \mid E) = \frac{(1)(1/3)}{1/2} = \frac{2}{3}$$

Door 2 is **twice as likely** as door 1. Switching wins 2/3 of the time.

The intuition: when you picked door 1, there was a 2/3 chance the car was behind one of the other two doors. Monty's revelation does not change that — it concentrates the 2/3 into the single remaining door. He has filtered out one goat for you.

## Key insight

> Bayes' theorem says: your posterior belief is the prior times the likelihood, normalized. A tiny prior acts like a narrow funnel — even strong evidence can only push so much probability through a narrow opening.
