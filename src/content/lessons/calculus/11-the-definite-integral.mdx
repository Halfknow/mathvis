We've seen that slicing the area into rectangles and adding them up gives an approximation. Now we take the limit and arrive at the **definite integral** — a single number that captures the exact signed area. The integral comes armed with a set of algebraic properties (linearity, additivity, order preservation) that make it a workhorse, and it extends beautifully to unbounded domains and unbounded functions through **improper integrals** — the gateway to infinite sums, probability, and much of modern analysis.

## The definition

The definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ is:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = \\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^{*})\\, \\Delta x"} />

where $\Delta x = (b - a) / n$ and <MathInline expression={"x_i^{*}"} /> is any sample point in the $i$-th sub-interval. As $n$ grows, the Riemann sum approaches a single number: the signed area under $f$ from $a$ to $b$. The continuity of $f$ guarantees this limit exists and is independent of the sample points.

## Theorem: Properties of the definite integral

Suppose $f$ and $g$ are integrable on the relevant intervals, and $c$ is a constant. Then the definite integral satisfies:

**Linearity** — constants pull out, and the integral of a sum is the sum of the integrals:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_a^b \\bigl[\\alpha f(x) + \\beta g(x)\\bigr]\\, dx = \\alpha \\int_a^b f(x)\\, dx + \\beta \\int_a^b g(x)\\, dx"} />

Geometrically, linearity means you can stretch the height of a region by a factor $\alpha$ (which stretches the area by $\alpha$), or place two regions on top of each other and add their areas — both give the same result as integrating the combined function. Linearity is what lets us integrate polynomials term by term.

**Additivity over intervals** — you can split the integral at any point:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_a^c f(x)\\, dx = \\int_a^b f(x)\\, dx + \\int_b^c f(x)\\, dx"} />

Additivity means you can cut the region into pieces and add them up. If you know the area from $a$ to $b$ and from $b$ to $c$, the total is just the sum — just like cutting a physical shape into two parts and weighing them separately. This also gives the reversal rule $\int_b^a f = -\int_a^b f$ as a special case.

**Order preservation** — if $f(x) \le g(x)$ for all $x$ in $[a, b]$, then:

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) \\le g(x) \\;\\Longrightarrow\\; \\int_a^b f(x)\\, dx \\le \\int_a^b g(x)\\, dx"} />

If one curve is everywhere above another, its area is bigger. Two important consequences: if $f \ge 0$ then $\int f \ge 0$ (non-negativity); and the **bound** $\left|\int_a^b f\, dx\right| \le \int_a^b |f|\, dx$, which says the net area is at most the total area. Order preservation is the tool you use to *estimate* integrals you cannot compute exactly — trap an integral between two simpler ones.

## When does the integral exist?

If $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$, then the definite integral exists. This is a powerful guarantee — continuity is enough. The Riemann sums will converge regardless of which sample points you choose.

Discontinuous functions may or may not be integrable. A function with a finite number of jump discontinuities is still integrable (cut the interval at the jumps and add the pieces). But a function like the Dirichlet function ($f(x) = 1$ if $x$ is rational, $0$ if irrational) is **not** Riemann integrable: every upper Darboux sum is $1 \cdot (b-a)$ and every lower sum is $0$, so they never squeeze together.

## Improper integrals: integrating the infinite

Everything so far assumes a *finite* interval $[a, b]$ and a *bounded* function. But many of the most important integrals in science stretch to infinity or blow up at a point. These are **improper integrals**, and they are defined by a limit. There are two types.

### Type I: infinite limits

When the interval is unbounded, we cut it at a finite point and let the cut run away to infinity:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{\\infty} f(x)\\, dx = \\lim_{t \\to \\infty} \\int_{a}^{t} f(x)\\, dx"} />

The improper integral **converges** if this limit is a finite number, and **diverges** otherwise (including diverging to $\pm\infty$). The integral over $(-\infty, b]$ is defined the same way, and over $(-\infty, \infty)$ you split at any finite point and require *both* halves to converge.

**Example:** <MathInline expression={"\\int_1^{\\infty} \\frac{1}{x^2}\\, dx"} />. Compute the finite piece: <MathInline expression={"\\int_1^t x^{-2}\\, dx = \\left[-x^{-1}\\right]_1^t = 1 - \\tfrac{1}{t}"} />. Then let $t \to \infty$: the limit is $1 - 0 = 1$. So the integral **converges** to $1$.

**Example:** <MathInline expression={"\\int_1^{\\infty} \\frac{1}{x}\\, dx"} />. The finite piece is $\ln t$, which grows without bound as $t \to \infty$. So the integral **diverges** to $+\infty$.

Notice the knife edge: $1/x^2$ has finite area, but the barely-larger $1/x$ has infinite area. The exponent is everything.

### Theorem: The p-test for integrals

The integral

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{1}^{\\infty} \\frac{1}{x^{p}}\\, dx"} />

**converges if and only if $p > 1$.** When it converges, its value is <MathInline expression={"\\tfrac{1}{p-1}"} />.

The intuition: $p > 1$ makes the function shrink fast enough that the accumulated area stays finite; $p \le 1$ leaves it shrinking too slowly, so the area piles up without bound. The borderline case $p = 1$ is the harmonic integral <MathInline expression={"\\int_1^\\infty \\frac{dx}{x} = \\infty"} />. This single test resolves a huge fraction of convergence questions, and it has an exact parallel for infinite series (the p-series test) — same threshold, same reason.

### Type II: vertical asymptotes (unbounded integrand)

When $f$ blows up at an endpoint or interior point, we approach the singularity by a limit:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{0}^{1} \\frac{1}{\\sqrt{x}}\\, dx = \\lim_{t \\to 0^{+}} \\int_{t}^{1} x^{-1/2}\\, dx = \\lim_{t \\to 0^{+}} \\bigl[2\\sqrt{x}\\bigr]_{t}^{1} = 2 - 0 = 2"} />

This integral **converges** to $2$, even though the integrand runs off to infinity at $x = 0$. The spike is tall but narrow enough that its area is finite. Contrast with <MathInline expression={"\\int_0^1 \\frac{1}{x}\\, dx = \\lim_{t \\to 0^+} [\\ln x]_t^1 = 0 - \\ln t = +\\infty"} />, which **diverges** — the singularity is too severe.

There is a Type II p-test too: <MathInline expression={"\\int_0^1 \\frac{1}{x^p}\\, dx"} /> **converges if and only if $p < 1$.** Notice the threshold is *reversed* compared to Type I: near a singularity, smaller $p$ (gentler blow-up) converges; at infinity, larger $p$ (faster decay) converges. Same underlying reason — the question is always "does the area under the tail or spike stay finite?"

### Comparison: the practical convergence test

Often you cannot compute an improper integral exactly, but you can still decide convergence by **comparing** it to a p-integral you understand. If <MathInline expression={"0 \\le f(x) \\le \\tfrac{1}{x^p}"} /> and $p > 1$, then $\int_1^\infty f$ converges (a bigger convergent integral squeezes it). If <MathInline expression={"f(x) \\ge \\tfrac{1}{x}"} /> and $f \ge 0$, then $\int_1^\infty f$ diverges (a smaller divergent integral drags it along). This comparison principle is the workhorse of convergence analysis.

## The many faces of the integral

- **Probability (the biggest application):** a continuous random variable is defined by a density $p(x)$ with <MathInline expression={"\\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} p(x)\\, dx = 1"} /> — a convergent improper integral over the whole real line. Probabilities and expectations are integrals: $P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b p(x)\, dx$. The Gaussian (normal) density integrates to 1 over $(-\infty, \infty)$; verifying this is a classic improper-integral computation.
- **Physics:** escape velocity and gravitational/electrostatic potentials involve <MathInline expression={"\\int_r^\\infty \\frac{1}{x^2}\\, dx"} />-type integrals; total energy of a distribution is an integral over all space.
- **Statistics and ML:** the variance, entropy, KL divergence, and marginal likelihood are all improper-integral-flavored quantities over possibly unbounded domains. The normalizing constant of a probability distribution is found by an (often improper) integral.

## Modern applications

In **probability and statistics**, every continuous distribution rests on an improper integral: the density must integrate to 1 over an unbounded domain. The Gaussian integral <MathInline expression={"\\int_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} e^{-x^2/2}\\, dx = \\sqrt{2\\pi}"} /> is the reason <MathInline expression={"\\sqrt{2\\pi}"} /> appears in the normal distribution — and that single fact underwrites most of classical statistics and much of machine learning.

In **machine learning**, the evidence (marginal likelihood) used in Bayesian model comparison is an integral over the entire parameter space, often unbounded. Variational inference approximates these integrals because they cannot be computed exactly.

In **physics**, the total energy stored in a field, the total mass of an unbounded distribution, and the self-energy of a point charge are all improper integrals — and their convergence (or divergence) is physically meaningful. The divergence of certain integrals is what triggered **renormalization** in quantum field theory.

<KeyInsight>
The definite integral is the limit of Riemann sums, yielding a single signed-area number for any continuous function. It is linear, additive over intervals, and order-preserving — three properties that make it a flexible computational tool. Improper integrals extend the definition to infinite domains and unbounded functions via limits: the p-test says ∫₁^∞ 1/xᵖ converges iff p > 1 (and ∫₀¹ 1/xᵖ converges iff p < 1). Convergence is the question that turns calculus into the foundation of probability, statistics, and modern physics.
</KeyInsight>

## Connections

This lesson adopts the [Riemann sum limit](/courses/calculus/10-riemann-sums) as the official definition of the integral and equips it with algebraic properties (linearity, additivity, order) plus the powerful extension to improper integrals. The next lesson, [the Fundamental Theorem](/courses/calculus/12-the-fundamental-theorem), will reveal that these integrals — defined by painful limits of sums — can almost always be computed exactly with a single antiderivative, collapsing the work to subtraction. The p-test for integrals foreshadows the **p-series test** for infinite series later in the course; the two are the same convergence idea in continuous and discrete clothing.

Across disciplines, the definite integral is the language of totals: probabilities and expectations in statistics, work and energy in physics, expected loss in machine learning. The improper-integral convergence question — "does the accumulated total stay finite?" — is the same question that defines whether a probability distribution is valid, whether a physical quantity is observable, and whether an algorithm is stable.
