The product rule says: $(uv)' = u'v + uv'$. Integrate both sides and rearrange. Integration by parts is the product rule filmed in reverse. Geometrically, the rectangle area $uv$ gets split into two pieces -- one piece is the integral you want, the other is the integral you compute. You trade one area for another, hoping the new one is simpler.

This is **integration by parts**. You trade one integral ($\int u\, dv$) for another ($\int v\, du$). The hope is that the new integral is simpler.

## The strategy

Choose $u$ and $dv$ from the integrand:

- $u$: the part you want to **differentiate** (it should simplify when you take its derivative).
- $dv$: the part you want to **integrate** (you need to be able to find $v = \int dv$).

A common mnemonic is **LIATE** — choose $u$ to be the function type that comes first in this list:
1. **L**ogarithmic ($\ln x$)
2. **I**nverse trig ($\arctan x$, $\arcsin x$)
3. **A**lgebraic ($x^n$)
4. **T**rigonometric ($\sin x$, $\cos x$)
5. **E**xponential ($e^x$)

## Example 1: $\int x e^x\, dx$

Let $u = x$ (algebraic, simplifies to 1 when differentiated), $dv = e^x\, dx$ (easy to integrate).

Then $du = dx$, $v = e^x$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int x e^x\\, dx = x e^x - \\int e^x\\, dx = x e^x - e^x + C = e^x(x - 1) + C"} />

## Example 2: $\int x^2 \sin x\, dx$

Let $u = x^2$, $dv = \sin x\, dx$. Then $du = 2x\, dx$, $v = -\cos x$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int x^2 \\sin x\\, dx = -x^2 \\cos x + \\int 2x \\cos x\\, dx"} />

The remaining integral $\int 2x \cos x\, dx$ still needs parts, but it's simpler (lower power of $x$). Apply parts again: $u = 2x$, $dv = \cos x\, dx$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int 2x \\cos x\\, dx = 2x \\sin x - \\int 2 \\sin x\\, dx = 2x \\sin x + 2\\cos x + C"} />

Combining: $\int x^2 \sin x\, dx = -x^2 \cos x + 2x \sin x + 2\cos x + C$.

## Example 3: The circular case — $\int e^x \sin x\, dx$

Let $u = e^x$, $dv = \sin x\, dx$. Then $du = e^x\, dx$, $v = -\cos x$.

<MathBlock expression={"\\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx = -e^x \\cos x + \\int e^x \\cos x\\, dx"} />

Apply parts again to the remaining integral with $u = e^x$, $dv = \cos x\, dx$:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int e^x \\cos x\\, dx = e^x \\sin x - \\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx"} />

The original integral reappears! This is the "circular" case. Substitute back and solve:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx = -e^x \\cos x + e^x \\sin x - \\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx"} />
<MathBlock expression={"2\\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx = e^x(\\sin x - \\cos x) + C"} />
<MathBlock expression={"\\int e^x \\sin x\\, dx = \\frac{e^x(\\sin x - \\cos x)}{2} + C"} />

<KeyInsight>
Integration by parts trades one integral for another by exploiting the product rule in reverse. Choose u to be the part that simplifies when differentiated, and hope the new integral is easier. When the original integral reappears, you've hit the circular case — solve algebraically.
</KeyInsight>
