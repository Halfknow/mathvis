This is the moment everything connects. The derivative and the integral — two ideas that seem completely different — are actually **inverse operations**. This single insight, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC), is why calculus is one subject, not two. It is widely regarded as the most important theorem in calculus, because it converts the agonizing problem of summing infinitely many rectangles into the easy problem of evaluating an antiderivative at two points.

Think of the dashboard of a car. The **speedometer** reads instantaneous velocity — that's the derivative. The **odometer** records total distance traveled — that's the integral. The FTC says they're inverses of each other: if you know the speed at every moment, you can recover the total distance, and if you know how the distance is changing, you know the speed. Differentiation and integration are two sides of the same coin.

## The area function

Define the **accumulation function** (or area function):

<MathBlock expression={"A(x) = \\int_{a}^{x} f(t)\\, dt"} />

This is the net area accumulated from a fixed starting point $a$ up to a variable endpoint $x$. Think of $A(x)$ as a running total: as $x$ moves to the right, you sweep up more area, and $A(x)$ changes. The two parts of the Fundamental Theorem describe, from two directions, the precise relationship between $A$ and $f$.

## Theorem: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1 (Evaluation)

Suppose $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$, and let $F$ be **any antiderivative** of $f$ on $[a, b]$ (meaning $F'(x) = f(x)$). Then:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\\, dx = F(b) - F(a)"} />

**Hypotheses:** $f$ continuous on the closed interval, $F$ an antiderivative existing on that interval. **Conclusion:** the definite integral — defined as a painful limit of Riemann sums — equals a single subtraction of antiderivative values.

**Intuition:** Integration and differentiation undo each other. The integral $\int_a^b f$ accumulates the rate $f$ over $[a,b]$; that accumulated total is exactly the net change $F(b) - F(a)$ of any antiderivative. You never have to compute a Riemann sum: find *any* antiderivative, subtract, done. This is why we spent [a whole lesson](/courses/calculus/13-antiderivatives) learning to reverse derivatives — Part 1 is the payoff.

## Theorem: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 2 (Differentiation)

Suppose $f$ is **continuous** on an open interval containing $[a, b]$. Define $A(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$. Then $A$ is differentiable on that interval, and:

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = f(x)"} />

**Hypotheses:** $f$ continuous near $x$. **Conclusion:** the derivative of the area function is the integrand itself. The "rate at which accumulated area changes" is exactly the height of the curve at the moving boundary.

**Intuition:** as you nudge the right endpoint $x$ by a tiny $dx$, the new sliver of area added is roughly a rectangle of height $f(x)$ and width $dx$, i.e. $f(x)\, dx$. So $\Delta A \approx f(x)\, dx$, giving <MathInline expression={"\\frac{dA}{dx} = f(x)"} />. Differentiating the integral returns the original function. Part 2 is what guarantees antiderivatives *exist* for every continuous function — the area function $A$ is one.

## A proof sketch of Part 2, via the Mean Value Theorem

The cleanest proof of Part 2 leans on the **Mean Value Theorem** for integrals. Here is the key idea.

Start from the definition of the derivative:

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = \\lim_{h \\to 0} \\frac{A(x+h) - A(x)}{h} = \\lim_{h \\to 0} \\frac{1}{h}\\int_{x}^{x+h} f(t)\\, dt"} />

The numerator is the area of a thin sliver from $x$ to $x+h$. By the Mean Value Theorem for integrals (which itself comes from the Extreme Value Theorem and the Intermediate Value Theorem), this sliver's area equals the width $h$ times the function's value at *some* point $c$ inside the sliver:

<MathBlock expression={"\\int_{x}^{x+h} f(t)\\, dt = f(c) \\cdot h \\quad \\text{for some } c \\in [x, x+h]"} />

This is the crux: the average height of $f$ over $[x, x+h]$ is achieved *at* some point $c$ in that interval. Substitute:

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = \\lim_{h \\to 0} \\frac{f(c) \\cdot h}{h} = \\lim_{h \\to 0} f(c)"} />

As $h \to 0$, the interval $[x, x+h]$ shrinks to the point $x$, forcing $c \to x$. Because $f$ is **continuous**, $f(c) \to f(x)$. Therefore:

<MathBlock expression={"A'(x) = f(x)"} />

That last step — pulling the limit inside $f$ via continuity — is exactly where the continuity hypothesis does its work. Drop continuity and the proof (and often the theorem) fails. The Mean Value Theorem supplied the average-rate-equals-instantaneous-rate-at-some-point fact; continuity handed the limit to $f(x)$. And once we know $A'(x) = f(x)$, Part 1 follows quickly: any two antiderivatives differ by a constant, so $F = A + C$, and $\int_a^b f = A(b) - A(a) = F(b) - F(a)$.

## The bank account analogy

The FTC becomes obvious once you internalize one analogy. Think of a bank account:

- $f(t)$ is your **income rate** at time $t$ (dollars per month) — the speedometer.
- $F(t)$ is your total **balance** at time $t$ (dollars) — the odometer.
- $\int_a^b f(t)\, dt$ is how much you **earned** between month $a$ and month $b$ — the accumulated deposits.
- The rate of change of your balance *is* your income rate: $F'(t) = f(t)$ (Part 2).
- Your earnings over $[a,b]$ equal the change in balance: $\int_a^b f = F(b) - F(a)$ (Part 1).

The two parts of the FTC are just two ways of stating the same bookkeeping identity: "the rate at which money comes in" and "how the balance changes" are the same fact viewed from two directions. Substitute "velocity" for "income" and "distance" for "balance," and you have the car analogy. The structure is universal.

## Why this is the most important theorem in calculus

Before the FTC, computing areas required infinite sums of infinitesimal rectangles — a philosophical headache that tormented mathematicians for centuries. After the FTC, you just find an antiderivative and plug in the bounds. The theorem bridges two worlds:

- **Differential calculus** (rates of change, slopes, derivatives)
- **Integral calculus** (accumulation, areas, integrals)

They are not separate subjects. They are two faces of the same coin. This is why calculus is one unified discipline — differentiation takes you from totals to rates, and integration takes you from rates back to totals. The FTC is the hinge.

**Isaac Newton** and **Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** both discovered this connection independently in the late 17th century, each developing his own notation and approach. Newton thought in terms of "fluxions" (rates of change of flowing quantities), while Leibniz thought in terms of sums of infinitesimal differences — his $\int$ and $dx$ notation is what we still use today. Their independent discovery of the same deep structure is one of the great parallel discoveries in the history of mathematics — and it sparked one of its bitterest priority disputes.

## The many faces of the FTC

- **Physics:** position $s(t)$ and velocity $v(t)$ are an antiderivative pair: $s'(t) = v(t)$ and $s(b) - s(a) = \int_a^b v$. The same structure governs charge and current, momentum and force, energy and power.
- **Probability:** the cumulative distribution function $F(x) = P(X \le x)$ is an antiderivative of the density $f(x)$: $F'(x) = f(x)$. The FTC is why densities and CDFs are interchangeable representations of a distribution.
- **Machine learning:** backpropagation and autodifferentiation are essentially the FTC in high dimensions — the chain rule (a derivative theorem) and integration by parts (its sibling) let us compute gradients of composed functions exactly. The "reverse mode" of automatic differentiation runs the derivative backward through the computation graph, mirroring how integration undoes differentiation.
- **Differential equations:** any equation of the form $y' = f(x)$ is solved by $y = \int f$ — the FTC is the one-step solution to the simplest ODE, and the seed of all solution techniques for harder ones.

<KeyInsight>
The Fundamental Theorem says differentiation and integration are inverses. Part 1 (Evaluation): ∫ₐᵇ f = F(b) − F(a) turns an infinite-sum problem into one subtraction. Part 2 (Differentiation): d/dx ∫ₐˣ f = f(x) means the area function's slope is the integrand itself. The proof of Part 2 hinges on the Mean Value Theorem: the average height of f over a thin sliver equals f at some point c, which continuity forces toward f(x) as the sliver shrinks. The bank-account analogy (income rate vs balance) makes both parts obvious.

Across disciplines, the FTC's inverse-pair structure is everywhere: position/velocity in physics, CDF/density in probability, and the forward/reverse modes of automatic differentiation that train every modern neural network. Once you see "a function and its accumulated total are derivatives of each other," you see the FTC operating beneath a vast amount of science and engineering.

Now continuity of f enters: because f is continuous, f(c) → f(x) as c → x. Hence A'(x) = f(x).
