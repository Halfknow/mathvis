The **normal distribution** (also called the Gaussian distribution) is the most famous probability distribution in statistics. Its bell-shaped curve describes everything from heights to measurement errors to test scores.

## The density function

A normal distribution is fully described by two parameters:

- **$\mu$** (mu) — the **mean**, which controls where the peak is centered
- **$\sigma$** (sigma) — the **standard deviation**, which controls how wide the bell is

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

The formula looks intimidating, but the geometry is simple: a symmetric bell, peaked at $\mu$, with width controlled by $\sigma$.

## The 68-95-99.7 rule

For any normal distribution:

- **68%** of values fall within $\mu \pm 1\sigma$
- **95%** of values fall within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$
- **99.7%** of values fall within $\mu \pm 3\sigma$

This rule lets you quickly estimate probabilities without computing integrals.

## Why is it everywhere?

The **Central Limit Theorem** explains why the normal distribution shows up so often: when you average many independent random variables, the result tends toward a normal distribution — regardless of the original distribution.

This is why:
- Sample means are approximately normal
- Measurement errors cluster symmetrically
- Heights, weights, and blood pressures are bell-shaped

## Key insight

> The normal distribution is not just one curve — it's a *family* of curves parameterized by $\mu$ and $\sigma$. The Central Limit Theorem ensures that this family appears naturally whenever many small independent effects combine.
