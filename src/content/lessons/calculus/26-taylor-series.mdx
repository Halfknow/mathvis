Take the Taylor polynomial and never stop. When the Taylor polynomial has infinitely many terms and converges, you get the Taylor series -- the function written as an infinite polynomial. Not all functions have this property. Those that do are called "analytic," meaning the function is completely determined by its local behavior at a single point. For analytic functions, knowing all derivatives at one point is enough to reconstruct the function everywhere within the radius of convergence.

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n"} />

This is a power series. Inside its radius of convergence, it converges to $f(x)$. The function and its infinite polynomial representation are identical.

## When does the Taylor series equal the function?

The Taylor series equals $f(x)$ when the remainder goes to zero:

<MathBlock expression={"\\lim_{n \\to \\infty} R_n(x) = \\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1} = 0"} />

For $e^x$, $\sin x$, $\cos x$: all derivatives are bounded by exponentials, and $(n+1)!$ grows faster than anything. The remainder vanishes for all $x$ — the series converges everywhere.

For $\ln(1+x)$: the remainder vanishes only for $|x| < 1$. Beyond that, the series diverges.

## The binomial series

<MathBlock expression={"(1+x)^\\alpha = \\sum_{n=0}^{\\infty} \\binom{\\alpha}{n} x^n \\quad \\text{for } |x| < 1"} />

where $\binom{\alpha}{n} = \frac{\alpha(\alpha-1)\cdots(\alpha-n+1)}{n!}$. This generalizes the binomial theorem to any real exponent $\alpha$. When $\alpha$ is a positive integer, the series terminates (it's a polynomial).

## Euler's identity from Taylor series

The Taylor series for $e^x$, $\sin x$, and $\cos x$ are:

<MathBlock expression={"e^x = \\sum \\frac{x^n}{n!}, \\quad \\sin x = \\sum \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}, \\quad \\cos x = \\sum \\frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}"} />

Substitute $x = i\theta$ into $e^{i\theta}$:

<MathBlock expression={"e^{i\\theta} = \\cos\\theta + i\\sin\\theta"} />

Setting $\theta = \pi$: $e^{i\pi} = -1$. Five fundamental constants connected by one formula, all derived from Taylor series.

## Analytic functions

A function is **analytic** at $a$ if its Taylor series at $a$ converges to the function in some neighborhood of $a$. Polynomials, exponentials, trig functions, and logarithms are all analytic on their domains.

Not all smooth functions are analytic. The classic counterexample:

<MathBlock expression={"f(x) = \\begin{cases} e^{-1/x^2} & x \\neq 0 \\\\ 0 & x = 0 \\end{cases}"} />

This function is infinitely differentiable, but all derivatives at 0 are zero. Its Taylor series is identically 0 — which doesn't match the function for any $x \neq 0$.

<KeyInsight>
The Taylor series is the infinite-degree Taylor polynomial. When it converges, it reconstructs the original function exactly. For eˣ, sin(x), and cos(x), it converges everywhere. For functions like ln(1+x), convergence is limited by the radius of convergence.
</KeyInsight>
