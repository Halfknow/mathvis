# Span and Linear Independence

Last lesson we saw that two arrows can sometimes build the entire plane — and sometimes get trapped on a single line. This lesson gives that observation a name and explains *why* it happens.

## The span

The **span** of a set of vectors is the set of all points you can reach by linear combinations. It's the answer to "how much space can these arrows cover?"

- One nonzero vector spans a **line** through the origin.
- Two vectors (pointing different directions) span the entire **plane**.
- Two vectors (pointing the same direction) still only span a **line**.
- Three vectors in 3D (pointing in three independent directions) span all of **space**.

The span is the reachable territory. It always includes the origin (set all scalars to zero). And it's always "flat" — a line, a plane, a 3D space, never a curve or a ball.

## When vectors are redundant

Two vectors that point the same direction are **linearly dependent** — one is redundant. You could throw one away without shrinking the span at all. Formally:

> Vectors are **linearly dependent** if one of them can be written as a linear combination of the others.

Conversely, vectors are **linearly independent** if none of them is redundant — each one contributes a genuinely new direction.

## The visual test

Here's a spatial test for independence: **do the arrows point in genuinely different directions?**

- In 2D: two vectors are independent if they don't sit on the same line through the origin.
- In 3D: three vectors are independent if they don't all sit in the same flat plane through the origin.

The zero vector is never independent — it adds no direction at all.

<KeyInsight>
The span is the space your arrows can reach. Linear independence means every arrow contributes a new direction — none is redundant.
</KeyInsight>

## Why it matters

Independence determines how much information your vectors carry. If you have 10 vectors but only 3 are independent, you really only have 3 directions of freedom. The other 7 are just echoes. Every algorithm in linear algebra — from solving systems to computing eigenvalues — depends on knowing how many independent directions you actually have.
