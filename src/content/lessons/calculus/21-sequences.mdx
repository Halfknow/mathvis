A **sequence** is an infinite list of numbers: $a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots$ Think of it as a function $a(n)$ where the input is a positive integer. A sequence is an infinite list; a series (coming in the next lesson) is the sum of that list. The central question: **does the sequence settle down to a fixed number, or does it run away?** Convergence asks whether the list settles down -- but be warned, the harmonic series shows that "terms going to zero" is not enough to guarantee that a sum converges.

## Convergence and divergence

A sequence **converges** to a limit $L$ if the terms get arbitrarily close to $L$ as $n$ grows. Formally:

<MathBlock expression={"\\lim_{n \\to \\infty} a_n = L"} />

A sequence **diverges** if it doesn't converge — it might oscillate, grow without bound, or behave chaotically.

## Examples

**Convergent sequences:**
- $a_n = 1/n$ → converges to 0. The terms shrink toward nothing.
- $a_n = (1 + 1/n)^n$ → converges to $e \approx 2.718$. This is the definition of Euler's number.
- $a_n = \sin(1/n)$ → converges to 0.

**Divergent sequences:**
- $a_n = n$ → diverges to $\infty$.
- $a_n = (-1)^n$ → oscillates between $-1$ and $1$, never settling.
- $a_n = n \sin(n)$ → oscillates with growing amplitude.

## Bounded and monotone

Two useful properties:

**Bounded**: there exists $M$ such that $|a_n| \leq M$ for all $n$.

**Monotone**: the sequence is either always increasing or always decreasing.

**Monotone Convergence Theorem**: A bounded monotone sequence converges. If it's always increasing and bounded above, it converges to its least upper bound. This theorem guarantees convergence without computing the limit.

## The squeeze theorem for sequences

If $b_n \leq a_n \leq c_n$ and both $b_n$ and $c_n$ converge to the same limit $L$, then $a_n$ also converges to $L$.

Example: $\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\cos n}{n} = 0$ because $-\frac{1}{n} \leq \frac{\cos n}{n} \leq \frac{1}{n}$ and both bounds converge to 0.

<KeyInsight>
A sequence converges when its terms settle toward a fixed number. Bounded monotone sequences always converge — they can't oscillate or run away.
</KeyInsight>
